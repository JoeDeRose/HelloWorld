Trip to the United Kingdom and Ireland

March 1-10, 2002
Billy Johnson and Joe DeRose

All photographic images in this section are from pictures that we took during the trip.

Anyone who wondered if we could possibly see everything we planned probably won't be surprised at the answer:  Nope; we batted .550.  But we did see almost all of the things that were of high priority to us.  Our original itinerary is available, newly edited with commentary about what we saw and what we missed (and why).

 

It is good to have an end to journey towards; but it is the journey that matters, in the end.
–Ursula Le Guin

 

Saturday, March 2, 2002

Having left Atlanta the previous evening, we arrived in London early, went through customs, and made our way into town on the Gatwick Express train.  We checked in at our hotel just in time for lunch, which we ate (a bit guiltily) at Burger King so that we would not lose too much more of the already short day.

After lunch we went immediately to the #11 bus – double-decker, of course – and rode on the top level from Victoria Station to St. Paul's Cathedral.  The bus gave us a great opportunity to see the city, including Trafalgar Square and various other areas on the north bank of the Thames.
     
When we finally arrived at St. Paul's, we immediately set to climbing the famous stairs, which took us first to the whispering gallery, and then between the inner and outer domes to the upper observation level.  We looked out at the Thames River, Tower Bridge, and the London Eye, and just enjoyed the sense of finally being in this place we had waited so long to get to.  Then we set to climbing back down to the street.

After departing St. Paul's, we crossed the Millennium Bridge – a new type of suspension bridge design in which the suspension "towers" rise barely higher than the level of the bridge itself;  it was originally opened as part of London's millennium celebration, and only recently reopened after some "wobbling" problems had been addressed.  Just on the south bank of the Thames, by the Millennium Bridge, we briefly passed through the Tate Modern; we didn't take time to see any of the exhibitions, but I had been quite keen on seeing the physical plant, as this building is known internationally for the successful conversion of an old electric plant into an art museum.  Notably, its Turbine Gallery, literally where the turbines once were placed, allows for huge works of art that simply cannot be displayed in most other museums.  We walked through the building and briefly surveyed the current installation in the Turbine Gallery, and then took our first white-knuckle taxi ride to the London Dungeon.

Billy had done the research on which of the "spooky" sites to visit – the London Dungeon or Madame Tussaud's – and had decided that the London Dungeon would be the one.  It is essentially a guided tour through the more gruesome history of London, from the plagues to the tortures to the executions, with large areas dedicated to telling the stories of the great London Fire and the saga of Jack-the-Ripper.  It was educational and entertaining; we gave it high marks.

After a quick return to the hotel via the London Underground (London Bridge Station to Victoria Station), we taxied to the British Airways London Eye, a huge Ferris-wheel type device that sits literally in the Thames River.  Its enclosed capsules carry tourists through the 450-foot circle over the course of a half-hour, and is well worth the time and expense.  It was from the London Eye that I framed the best picture I never got:  Nearing the top of the circuit, which we began at sunset, I spotted St. Paul's Cathedral gleaming white like a gemstone against the dimly lit surrounding neighborhood.  Its spires and its dome genuinely glistened under the heavy spotlights, and from this height, the entire building was visible – an impossible view from the ground.  Instead of this shot, I got a message on my screen reading, "Check Your Batteries."  Use your imagination.

After the London Eye, we hopped another taxi to the Medieval Banquet – a tourist show that I remembered from my previous visit to London with my mother, more that 20 years earlier.  The Medieval Banquet is a bit hokey, but fun:  A four-course all-you-can-eat meal is served amid "Medieval" entertainment – sword fights, sorcery, and strongmen.  After the meal, we took the Tube from Tower Hill Station back to Victoria Station finally to get some much-needed sleep.

 

Sunday, March 3, 2002

We got a later start than we wished getting out of London, but quickly made our way to the Gatwick Express for a return trip to the airport.  There we picked up the rental car and I got my first taste of driving on the left.  It was far more disorienting than I had expected:  Staying on the left side of the road was no problem (especially since my first hour-and-a-half were on a motorway).  And even shifting gears with my left hand wasn't too difficult.  But sitting on the right side of the car was unexpectedly confusing:  It wasn't just a matter of walking to the wrong door each time we returned to the car (an error that for some reason never plagued Billy).  Rather, I found it complicated to center the car in the lane, and had my left wheels hanging off the edge a lot of the time at first.  (This error did indeed plague Billy just a much, when he finally drove!)  I had been driving for half a week before the first time that I reached in the correct direction to buckle my seat belt.  And on the few occasions that I actually looked in the proper place for my rear-view mirror, I found that I could not get nearly the sense of traffic flow behind me that I would be able to get back home.  Entering a parking space (or any one-lane area) was a nail-biting experience at first.  And I don't think I ever actually backed out of a parking space onto the correct side of the road.  By the end of the trip, though, I was a pro:  zipping along narrow mountain roads in the rain, passing the lorries at the merest hint of a straightaway, parallel parking in Dublin, and even dealing with those ubiquitous but absurd "Oncoming Traffic in Center of Roadway" signs.  The picture to the right is actually from Scotland.  Perplexingly, we saw no such sign in England, where most people get their first taste of driving on the left – but a ton of them in Scotland, by which point we thought that most people would be accustomed to the system (or hospitalized).

We made it to Stonehenge as expected, however, and immediately set about seeing the ancient monument.  I was captivated by the Ravens atop the stones, and tried to get some pictures that highlighted what seemed to me a very poetic juxtaposition – the delicate and dark birds drawn to land on the heavy and severe, yet bright and skyward-reaching, monolith.  (Click on the picture for a full set of our pictures from Stonehenge.)

After leaving Stonehenge, we went north to Avebury, another stone circle significantly larger than its more famous cousin.  The stones themselves here are much larger than at Stonehenge, and the circle is vast enough to encircle part of the town in which it is set.  Billy was exhausted from taking Benadryl for an allergic reaction, and had trouble fighting the drowsiness at this stop – so we couldn't give Avebury the attention it deserved.  Fortunately, we were able to return here on the last day to take pictures and explore it more thoroughly.

Because of our late start in the morning, it was well into the afternoon by the time we left Avebury.  Not wanting to let it get dark on a challenging route on my first driving-on-the-left day, we decided to skip the Uffington White Horse and Ironbridge, and instead head directly to the motorway to reach Newcastle-upon-Tyne in a timely fashion.  It was on this drive that I first learned the limitations of asking directions of the insular English:  Without exception, if I asked directions to a town any farther than the closest village on a major road, I would be met with the reply, "I've never heard of it; can you tell me the name of a town on the way?"  I left convinced that if I had asked the directions to London, I would hear back the identical response.  Perhaps I just had bad luck with the people that I asked for directions – but I quickly found myself wondering how it was possible that England had given birth to so many explorers.

While we regretted having to skip some of the stops on our itinerary, the beauty of this drive was a nice compensation:  Before night fell, we had the chance to drive through one quaint English village after another, marveling at the thatched roofs and the stone walls and the monumental churches and clock towers that dominated village squares.  Night fell as we reached the motorway, and then we simply enjoyed the rolling hills of north England, which looked as if a blanket of Christmas lights had been gently draped upon them.

We certainly could have made it to Newcastle-upon-Tyne at a reasonable hour, but were panicked by a service area attendant who told us (inaccurately, we now know) that we should not expect to find a hotel room without a reservation, and that hotels might not be prepared to receive guests in the late evening.  With this warning, we made a reservation for a motorway TraveLodge in Scotch Corner, an hour shy of Newcastle-upon-Tyne.

 

Monday, March 4, 2002

Spending the night in Scotch Corner actually left us better positioned for seeing Hadrian's Wall early on this leg.  We had to skip the Gateshead Millennium Bridge – but that stop had really just been an afterthought, added because we anticipated spending the night very near it.

I was still wrapping my mind around the signposting of the British routes, and found the signage almost impenetrable at this point.  But in moving north, we eventually came to signs for Hadrian's Wall, and followed them religiously.  These led us to Chesters Roman Fort, at which we thought we were just asking directions, so we didn't take the camera – a decision that later filled us with regret, as it turned out that Chesters was easily the most spectacular part of the wall that we saw.  It included a museum of Third Century Roman carvings and inscriptions as well as impressive sections of the Fort that had somehow survived the ages.  We did get out at Carrawburgh (pronounced "CARE-a-berf") and took a couple of pictures of wall segments that we saw.  (Click on the image to see these.)

Shortly after leaving Hadrian's Wall, we entered Scotland, and briefly paused for pictures at the border overlook.  We were happy to find ourselves posing beside the same marker that my mother had posed beside on her visit a few years ago.

We looked for lunch in Jedburgh and there stumbled upon the spectacular Jedburgh Abbey.  We hadn't even realized that this spectacular ruin existed before pulling into the town hungry for a meal.  And in the shadow of Jedburgh Abbey, we enjoyed – indeed, enjoyed very much – our first fish and chips of the trip.  According to the information I have been able to find upon our return, this incredible building has lay unused now for over 850 years – in ruins for as long as there has been a written history on my continent!  (Click on the image for a the two pictures I took of the abbey.)

Moving north we bypassed Edinburgh, and crossed the Firth of Forth on the Forth Road Bridge, which runs alongside, and afforded us a spectacular view of, the famous Forth Rail Bridge.  The rail bridge would be a amazing feat of engineering even today, and is particularly phenomenal in that it was constructed over a century ago.

We spent the night at Castle Stuart near Inverness, a magnificent building that had been left alone with its ghosts as a ruin for 300 years, from shortly after its construction to the recent renovation to Jacobean elegance at the loving hands of the Stuart family.  We stayed here in the "Stuart of Appin" room overlooking the Moray Firth and Inverness, and enjoyed exploring the castle's beautiful rooms and secret passages, of which Caroline Stuart showed us many.  At Castle Stuart we had two of the best meals of our trip.  The first of these meals was the enormous dinner at which we both tried for the first time (and liked) haggis.  We knew in general how haggis is made, but couldn't remember the specific set of icky organs that goes into it (sheep's heart, liver, and kidney, boiled in the stomach), and so, when the chef came out to inquire how we had enjoyed our meal, we asked him what it was composed of.  He looked concerned that we might ask, shuffled his feet nervously, looked to the floor, and then said (in a thick Scottish accent), "It's just offal."  We misunderstood his pronunciation, and hilarious hijinks ensued as we tried to reassure him.  (Click on the image for more pictures of Castle Stuart, including one of the secret passages.)

The next morning at Castle Stuart, we partook of a breakfast even more generous than the dinner:  Piled before us were fruit, toast, porridge, cereal, milk, juices, sausage, eggs, bacon, ham, and, for dessert (I had never even heard of a dessert course at breakfast!), pancakes and waffles with fruit spread.  The cook at Castle Stuart sent us on our way, noting that it was windy out and she hoped the ferry wouldn't take her breakfast.  As we would learn several hours later, she was right to be concerned.

As soon as we left the castle, we drove north up the amazing Caithness and Sutherland coast, which was a series of stone cliffs against the North Sea.  Here we were impressed by the slate fences that some farmers erected between their fields – constructed simply from squares of  slate, set between posts to keep them upright.  We also saw ruined homes that we later learned were vestiges of the "Highland Clearances," a disreputable period of Scottish History in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries in which clan chiefs burned people out of their homes, and forced them out of Scotland, to turn their land into more profitable sheep farms.

We arrived in Scrabster (the town is about as pretty as its name) exactly on schedule, patting ourselves on the back for the precision of our itinerary.  Then we waited for the late ferry to arrive.  At about the time we should have been disembarking in Orkney, the St. Ola finally set sail.  The first part of the crossing was particularly rough and rainy, but we did enjoy passing the lighthouse atop the magnificent cliffs of Dunnet Head, the northernmost point of the island of Great Britain.  (Click on the picture for a more spectacular view of the cliff.)  Instead of its normal route along the western edge of the archipelago, the ferry sought calmer waters by going between the islands, through the bay known as the Scapa Flow.  It was a successful strategy:  For the latter two-thirds of the voyage we were plagued only with rain rather than rolling water.  We were really lucky that we tackled the ferry crossings in the order we did, as the boats got progressively better.  It was painful enough as we did it; to have gone in the reverse order would have been excruciating.


Stromness

The ferry arrived in picturesque Stromness at about 4:30 in the afternoon – almost three hours late, and after closing time for two of the places we had really hoped to see – Maes Howe (a Neolithic tomb with the largest collection of runic graffiti outside Scandinavia) and Scara Brae (an excavated stone-age town).  This was the only day of our trip in which rain proved a problem, but it was quite a problem in Orkney.  I found myself thinking that if this wasn't a hundred-year flood of some sort, that the Orcadians really should seek higher ground.  We also found in Orkney – perhaps because it is both small and remote – that all pretext of adequate signage had been abandoned.  Nonetheless, we did get to see the spectacular Standing Stones of Stenness and the Ring of Brodgar (marked there as the "Ring of Brogar").  (Click on the picture for more pictures of Orkney stones.)

We then explored much of the rest of the mainland (which is what the unnamed primary island of Orkney is called), and crossed the shockingly narrow Churchill Causeways to a couple of other islands, Burray and South Ronaldsay, in search of the Italian Chapel.  We found the chapel after it had closed, but nonetheless enjoyed the drive, and especially the amazing shipwrecks we saw from the Churchill Causeways.  We had heard that the waters of Orkney are among the most dangerous in the world, and it was certainly easy to see confirmation of this as we drove around:  here a crow's nest askew in the water, there an upturned hull, and jagged pieces of metal rising from the water all around.

At the Kirkwall Hotel we had a fine meal (haggis again for me; Billy had fish) and stayed the night.  The Kirkwall Hotel is dignified and quaint in all the ways one might want a hotel in the biggest city of a remote place to be dignified and quaint.  It still had the old lift doors that one had to open manually; it was obvious what portion of the room had been carved out to allow for en suite facilities; and the phones, like all that we found in Orkney, were not designed for tone dialing.  In uncharitable moments we found ourselves wondering if the staff of the Kirkwall might consider 1900 House science fiction – but it kept us warm and dry on a cold and rainy night, and now that I'm back home, I think it was my favorite stop.

 

Wednesday, March 6, 2002

We left the Kirkwall Hotel early, and made a quick detour by the Italian Chapel to see it in more detail, and, we hoped, view the interior.  The chapel is a remnant of the use of its small island as a place for Italian prisoners of war (before the construction of the roadway that connected it to several other islands).  These prisoners used refuse and spare parts to transform one of their Quonset huts into the type of chapel that they remembered from home.  It was supposed to open at dawn every day – but we were there well after sunrise and it remained locked down.  We got a good look at the exterior though.

After visiting the Italian Chapel, we hurried back to Stromness and the St. Ola to "go south" (as the Orcadians say when they're leaving Orkney).  We had considered staying in Orkney longer to see the sites that we had missed the previous day – but decided that we didn't want to throw the rest of the trip off schedule.  The harsh ferry ride from the previous day was a picnic compared to this one:  The St. Ola resumed its normal route west of Orkney, which afforded us a much-desired view of the Old Man of Hoy, a towering stone column on the northwest coast of the island of Hoy, but also exposed us to the whimsy of the rambunctious north Atlantic.  For three hours we would look out the windows and see nothing but water, then nothing but sky, then nothing but water again.  And constantly there was the scraaaaaaaaaaaape - clank! - scraaaaaaaaaaaape - clank! of some metal implement loose in its housing.  At times the aft or stern of the St. Ola would crash down upon the water, sending a mighty splash to the sea birds that surrounded us.  And even the crew – seasoned veterans of this sort of crossing, one assumes – were having trouble walking on the voyage, and all through the trip we watched as our shipmates stumbled helplessly into walls and each other.  To compound the challenge to my stomach, I had decided to stop at the snack bar as the ship got underway to sample the black pudding – blood (blood substitute, they assured me) and oatmeal as best I could tell.  Billy and I both confessed to each other afterward that we had spent a sizeable portion of the trip familiarizing ourselves with the location of the life vest closets and lifeboats.

Finally on blessed terra firma, we continued southward toward Drumnadrochit, where we toured the Loch Ness 2000 Exhibition.  We had expected a hucksterish promulgation of the myth, and were surprised when this multimedia presentation emphasized the science that all but disproves the Loch Ness Monster stories.  It did give an excellent history of the search for Nessie, and extolled the grandeur of the environment.  Notable among the things we learned was that Loch Ness is the largest body of water in Great Britain, and holds a greater volume than every river, pond, and lake in England and Wales combined.

We continued south by Urquart Castle (pronounced "ER-kert"), which overlooks the widest part of Loch Ness.  It was getting dark at we approached Fort William, but we enjoyed the scenery while we could, especially the beautiful cliffs that rose straight up from the car on one side, and plummeted to the shore on the other, as well as the amazing view of the mountains rising up from the mists of Loch Lochy.  After dinner, I was glad to have several days of British driving under my belt, as I faced the most challenging portions of the route I had yet seen:  first the rainy, dark roads along Loch Lomond, and then the urban driving as we passed through Glasgow.

We left our hotel in Kilmarnock early, and made our way to Stranraer for the ferry crossing to Belfast.  If every crossing were as easy as this one, I wouldn't be cursing ferries today:  The drive was pleasant and relaxed as we descended the coast of Scotland and enjoyed still more spectacular cliffs and mountains, and especially tiny Ailsa Craig rising distant in the water.  Then when we reached Loch Ryan, the bay at the bottom of which Stranraer rests, we saw our ferry and paced it as we both approached the port – both of us on time.  In Stranraer we went through a bit of scrutiny, perhaps because of the political situation in Northern Ireland, and then boarded the luxurious European Causeway, which took us perfectly on schedule across the North Channel to Belfast.

The approach to Belfast is nothing but surreal.  The city and its port sit deep in Belfast Lough, and can only safely be approached between a long straight row of buoys that lead one in turn past the breakwater, then past a split in the waterway from one to three channels, then to an industrial dockland with huge cranes that looked like giant horses and enormous birds (which dockland we later learned was where the Titanic was built).  The tedious entry to Belfast reminded me of nothing so much as the processional at the end of Star Wars: Episode 4, if Star Wars had been set in a 60s Pittsburgh steel mill.

Belfast seemed instantly dispiriting, and we immediately vacated it for Giants Causeway.  We had hoped that on the way we would visit the Carrick-a-Rede rope bridge, but it had been removed in its entirety for some sort of maintenance.  We did, however, get the pleasant surprise of discovering the ruins of Dunseverick Castle (at left) just before we arrived, and of Dunluce Castle (at right) just after we departed.

When we finally reached Giants Causeway, we were both captivated.  It seemed inconceivable that these acres of hexagonal paving stones – so they appeared to be – were a natural basalt formation.  But apparently so they are.  Billy actually outlasted me as he ran from one section of the formation – Ireland's first World Heritage Site – to another.  He even suggested we walk the seven-mile trail, although I talked him out of it.  (Click on the picture for our set of Giants Causeway pictures.)

After leaving County Antrim, we drove south to the Irish border (where, to our amusement, we noticed that they sold fireworks on the Northern Ireland side), and were surprised to find no notice at all of the change in jurisdiction – no border station, no "Welcome to Ireland" sign, nothing but a slight change in how the lane markings were painted.  We had a quick supper in a pub in Drogheda (pronounced "DRO-uh-duh"), and then continued to Dublin.  I had enjoyed familiarizing myself with the Pounds and Pence in the U.K., and I especially enjoyed getting my first glimpse of the brand-new Euro in Ireland, which had replaced the Punt just two months earlier.  The novelty of the Euro was especially evident every time someone would open a till, whereupon the glare from the piles of shiny new coins was almost blinding.

Nothing could have prepared me for driving in Dublin.  And, indeed, nothing did.  It was only through dumb luck that I stumbled to the city center and found one of the hotels on my list.  It couldn't accommodate us for the two nights we would be in Dublin, but the staff helped me find a room at the Trinity Capital, near the Temple Bar shopping district and across the street from Trinity College.  It was a superb place to spend the two nights, all the more enthusiastically received amid of my dread at spending any more time behind the wheel in the Irish capital.  I did have to maneuver my way to a parking garage in the Temple Bar, but the currents and eddies of the Dublin traffic pushed me downstream in just the right direction:  I found it on the first try, and was even able to walk back to the hotel without undue confusion.

 

Friday, March 8, 2002

We spent the first part of our one full day in Ireland traveling up to Newgrange (or Brú na Bóinne) to see the magnificent Neolithic tomb.  It predates Stonehenge and is, indeed, one of the oldest constructions in Great Britain and Ireland.  We weren't permitted to take pictures inside the tomb, but we could get shots of the exterior.  (Click on the image for our Newgrange pictures.)

The trip to Newgrange made it impossible for us to see Blarney Castle.  This was a bit of a disappointment, but we certainly appreciated having time to relax at this point in the trip.

After returning to Dublin (and putting the car safely away), we walked over to the Temple Bar district and looked for souvenirs.  We found very little that could be described as neither "outrageously expensive" nor "embarrassingly tacky," but the excursion was fun, and Billy did find a ring that he liked, with a design reminiscent of the Newgrange spirals.  As we shivered our way from one shop to the next, the proprietors kept telling us how balmy and pleasant the weather was that day.

"Balmy and pleasant" though the weather may have been that day, we certainly got a healthy exposure to the capriciousness of Ireland's climate the next morning as we prepared to leave, though I'm happy to say that the same dumb luck that got us into Dublin eventually got us out.  We had not set an alarm that morning, certain that we had time to catch the noon ferry from Dun Laoghaire (pronounced "dun leery"; Gaelic spelling provides merely a hint as to the pronunciation).  I got bored waiting for Billy to finish his shower, so on a lark I called the ferry confirmation line and was notified that snow in Dublin and rough water in the Irish Sea had caused our crossing to be cancelled, and a later crossing to be in doubt.  The recording mentioned that passengers with reservations on the noon sailing would be accommodated on a 9:30 AM sailing of a different boat from Dublin Port.  It was 8:20 when I heard this, and we still had to pack, check out, retrieve the car, and get through Dublin to the port ... in the snow!  And Billy was still in the shower!

By some miracle we made it to Dublin Port in time – but as of our arrival, Stena Line had cancelled even that one remaining crossing.  However, the drenched attendant at the entry alerted us that Irish Ferries was still taking passengers for a 9:30 sailing.  We promptly got in the wrong line, then corrected ourselves, parked, bought tickets, returned to the car, made our way to the now-empty queue without regard to lane markings or traffic laws, and drove into the hold of the Ulysses with only one car behind us before the massive doors closed shut.  Even the stormy seas ahead were not enough to dissuade us from a feeling of tremendous good fortune.  As it turned out, however, the Ulysses was a massive and stable ship:  It barely wobbled as we made our crossing to Holyhead.  We wondered what the people who considered this a perilous crossing would have thought of the journey to and from Orkney.

When we reached Wales just after lunch, we found ourselves exactly on schedule:  The ferry had taken longer than we planned, but had left earlier.  We drove down the island of Anglesey, stopping quickly to photograph the train station sign in Llanfairpwllgwyngyllgogerychwyrndrobwllllantysiliogogogoch, the town with the longest name in Wales.  (It translates to English as, "The church of St. Mary in a hollow of white hazel near a rapid whirlpool and near St. Tysilio's church by the red cave.")  (Click on the image to see the train station on which the sign is placed.)  Upon crossing to the mainland – or, more accurately, the much larger island of Great Britain – we followed the beautiful north Wales coast to Castle Conwy.  We poked around Conwy a bit to see the castle from all angles, and to enjoy seeing the walled town, and then headed south through Snowdonia.

Snowdonia, the mountainous national park surrounding Snowdon (the highest peak in England and Wales) is an indescribably beautiful region – easily, in my opinion, the most beautiful place we saw on a trip filled with beautiful places.  Water and stone dominated the environment:  At one point we came upon a waterfall plummeting from a cliff on the side of the roadway, literally into a well that had been hollowed from the road – hazardous, but picturesque.  Then we came upon an immense wall literally bridging between two mountains, which we could only assume to be an aqueduct, its face of stone broken only with arches to allow our roadway to cross through, and to allow a river beside us to pass under it.  Farther along, we came to mountains of slate, covered hundreds of feet high with black slate rocks.

We were struggling to get to the south of Wales in time to see Pentre Ifan (pronounced "PEN-tray EE-van") by daylight.  This proved impossible, but we did get to see the massive dolmen, or tomb, by the light of the moon.  It was too cold and dark really to enjoy it as much as we might have, but I did get to touch the stones and to stand underneath the giant top stone.

After Pentre Ifan, we made haste for the motorway to get as far east as possible, in preparation for our flight home the next day. 

 

 

Sunday, March 10, 2002

We had but a little time to spend before making our way to Gatwick, but decided to spend this time returning to Avebury, which we had not seen as much as we wanted on our first day out of London.  It was interesting to drive the very same roads that I had found so unnerving just a week earlier, now feeling quite comfortable with driving on the left.

At Avebury, we walked around the circle just to take in the scope of the place.  The stones themselves are gargantuan (at left), the circle is immense, and all of this is dwarfed by the pit that surrounds the structure (at right).  We explored here until we felt content, and then went to Gatwick to return the car and wait for our flight.

 

 

 

Advice

People don't ask for my advice nearly as often as I wish they would.  Nonetheless, here are a few things I picked up that took me by surprise.  Perhaps reading of them here will help someone else plan more effectively.

  1. There is no yellow center line in the United Kingdom:  The center line is white, just like the lines separating the lanes.  Indeed, the center line in areas where  passing is permitted is often no different than the line between lanes going in the same direction.  In such a circumstance, one must rely on memory, and occasional arrows, to keep in the right place.
  2. A curved arrow in your lane pointing opposite your direction of travel and curving toward the center line is not cause for concern; it is merely a warning to vehicles passing in the other direction to vacate your lane before some impediment, such as a sharp curve or the top of a hill.  The symbol may be in the center of the passing lane (your lane) or it may be superimposed on the center line.  (A straight line pointing opposite your direction of travel does, however, indicate you are going the wrong way.)
  3. You won't have time to figure things out once the car is moving; plan the route when stopped for gas/snacks/sightseeing, or use layabouts (parking areas).  Roundabouts are (usually) convenient for keeping traffic moving, but allow no time for figuring out the route.  There are virtually no stop signs in the U.K. – we drove the length and breadth of Great Britain, and much of Northern Ireland, and saw only three – and there are few traffic lights.
  4. Some roundabouts have two-way traffic flow in them.  One that we experienced like this, the "Magic Roundabout" in Swindon, was one large roundabout with five separate roundabouts at its exit points.
  5. U.K. routes are numbered, but have no direction (north, south, east, west); thus it is easy to find yourself pointing in the wrong direction even when you have found the proper route.  Always know the next few cities through which you will pass to follow the route successfully.
  6. Scotland & Northern Ireland have separate bank notes, which may not be accepted in England & Wales; Bank of England notes are accepted everywhere in the U.K.  Scottish notes are printed by the Bank of Scotland.  Northern Irish notes are printed by various banks, and there is quite a lot of diversity among them.
  7. Street signs in Dublin are not on posts at the intersection; rather, the street name is mounted on a building at the intersection, usually just above the ground floor.
  8. Due to the unpredictability of weather, ferries cannot be expected to run uninterrupted on published schedules; be aware of weather forecasts and check schedule update information.  Passengers with booking on cancelled sailings may be accommodated on earlier sailings, so be prepared for this contingency.  (As for myself, I would not again plan a trip that depended on use of ferries.)
