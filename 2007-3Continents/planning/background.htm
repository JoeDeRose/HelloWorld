May 27, 2006 (328 days to go)

I have tickets!  Well, at least in one direction, I have tickets.  Our departure is April 20, 2007, and airlines will book seats only 331 days in advance.  To use my 200,000 miles to maximum benefit for two tickets, I needed SkySaver, not SkyChoice, seats – so I started as early as the law would allow:  On the 331st day (May 24), nothing was available; on the 330th day, there was still emptiness; on the 329th day, they could get me from Miami to Athens, but not from Atlanta to Miami; but on the 328th day a very diligent Delta representative found me a Delta flight to Düsseldorf, connecting to a Czech Airlines flight to Prague, connecting to another Czech Airlines flight to Athens.  Now I have eleven months to learn how "Athens" is written in Czech.

Language:  Over the last 26 years, I have traveled to 16 countries outside the U.S. – and I still have never spent the night in a place where I didn't have at least a passing familiarity with the language.  That will all change on this trip.  I've studied a bit of Arabic – enough that I can introduce myself, ask a few basic questions, and read and write the script.  But the best I can do with Greek and Turkish is sound it out from the writing.  And I can't even do that with Hebrew.  My plan is to continue the Arabic classes, try to pick up a bit of Greek if time and money allow, and familiarize myself with the Hebrew alphabet and pronunciation.  Jeff is also planning on studying Greek, so that will help overcome our communication barrier.

Next steps:  (1) Get the return flight from Cairo on May 6, 2007.  (2) Get the interior flight from Athens to Tel Aviv and back.  (3) Decide if Turkey stays on the itinerary.  (4) Get the flight to Egypt.  (5) Start considering hotel and car reservations.

May 30, 2006 (325 days to go)

Okay, now I know how to say and read "Athens" in Czech:  Atény.  "Greece," however, is more of a challenge:  Řecko (that's "r̝ɛtsko" for you IPA types).  Apparently, it's pronounced sort of like zhretsko (and if that's not scary enough, the r is trilled and the zh and r are voiced simultaneously).  We'll only be there for an hour and a half; let's hope the signs are clear enough that I don't have to say it.

So, with the outbound ticket in hand I'm starting to do research into the interior transits – (1) Athens to Tel Aviv and back, and (2) either Athens to Cairo or İstanbul to Cairo.  Flying from Greece, I feel like I should make an effort to use Olympic Airways, but, unfortunately, that's not gonna happen.  I'm not put off (too much) by the fact that its six-ring (!) logo looks like it was very carefully crafted to avoid trademark infringement – but the airline earn demerits in virtually every area I could assess:  Its only flight to Tel Aviv leaves every night/morning at 2:20 AM and arrives at Ben Gurion at 4:15 AM.  And almost as inconvenient is that the only Olympic flight from Athens to Cairo leaves at 12:50 AM and arrives in Egypt at 2:45 AM.  Its website invites one to check in online via "ONLINE SERVICES - e-checkin" [sic], but no such menu exists.  The deal-breaker, though, was when I learned that the frequent flyer plan is called the "Icarus Club" (!). One thing I'll say for it, though:  The drop-down menus on the Greek-language version provided a wealth of information on how destination cities were written in Greek.  (I was having a dickens of a time trying to find İstanbul in the iotas.)

June 10, 2006 (314 days to go)

The return tickets were a breeze!  On the first call a Delta representative was able to find us Affaires (Business) class seats on Air France, connecting in Paris on a four-and-a-half hour layover.  She offered to put me on the waitlist for an earlier flight, but I prefer the bird in hand to the birds in the bush.  Anyway, the long layover may give us time to take a taxi downtown and see the Eiffel Tower or the Louvre.

Last week at Billy's renal appointment, his doctor said he may be nearing the point of placement on the kidney/pancreas transplant list.  It's unclear whether travel would be completely prohibited, or prohibited only if he were near the top of the list just prior to departure, and it's also unclear when and if he will be placed on that list – but it adds another layer of complexity to the planning.  Fortunately, Delta will allow re-booking or cancellation (and refund of the SkyMiles) for only $50.

July 9, 2006 (285 days to go)

So in the forward-thinking parts of the eco community, a lot of attention is being given to the burden of air travel on our biosphere, and the attention ain't pretty:  Lonely Planet, an agency I respect, offers the following on the Responsible Travel page of its website:  "[T]wo people taking a return flight between Europe and the US will contribute as much to climate change as an average household's gas and electricity consumption over a whole year."

They offer a solution, though:  carbon offsets, which promote energy and reforestation projects that will offset the rate of change.  For the legs of our trip, Billy and I will jointly be responsible for the generation of 5.63 tons of CO2 (or, more precisely, to various greenhouse gases which are measured in CO2 equivalents).  This breaks down as follows, according to the convenient calculator at Climate Care:

Flight      Tons CO2 (one-way; 2 passengers)
Atlanta to Düsseldorf   2.04
Düsseldorf to Prague   0.28
Prague to Athens   0.35
Athens to Tel Aviv   0.13
Amman to Cairo   0.15
Cairo to Paris   0.70
Paris to Atlanta   1.98

I've never been into carbon offsets before, as I feel like it's better to reduce my footprint than to adopt a "pay-to-pollute" attitude.  But I feel like a lot of social good – not to mention personal fulfillment – comes from international travel, so this is not an area in which I am prepared to cut back.  Instead, I'm thinking seriously about buying carbon offsets for this trip, and making a decision to do so on an ongoing basis for my air travel.  It's not a trivial amount of money, but it's not unmanageable either:  Offsetting the 5.63 tons of CO2 for which Billy and I will be responsible comes to about $78.06, less than the amount we might splurge on a nice meal in a world capital somewhere.

I'll give it more thought, but this in the direction in which I'm leaning.

July 16, 2006 (278 days to go)

Jeff is thinking of this as a Greece-Egypt trip, and isn't even planning on joining our excursion into Israel and Jordan.  But for me, this has always been a trip to the Mideast.  It already looks like time constraints will force us to drop Turkey – but truthfully, it is only convenience and expediency (and the fact that I already have tickets to it) that keeps Greece on my itinerary.  Indeed, the trip has existed in the back of my mind for years, through many variations and whimsies:

  • A monumental road trip from Athens to Cairo (back when peace and open borders seemed a possibility);
  • A sampler platter of nations that included diversions into not only Turkey, but also Cyprus, Syria, and Lebanon;
  • A water exploration around the eastern Mediterranean, focusing more on the ports of call than on the interiors; and, finally,
  • The current air route between capitals (which is really founded in a recognition of Otto von Bismark's famous quote:  "Politics is the art of the possible").

Nonetheless, despite the ongoing flexibility, I really like this itinerary – so it is with much frustration that I read daily in the papers and on CNN that Israel and Hezbollah are vowing all-out war.  As I write, the altercation is only affecting Gaza and the Israel-Lebanon border, but I am fearful that the problem will escalate such that Jordan or Egypt (or both) will close borders with Israel (or worse).  A lot can happen in the Middle East in nine months, and all I can do now is hope that what happens is good.

I do have a Plan B (actually, it's probably more like Plan H) to drop Israel and Jordan, add some Greek Islands (I'd like to see Rhodes and Santorini; Billy is heavily interested in Crete), and restore Turkey.  But I hope it doesn't come to that.

July 28, 2006 (266 days to go)

I've learned – sometimes with joy, sometimes with frustration – that if Billy drops it into casual conversation that a thing might be fun, I should give that a lot of weight.  He knows I enjoy planning these trips, so he drops a casual hint and leaves it there for me to pick up or ignore.  But his instincts are superb.  Many of our most pleasant diversions in the U.K. and Ireland came because of his suggestions.  It was his research and planning that took us to Ercolano, which was easily the highlight of our last Italy trip.  And he recommended Palenque on the Mexico trip – but I skipped it to my ongoing regret for an unsatisfying visit to Chichén Itzá.

Billy has mentioned, in passing, that Crete sounds like fun.  I'm not going to make the Palenque mistake:  Instead, I've dropped Monemvasia and Mystras, and juggled some schedules so that we can sail to Heraklio and see Knossos and Phaesos, and spend the night somewhere on the island.  And a bonus:  The time issues in returning to Athens and continuing onward to Israel with this plan may give us an eight-hour layover in Larnaca, so that we can see a bit of Cyprus.

September 16 (216 days to go)

I've started the real Arabic 101 class.  The Center for Lifelong Learning class provided a good introduction to the language, but I'm convinced I'll emerge from the 101 class with a much better level of comprehension.

I had a brief flirtation (really, a torrid one-night stand) with the idea of going to Siwa, an oasis in far western Egypt near the Libran border.  The ruins of the old town of Shali would have been the top draw, with other interesting sites being the Mountain of the Dead (Roman burial sites) and the Oracle of Amun where Alexander was proclaimed a deity.  The nine-hour bus ride, the limited access, and the fact that it would have made Abu Simbel impossible, forced me, however, to reconsider.

I'm still deciding whether a Modern Greek for Travelers class through the Center for Lifelong Learning will be worth the money ($130), time (1.5 hours, eight sessions), and distraction from Arabic.  I'll probably do it, but a decision has to be made soon; it starts on October 2.

We've put Monemvasia back on the itinerary, made possible by not staying overnight on Crete.  (None of the more attractive cities are near where we plan to be.  As for getting to Tel Aviv, I had been planning a morning flight to Larnaca, a 10-hour sightseeing layover in Cyprus, and a late flight into Tel Aviv (arriving at 11:30 PM).  For a little more, though, we can take a late morning flight to İstanbul, have a 2-hour layover there, and arrive in Tel Aviv in mid-afternoon.  It tears at my soul to set foot in İstanbul with no opportunity to see it, and I would have loved to have seen something off the beaten path like Cyprus.  But the late arrival in Israel is a deal-breaker.  I think I have to go to İstanbul.

September 25 (207 days to go)

So now that I've given in on leaving the mainland, I'm considering an entire switch to the islands for the Greek part of the trip:  at least one night on Santorini, and maybe a night on Rhodes if I can swing it.  Rhodes looks incredible, but its location is a damnable inconvenience.  This would involve dropping Monemvasia and Meteora – but I'll eventually get back to Greece.  I could include Meteora in a future Greece-Turkey trip.  And Monemvasia may not be that important.  For a little while I was starting to worry that people were right when they told me I had packed too many things into one trip – but now I've come to realize that I have not denied myself anything; rather I am simply seeing a greater diversity of attractions than most people.  So what if the twenty amazing things I see are scattered across four nations, while the twenty amazing things on somebody else's trip might be all in a single peninsula?  I'm still seeing things, fulfilling a lifelong dream, and saving on hotel bills with overnight transportation options such that I can afford some nicer hotels when we do sleep in a place that anchored to the ground.  And most important, traveling like this makes me happy.  Forget the dichotomy between "tourist" and "traveler"; neither applies to my style – call me a "voyager."

Anyway, I look forward to the day that I start making reservations for interior transits and hotels.  Then I won't be able to change my mind any more.

October 8 (194 days to go)

I'm hip deep in my Arabic class now, and feel like I could comfortably introduce myself and ask basic questions.  Reading the script is not nearly as hard as it seems, even though the 28 letters manage to mold themselves into 86 different forms (shaped from 28 or so discrete formations embellished with dots and tails).  Yet note, as you try to share my pain, that the handwritten versions of some letters differ greatly from the printed versions.  Also note that this does not include nine variations of the vowel marks (ـِـ [kasra], ـُـ [Damma], ـَـ [fatha], ـٍـ [kasra tanween], ـٌـ [Damma tanween], ـًـ [fatha tanween], ـّـ [shadda], ـْـ  [sukkun], and ـٰـ [dagger alif]).  Nor does it include the hamza (a consonant not included, for Qur'anic reasons, in the regular alphabet) that can manifest itself independently (ء), seated on the ي (as ئ), the و (as ؤ), and the ا, and that in the latter case it may be one of its two basic forms (أ or إ, depending on pronunciation), or elidable (ٱ), or lengthened (آ). Generally, one must know how to read text without the vowel marks, and must also know the hierarchy of where they appear, ranging from fully vocalized text (e.g., Qur'an) down to non-vocalized text (e.g., newspapers). October 15, 2006 (187 days to go)

So about fifteen minutes ago I was standing in the shower thinking about the trip (which is the routine almost every morning), and had the blood-draining realization that I couldn't remember checking our travel dates against holidays in the Julian calendar.  Greece, and the Greek Orthodox Church, both use the Gregorian calendar for official purposes.  The latter even excommunicates "palaiomerologitai" who insist on using the Julian Calendar for daily purposes (except the monks on Mount Athos, who get a pass).  But for doctrinal reasons Easter is calculated by the Julian Calendar.  And Easter comes in the spring.  And if Easter happened to come on April 22, 2007, that would be a disaster.  Fortunately, after drying off, and walking the dogs, and panicking inwardly, I found that Easter falls earlier in 2007:  It is celebrated in both the Gregorian and Julian calendars on the same day:  NS April 8, 2007 (OS March 26, 2007 for you palaiomerologitai out there).

November 13, 2006 (158 Days to Go)

So I learned something interested today:  There is a vote (misguided in my opinion) to name seven wonders of the world to replace the historic seven wonders – as only one of the ancient wonders is still available for viewing. (And, oh, by the way:  we'll be viewing it on Day 15 of this trip.)

The contest started with almost 200 entries, pared down to 77, and then to 21.  The winning seven will be announced on July 7 of next year (07/07/07, get it?).  Anyway, the cool part is that Billy and I have already been to almost a quarter of the finalists, and with this trip we will have seen eight of the twenty-one.  The purple blocks below are the ones we've seen; green are the ones we'll see this time; blue is the one that I've seen but Billy hasn't; and gray – someday!

Eiffel Tower Great Wall of China Hagia Sophia Kiyomizu Temple Kremlin and Red Square Machu Picchu Neusch-wanstein

But even though it's fun to be able to say you've seen things that make lists like this, it's clearly just a popularity contest:  Tikal is far more spectacular than Chichén Itzá; the Colosseum is amazing, but so are the catacombs down the Via Appia Antica; Avebury is more stunning than Stonehenge.

And here's something I learned just while writing these paragraphs:  I can offer these opinions because I've seen more – far more – than the average tourist on my vacations.  I've really be hammered with this trip, more than usual, by the accusation that I "rush through" my experiences to "check something off a list" rather than give it the time and attention it deserves.  (Never mind that, as this document proves, I spend months in advance giving every flyspeck on the map more attention than my accusers ever would.)  They say (not without a hint of condescension) that they stop at least long enough to read the plaques – but I've read all the plaques already; all that is left is to see a place and touch its earth and smell the air around it and hear the sounds in its vicinity and speak to the people that live in its shadow and taste the food that their culture has refined. And you don't get that on a plaque.

Yet with this ill-conceived contest, I discover that my "rushing through" now offers its own defense:  In short, I don't have to settle for Chichén Itzá (just up the expressway from the Cancún airport), because I also (though it required driving eight hours across three nations) saw Tikal.

December 11, 2006 (130 Days to Go)

It's (almost) exactly as if English lists were prefixed with letters in the following order: A, B, E, H, Z, K, F, P, V, W, X, Y, L, R, T, N, U, J, M, C, D, G, I, O, Q, S.

Even for ordinary alphabetization, Arabic offers some hair-pulling challenges.  The language is built around word roots that are usually three consonants, which are modified with all sorts of writing gymnastics to make new words.  To use an example, كتاب (kitaab) means "book" and مكتبة (maktaba) means "library" and كتب (kataba) means "he read."  Because these are all formed by the same three-consonant root (ك ت ب or k t b), they are listed side-by-side in the dictionary.  It is as if an English dictionary showed the following words together on the same page:  "bespoken," "misspoke," "speaker," "spoke," "well-spoken"; it works just fine if you know the language.  But if you don't know which of the letters form the root, and which ones are the add-ons, you've got several possibilities to check.

And how's this for an oddity:  There's at least twelve different standard ways to make singular nouns plural, which involve adding letters to, or removing them from, the beginning, middle, and/or end of the word.  For example, the plural of مدينة (madeena or "city") is مدن (mudun) and the plural of دكتور (duktuur or "doctor") is دكاترة (dakaatira).  But all the verbs are regular!

December 28, 2006 (113 Days to Go)

Here are a few random Arabic facts:

  • In the early days of Arabic, it was the rural Bedouins who were regarded as speaking the most pure form of the language. And wealthy families would often send their sons to live in the desert so that they could learn to shoot, hunt, and speak properly.
  • Arabic has only three vowels: ا (corresponds to a/e), و (corresponds to u), and ي (corresponds to i). But each of the three has both a normal and emphatic pronunciation (for ا, the normal pronunciation is like the e in bet; the emphatic pronunciation is like the a in father). And for each of the two pronunciations, the duration can be short (which corresponds to what we think of as normal in English) or long (twice as long as short). And, as in English, they can be stressed (accented) or unstressed.
  • The script is written right-to-left, but the numbers are written left-to-right.
  • Arabic distinguishes only three parts of speech: nouns (reference to objects), verbs (reference relative to time), and particles (make the sentence complete). What we understand in English as adjectives and pronouns, and even some adverbs and propositions, are, in Arabic, "nouns" («اسماء»).
  • Arabic verbs can come either before or after the noun without changing the meaning of the sentence. (verb-noun constructions doesn't change the sentence to a question as in many romance and germanic languages.) But here's the odd part: If the verb comes first, you use the singular congugation, while if the noun comes first, the verb must properly reflect the distinction between singular and plural (viz., "he says," "says he," "they say," "says they").
  • There are a couple of stranges rules regarding numbers that preceed nouns (quantities of 3 and above): (1) where they have gender (all except multiples of ten) they take the opposite gender of the noun; (2) the noun is in the plural only for 3-10, and in the singular for 11 and above.

January 9, 2007 (101 Days to Go)

And here's an interesting tidbit about the Greek alphabet: The only language it is used to write is Greek. What makes this odd is that it is (1) the oldest alphabet in the world (so it has had time to expand) and (2) it is ubiquitous (the only non-Latin script to show up in ASCII, for example). Yet as spoken languages around the world have been put to print, various obscure, isolated, and particular languages have been incorporated with just about every other style: Chinese characters show up in Japanese and Korean; Arabic is used for Farsi, among many others; Bengali applies itself also to Assamese; Cyrillic spreads beyond Russia to many Balkan and central Asian languages; Devangari is distributed to languages throughout south Asia; Hebrew characters are used in Ladino and Yiddish; even styles that are further off the beaten path (Baybayin, Georgian, Ge'ez) show up in more than one language. But only Greek is written in Greek.

The visa process is underway. I've always held up the Grateful Dead Ticket Procedure as the hallmark of a process that separates an anal-retentive rule follower from (let's say, ironically) an unmotivated pothead. For Egypt, after our passports are returned with the Jordanian visas, I have to prepare two more applications, with pictures; attach them to our passports; enclose a money order for the cost of the visas plus the cost of return postage ($15.50); and send it all to the Embassy of Egypt using any service (but preferably Federal Express).

The postal service has confirmed that my package has been delivered to the Jordanian embassy. In about a week we'll know if I'm enough of an anal-retentive rule follower.

February 17, 2007 (62 days to go)

This trip will take me to nine countries (ten if you count the Palestinian Territories), five (or six) of which I have never visited before. But four of those countres (and two of the ones I've never visited), are just airport transits. Thus, I've been giving some thought as to the minimum requirement for saying that you have been in a country. I've heard a few divergent ideas, in order from least to most restricting:

  1. You must be conscious of being in the country.
  2. You must be subject to its laws.
  3. You must take a breath of air in the country.
  4. You must exit the airplane (or car or train or whatever vehicle you used to get there).
  5. You must be legally admited.
  6. You must go outside in the open air.
  7. You must touch the earth with your feet.
  8. You must have a meal in the country.
  9. You must spend a night in the country.

Some countries don't even lend themselves to all of these items: Vatican City, for example, has no facility for spending the night. I'm not aware of a public restaurant there (although perhaps there was one in the Musei Vaticani). And I don't think there is any public area in Vatican City that is unpaved, allowing you to touch the earth with your feet.

But on the flip side, how useful are any of these? To this question, I must offer two countries from my own experience:

  • Austria: There is absolutely no basis for dispute that Billy and I have been to Austria: We were consciously there as we took tens of thousands of breaths of Austrian air. We repeatedly exited the car we used to get there. We were officially admitted by the Gendarmerie at the Swiss-Austrian border (had our passports checked and our trunk searched and everything) and were thus subject to their laws. We stood in the open air. Our feet touched the unpaved earth. We ate several (wonderful) meals. And we spent the night in Nassereith. But we have little in common with people who have traveled to Austria: We have not seen Vienna or Salzburg or Innsbruck; we have no overlapping frame of reference for tourist activities; we simply entered a corner of the country and – by chance, not intention – managed to jump through every hoop that has been presented as a marker for having visited a nation.
  • San Marino: Before I discuss San Marino, let me start closer to home: the United States of America. It would be absurd even to go through the checklist for the United States – I'm a U.S. citizen resident in the U.S.; clearly I have been to the United States of America. And I have seen a lot of the U.S., but by percentage I have seen more – a lot more – of San Marino. But I haven't been in the position to check off every item on the list. In fact, there are several countries that I have seen (due to relative size, mostly) more of than my own country. It would be crazy to say that I haven't visited such places just because I didn't spend the night or have a meal or whatever.

I feel most comfortable with an extremely generous assessment of whether a country has been visited. In fact, the only situation I can imagine in which one might be in a country's physical space without having visited it would fall when you are traveling in a vehicle that dips into the country briefly but at a time when you are unaware of its having done so – for example, train tracks in Europe often dip into a neighboring country for a short distance to avoid some impediment, but without stopping and without the border being announced to passengers.

So here is the philosophical ground on which I land: After this trip is over, I will have visited (in shaa' Allah) the Czech Republic and Cyprus. I won't have seen them thoroughly; in fact, for most purposes I won't have seen them at all. But I will have visited them. And I hope that someday I will get back to both of them – but when I do, it won't be my first time there.
