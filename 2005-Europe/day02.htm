We departed early through a slippery glaze of ice and snow, and drove west from the mountains of the Tyrol through the 13.97 km Arlberg Tunnel, the third longest road tunnel in the world.  Our route then took us back through the (Swiss-controlled) customs station at the Liechtenstein border, through Vaduz, and back into Switzerland.

Our original plan had been to go through St. Moritz, which would have brought us two enticing opportunities:  First, we would be able to take the ninety-second taxi ride as passengers on the St. Moritz bobsled run, the only remaining natural bob run in the world.  And second, we would pass through that tiny corner of the world where dwell the one percent of the Swiss who still speak Romansch on the street.  The former would have been really, really cool – but the latter was a more enduring fascination for me.  Unfortunately, we learned before leaving home that the bob run was closed for a race and thus St. Moritz fell from our itinerary.  So as we turned from highway 13 to go west on highway 3, we got as close as we would ever get – 10 km to be exact – to the canton (let's call it "Grischun," although the Germans would say "Graubünden"; the Italians, "Grigioni"; and the French, "Grisons") that acts as the linguistic preserve of this tongue.  Romansch fascinates me not only for its rarity, but also for its almost ridiculous flexibility.  Even the name of the language is highly elastic, showing up in English as "Romansch," "Romansh," "Rumantsch," "Romanche," "Rhaeto-Romance," and "Rheto-Romance."  And when it comes down to actually speaking the language, it's really a moving target:  There are four or five dialects (depending on whether Putér and Vallader are counted separately or lumped together as "Ladin") and a fifth (or sixth) dialect that is the standardized written form.  Few phrases are identical in all, and many words, including common words like "chicken" and "I" are entirely different in each dialect.  The following comparison may shine a spotlight into this haze:

English chicken I The fox was again famished.
Romansh Sursilvan gaglina jeu L'uolp era puspei inagada fomentada.
Sutsilvan gagliegna jou La vualp eara puspe egn'eada fumantada.
Surmiran gagligna ja La golp era puspe eneda famantada.
Ladin Putér gillina eau La vuolp d'eira darcho üna vouta famanteda.
Vallader giallina eu La vuolp d'eira darcheu üna jada fomantada.
Rumantsch Grischun giaglina jau La vulp era puspè ina giada fomentada.

We drove past Zürichsee (the lake south of Zürich) and finally to Luzern, where we stopped for lunch at an attractive but otherwise forgettable shop facing the lake.  After lunch we explored the beautiful Chapel Bridge, which is a gorgeous monument that cuts across the surprisingly shallow lake at an odd diagonal to the streets of the city.  In joists above the walkway are seventeenth century paintings that illustrate Swiss history.  And near the center of the bridge is the water tower, which has also been used as a treasury, prison, and torture chamber – the latter to great effect as the screams of the accused would echo across the lake.

After lunch, we traveled with our mouths agape past even more spectacular views in the Bernese Oberland, in ribbons of roadway past ice and granite cliffs, around frigid lakes, and through tunnels and avalanche shelters too numerous to count.  It was spectacular in every way, and all doubt of whether we had been wise to plan a trip to Switzerland in January evaporated like the mist of our breath.  This is truly a great country to see.  And this is truly a great time to see it.

Traveling south, we went as far as the road would take us in a long narrow valley, and crossed the Alps in a car train through the Lötschberg Tunnel from Kandersteg to Goppenstein.  (If size matters, this is the twenty-eighth longest rail tunnel in the world, at 14.61 km.)  The car train experience is utterly surreal:  One drives the car onto a flatbed train as if it were just another lane in the road, parks the car without assistance (moving as far forward as possible while ensuring that all four wheels are on the same flatbed), puts the car in park, and waits.  The train begins to move, lumbering at first and then shooting forward.  And inside the tunnel absolutely nothing is visible except the faint outline of the car ahead as you're zooming through at high speed.  The hypnotic effect of the rhythmic motion and darkness lends itself to drowsiness that is occasionally interrupted by an instantaneous sensation of panic as you notice that you're going about 60 and the car ahead of you is two feet away.  That dream you have where you're falling:  It's like 20 minutes of that.  (The image at right is Loetschy, the mascot of the tunnel, adopting the same facial expression and posture that I exhibited several times during the short trip.)

   

Car Train Ticket
   

Finally, you come out of the tunnel and the world is bathed in light.  Moments later the train stops, and with a very Swiss efficiency everyone drives forward off the other end of the train and resumes the journey into Wallis (Valais).

We continued to the south, out of one long narrow valley into another, up to the point where the road ended in Täsch.  Our journey was to Zermatt, a little farther south – but since cars are forbidden in Zermatt, we parked the car in Täsch, bought train tickets, and rode the railway (part of it cogwheel) up to Zermatt.  (Here the luggage carts were even more useful than at the Zürich airport:  You could put your luggage in a cart in Täsch, roll it on the train, latch it in place, and roll it off in Zermatt.)

   

Round-Trip Ticket between Täsch and Zermatt
   

Our visit to Zermatt had been conceived around taking part in the Zermatt Gorge Adventure, for which much preparation, purchasing of warm clothes, and confirmation phone calls had been expended. So when I called the Alpine Center as instructed after our arrival, we were greatly disappointed to be told that, unfortunately, the gorge was closed ... for "maintenance" ... of which they had been unaware two days before ... and that it was unlikely to reopen "for some time." This was particularly odd as the Gorge Adventure is known in Zermatt as the thing to do when nothing else is possible, as the tours are given in any weather.  I don't know exactly what went wrong in the gorge – but as disappointing as it was, we may have been lucky that it happened one day before our visit rather than during it.

As a town without cars, all transportation in Zermatt is done by foot, bus, sleigh, or "Elektro Taxi."  I really wanted to find a sleigh – but, unable to do so, we instead took one of the strangely boxy Elektro Taxis to the Pension Hotel Mischabel and checked in.

Sleigh in Zermatt
  

Elektro Taxi

Rick Steves described the Mischabel as "the rustic kind of place you imagined staying at in Switzerland" – and it was exactly that indeed: completely constructed of old, somewhat creaky, exposed wood, from the stairs to the walls to the doors to the furniture to the balconies. And those balconies had a gorgeous view of the mountain that is Zermatt's claim to fame:  It's called "Cervinia" in Italian and "Le Cervin" in French, but you and the Germans know it as the "Matterhorn."  The Mischabel was extremely cheap by Zermatt standards – perhaps because it had no elevator to take us up the four flights to our room and because the bathroom was in the hall. This was my first exposure to a hotel with a shared bathroom and I was glad for the experience; in the end, I am surprised to realize that I have no strong preference for or against en suite facilities – and if it saves me 150 francs to traipse down the hall, all the better.

After resting briefly at the hotel, we wandered the main square and had that wonderful fondue dinner that we had been dreaming of since Switzerland was first added to our itinerary.
