Switzerland     Zermatt
        Gornergrat

With the influence, perhaps, of the rest of the hotel's construction, the extremely firm mattresses at the Mischabel were not entirely unlike old, somewhat creaky, exposed wood, and the down pillows only reinforced the impression of lying on a cushy plank.  But we slept well nonetheless, and I have fond memories of the Mischabel; I would return there in a flash.

Speaking of creaky exposed wood, Switzerland is overrun with little wooden shacks that seem to provide some benefit to the livestock – perhaps to store feed, or provide shelter to the animals, or both.  These rustic shacks are really quite picturesque, and we enjoyed seeing them on snow-covered fields and inclines alongside mountains everywhere we went.  I took an early stroll through Zermatt as Billy was getting ready, and found these shacks even in downtown Zermatt.

Livestock Shacks
  

Livestock Shack

As we checked out, I noticed that the Mischabel is adjacent to the Hotel Atlanta. I went in to ask if there was any connection to our hometown, but the proprietor claimed not.  As the conversation was taking place in broken German, I was unable to get any further information.

   

Hotel Atlanta
   

Try as we might to regroup and make the best of the Gorge Adventure cancellation, Zermatt provided a series of minor disappointments, impediments, and delays to follow up on that major one: We first tried to go paragliding, but it was impossible due to wind. We then tried to take ski lessons, but both major agencies were fully booked. We planned to visit the Glacier Paradise exhibit, but were informed that the lift was unexpectedly closed (although, in fairness, it did open later in the day). And even as night fell, we waited in a cold train station for nearly an hour because our train left early without us.

So our efforts at amusement in Zermatt were relegated to third-tier offerings. We walked around town for a bit and then bought a one-day lift pass so we could explore the mountains.  First we took the cable subway to Sunnegga (yellow line on the map) where we had another delicious meal of cheese fondue. (It was so generous, in fact, that we couldn't finish, despite our best efforts – and the cook came out to see if we had been dissatisfied. After three visits in four years, I must say that Europeans might enhance their awareness of portion control.)

Then we took a cable car to Blauherd (orange line on the map) to check out the view of the Matterhorn. Blauherd is one of the points known for having the "classic" view of that mountain, with its jagged overhang that makes it look as if the Matterhorn is tilting left.  The cable car was a fun ride, too, as we rode beneath its thin ribbon of steel above jagged rocky chasms.  (I know children and old people ride these devices without concern; for some it's even their daily commute to work; but to us it felt adventurous.)

When the lift to Klein Matterhorn reopened in the afternoon we took the opportunity to visit the Glacier Paradise, which was a pleasant and lengthy journey on a day when we had time to spare:  First we had to take a bus to the far side of town, where this lift system meets Zermatt.  Then we rode a cable car to Furi (light blue line on the map), a shockingly crowded gondola to Trockener Steg (dark blue line on the map), and finally a slightly less crowded gondola to Klein Matterhorn (purple line on the map).

Zermatt from the cable car
     

Matterhorn from the cable car

I'm suspicious of things that are named in English when they rest in a non-English-speaking land – but the Glacier Paradise was pretty cool (and, for what it's worth, free):  It's a cave fifteen meters under the surface of the Oberer Theodulgletscher, inside which one can see the glacier's natural crevasses, an assortment of (somewhat cheesy) ice sculptures, and the exhibit on ice fleas that live in the glacier.

On Klein Matterhorn, we also climbed the stairs to Europe's highest observation platform, which was the highest place I have ever been while still touching the earth (3,883 m, 12,736', above sea level).  This was a climb of about sixty steps from the station.  At ground level – if I'm breathing warm air that has oxygen in it – I could be up there in less than fifteen seconds.  But we were not at ground level breathing warm air with oxygen in it, and Billy and I took almost half an hour to climb this short staircase.  I don't know how people make it to the top of Everest.

(Even months afterward, I still look at this picture and the one of Monte Rosa and think, "Wow – I actually saw that!")
 

The Klein Matterhorn station and the Oberer Theodulgletscher beyond it.  The entrance to the Glacier Paradise grotto is a small tube directly behind the stairs in the bottom center of the picture.  On the glacier, everything in the foreground is Switzerland; beginning at the ridge is Italy.

Atop the observation platform we made the acquaintance of an Australian couple.  They ranted at us a bit about American immigration policy.  (We promised to do what we could about that.)  But we enjoyed talking with them on the gondola ride down, and sat with them for a long time at a café at Trockener Steg while we all tried to recover from the brutal environment at the top of the mountain.

Finally, when it became clear that we would all survive, we continued downward to Zermatt.  Back in town, we did a little shopping in the watch, clock, and chocolate shops, and I bought a wonderful clock that I hope will be shipped to America without incident.

   

The clock that I bought
   

During our first trip to Italy, in which we used trains as our primary mode of transportation, we were pleasantly surprised to find that they left unfailingly on time – almost to the second.  Now in Switzerland (which has a reputation for obsessive punctuality), we wondered how they could differentiate themselves from the Italians (who lack that reputation).  Zermatt gave us our answer:  They run their trains early.

We wanted to be on the 5:12 PM train to Gornergrat, so we would be able to claim our room and rest a bit before supper, so we retrieved our luggage and went to the station at the appointed time.  Though not quite as early as we intended, we did arrive at 5:10 PM (according to both the station clock and my watch), only to see the 5:12 train disappearing up the mountain.

After swinging and missing on our first attempt, we eventually caught the train at 6:00 and made it to Gornergrat without further frustration (green line on the map).  There, in a wonderful reversal of fortune, we were greeted (at the station! by name!) by the proprietor. What made it all even cooler was that he had us arrange ourselves and our luggage on the front blade of a bulldozer, raised the blade, and drove us up thusly and directly to the front door of the Hotel Kulm Gornergrat.

And the Gornergrat was fantastic! It must be said that it bears a resemblance – in countenance if not in actual appearance – to the hotel in The Shining. But the hotel was wonderful indeed, with long wide halls, cozy warm rooms, and a delicious restaurant where we enjoyed our half-board (i.e., supper and breakfast included) meals. Especially noteworthy were the clear tomato essence soup with lemon dumplings, the cheese rösti (hash brown pancake, a Swiss specialty) with cheese and a fried egg, and the orange mousse. I must say that I lose more faith in the rating system every time I stay in a European hotel: The Gornergat was rated at only two stars, but was a fantastic location. They may have lost points because there is no elevator and the bathrooms are in the hall – but ambience should count for something.  In fact, ambience should count for a lot.
