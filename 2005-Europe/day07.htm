San Marino

    Notes on the Flag:  The three towers in the center of the coat of arms are the towers that protect San Marino:  Guaita, Cesta, and Montale.  In the coat of arms, each tower is topped with an ostrich feather.

The rest of our crowd went wine tasting in Chianti, which didn't really interest Billy and me – both teetotalers.  Since there were six others, and since each car could barely squeeze five, we were offered our reprieve as follows:  "All you have to do is convince one other person to go with you, and then you won't have to do the Chianti trip."  Our goal was to enjoy our vacation, not to serve as a free tour bus, so we weren't going to Chianti whether or not we could "convince" another person to go with us.  Fortunately no drama evolved, as Kyle preferred to spend the day at home resting, the five others packed in Tim's car for wine tasting, and Billy and I drove alone to San Marino.

Flag of San Marino
  

A Sammarinese license plate and its associated "RSM" (Repubblica di San Marino) sticker

We had spent a little while in San Marino on our previous trip, but Billy had stayed back while I explored the Prima Torre, and I had skipped the Seconda Torre in the interest of not delaying our group. This return offered us a coveted second chance at San Marino.

We Americans tend to think of Europe as an ancient place, because its buildings and structures are so much older than anything we have here at home.  This is true, of course – but what we forget is that our government is typically older, and sometimes much older, than the governments there.  There are, however, exceptions – and San Marino is the most glaring of these exceptions.  Founded at the dawn of the fourth century in 301 CE, it is the oldest state in Europe – older, indeed, than just about anything in it.

We parked at the top of the hill near the Porta San Francesco and immediately made our way up the ramping streets to the Prima Torre (First Tower, also known as "La Rocca" or "Guaita").  Once there we climbed the tower, walked along the ramparts, and looked in the chapel.

After the Prima Torre, we walked the path to the Seconda Torre (Second Tower, also known as "La Cesta" or "Fratta").  This precarious walkway runs along the cliff against which San Marino is set, and we gawked a bit at the cliff's vertical drop.

The sun set on us in San Marino, so we had dinner at a cliffside restaurant, descended the hill back to the Porta San Francesco, and drove the three-and-a-half hours back to the villa.  We had enjoyed the sights on the ride to San Marino along the back roads through Le Marche – but for the trip back we decided to take the autostrada on a route through Bologna and Firenze; it was much longer in distance, but took the same amount of time and was easier and more comfortable.
