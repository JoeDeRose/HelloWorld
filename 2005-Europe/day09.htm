First we drove to Arezzo (Kyle, Billy, and the empty seat with me; the five others in Tim's car) with the arrangement that we would park at the train station and meet at the ticket office.  We had to rush to catch the others after they sent me a text message:

Not at the train station went to look for bathroom text us when you arrive and we will find a place to meet.

If today's occurrence had been the only time this happened without apology, it would be annoying enough – but it had already happened multiple times (without apology).  And the result was always that we would have to run to the new location, arrive exhausted, and then rush to see things that they had already seen. So our first few minutes in Arezzo were taken up with me snapping about how I didn't want it to happen again.

After this discussion concluded, our first order of sightseeing business was to visit the Basilica di San Francesco, with its chapel containing a fresco series by Piero della Francesca detailing the story of the True Cross. Normally, this sort of experience would be lost on me, but the admission fee included a very detailed audio description that informed us regarding the composition of the fresco series and its individual components.  I wish all of the art sites were so well thought out.

We then had lunch, which I had to leave early to feed the parking meter by my car – a task that I admit fed my not-completely-abated resentment a bit.  If we had all gathered together in Arezzo at the appointed place, I would have known that the original plan to leave Arezzo after lunch had been modified, and could have made parking arrangements accordingly.  As it was, however, I didn't learn of the updated plan until lunch – so I had to finish early, run back to the car, pay, and run back up the hill to meet the others at the Duomo



I did have a pleasant time alone, though.  My route back to the car was along an attractive tree-line boulevard.  (You don't notice how rare urban vegetation is in Italy, until you suddenly find some.)  And on the run up the hill I found myself intrigued by a plaque at the birth home of Guido Monaco.  I had never heard of Guido Monaco before, but later learned that he had invented what we know today as the modern musical scale (ut re mi fa sol la, or something like that).  What intrigued me, instead, was the odd style of Roman numerals in which the date was written.  I had assumed, incorrectly, that the plaque was very old, or a copy of one that was very old.  This was not the case – but the style of Roman numerals was archaic, as unusual in 1821 when the plaque was set ( = 1821; see below) as they are today.  What I learned was that Roman numerals initially used a system of backward Cs to form numbers upward from 500.  (Note that the later use of D for 500 evolved from the shape .)  It would have been cooler if the plaque were more than 184 years old, but this is exactly the kind of knowledge I enjoy accumulating

Once reconvened at the Duomo (which we found closed), our group took a meandering path back to the point of divergence for our two cars, past some other ancient buildings and a Roman amphitheater.  There had been some enthusiasm among the group for going inside the amphitheater, but after I had translated the Italian sign to figure out how, and after six of the eight of us had signed in (as was required) at the archeological museum, J.R. and Tim instructed that there was not time and we needed to come along with them.

So this is what I got out of Arezzo:  a lot of exercise, and a little wisdom that might come in useful later.

For the afternoon we drove down to Cortona, where we walked up to the Duomo to look in it and tour the nearby Museo Diocesano.  Pictures were forbidden inside the museum, of course – but I can report that it did have a spectacular quality and diversity of art:  paintings, vestments, and (my personal favorite) a ceiling with tremendously elaborate architectural detail.

One thing that I particularly enjoyed – probably much more than my car-mates – was that our route out of Cortona took us through the historic city center inside the walls.  So I was able to enjoy some real European car-commercial-type driving on 16° hills through narrow cobblestone streets.

Our established plan had been to meet back at the villa, and then go into Siena for a group supper. Unfortunately, we instead received first one text message:

Tim says to meet at siena campo for dinner if you guys would like to eat with us

And then, before there was time to respond, another:

Guys since jr did not get lunch he is hungry and we are going to in aheag and do dinner.

So we were unexpectedly on our own.  And mad.

Fortunately, we mounted an excellent recovery:  I had heard of a great restaurant in Siena:  the Antica Osteria da Divo, which has an international reputation for its food – elegant presentations of traditional Sienese dishes – and its environment – layers of grottoes underneath a historic structure.  Billy, Kyle, and I went there and partook in a spectacular tasting menu ("menù a degustazione") of seven-courses, not counting the aperitif and the amuses bouches – which, because it was so wonderful, we dubbed the "Meal of Revenge":
