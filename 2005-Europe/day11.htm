Italy     Siena
        Bagno Vignoni
        Ercolano (Herculaneum)
        Napoli (Naples)

For our last full day at the villa, Kyle, Billy, and I had ambitious plans:  Billy had hoped to work a trip to Ercolano (Herculaneum) into this trip since we first started talking about Italy, and by now he had managed to get me and Kyle on board.  Billy and I saw Pompei on our previous trip, but Ercolano was one of the things we just didn't have time to include; it has a reputation for being a richer experience than Pompei, with its opulent multi-story buildings, and we were both really glad to have a second chance at it.

We started the day with a side trip to Bagno Vignoni, a site of ancient thermal baths.  I regret that I didn't take the camera with me when I ran to the center of town to see the piscina – the flooded piazza where the bubbling hot water is collected – and that I didn't return there with Kyle and Billy after I found it.  But the short drive to Bagno Vignoni had taken much longer than expected and I was in a bit of a panic as to whether we would be able to see Ercolano.  We did, however, thoroughly explore the cliffside area where the water drained along steaming rivulets into private showers and baths.

And despite my fears, we did make it to Ercolano, which was spectacular, beautiful, and warm.  (After a frigid week in the villa, "warm" is really important).  So we took off our jackets, paid our admission, and walked among the ruins of this incredible town.  Below are the pictures, but what can I really show you that will help you understand what it is like to walk in Ercolano?  This is a place you have to touch.  This is a place where you can stumble over a poorly placed stone in a walkway, and know that 2,000 years ago people stumbled over that self-same rock.  It is a place where your feet touch mosaic floors that were set a millennium and a half before Columbus got his first boat.  It is the remains of a thriving, commercial city that had lain under ash for 900 years when the mound builders of North America started their legacy; for 1200 years when the Mayans started building Tulúm.  It is a place where you can walk along streets and look up at the volcano that savaged these people and their culture, and realize that the destruction occurred when Jesus was dead, but the gospels had not yet been written.

And then when you are stunned at the depth of this history, you realize the power of the destruction.  You see the boathouses, and realize that they are almost half a kilometer from what is now the shoreline.  You see the modern street and notice that it is several stories above the ancient street, built on ash.  And then you read of the Plinian eruption and learn that the descriptions of Pliny the Younger were so graphic and horrifying that it was thought these accounts could only be the result of exaggeration and poetic license – until, in the last century, it was revealed that these types of eruptions did not violate the laws of science (as had been supposed) and had, in fact, been what snuffed the life from Ercolano and Pompei.

I wish I could somehow share the bond that Ercolano makes with the people who pay homage to it – but I simply cannot.  You must go there to feel it.  Here though, as a shadow is to a butterfly, are the pictures:

We ended our day at Ercolano still with some daylight and some energy.  So we walked back to the car past a pastry shop where I picked up the best cannoli I have ever tasted.  And then we drove up the volcano responsible for the destruction we had seen.

 

The ruins of Ercolano in the foreground, the new city in the background, and Vesuvio (Vesuvius) rising up in the distance.  To the left of the volcano is  the Somma rim, which was formed by the collapse of a stratovolcano 17,000 years ago where Vesuvius now stands.

From the top of Vesuvius, we watched the sun set across the Bay of Naples.  And then, having exhausted our opportunities in Campagna, drove four hours into the cold night back to Siena
