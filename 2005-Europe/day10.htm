Italy     Siena
        Orvieto

Having come to the realization that our crowd of eight was traveling concurrently rather than together, Kyle, Billy, and I decided Thursday that we would break away entirely on our own:  With the hope of seeing attractions instead of text messages, and waiting in lines rather than waiting in parking lots, the three of us went down to Orvieto (in Umbria).  We stopped on the way for lunch at an autostrada rest area, where I had a surprisingly good dish of thinly sliced tuna with cream sauce and capers.

Orvieto turned out to be an incredibly rewarding excursion.  Not only did the town have beautiful and unusual sights, it also provided us with two bonuses:  First, even though the day was a bit drizzly, we spent most of our time underneath Orvieto's streets. Second, Unlike most hill towns, Orvieto has a set of elevators and escalators to the town center from the parking area, so we didn't start the day exhausted.

   

Orvieto parking ticket
   

We commenced our day with the Pozzo della Cava – one of hundreds of artificial caves in Orvieto, this one containing an ancient Etruscan well and several other interesting artifacts and rooms.  This was our first introduction to the elaborate system of caves underneath Orvieto and the incredible legacy of the Etruscan founders of this town.  The Etruscans were able to withstand two years under a Roman siege because they had fortified their city, collected food, and dug a series of tremendously deep wells to ensure an unlimited supply of water.  But they did eventually fall to Rome:  Though their water supply was perpetual, in time their food supply ran out.

From the Pozzo della Cava we made our way by foot across town toward the Pozzo San Patrizio, enjoying sights along the way.

   

A tower we passed in Orvieto
   

The Pozzo San Patrizio is an amazing, sixty-two meter deep well, constructed with a double-helix stairway around the central shaft.  When it was an active source of water, pack animals would be sent down one staircase, loaded with water at the bottom, and sent up the opposite stairs.  We paid our admission and followed in the path of the pack mules.

Then we took the Orvieto Underground tour – which was a guided tour of a cave network larger than the Pozzo della Cava, for which they generously found us an English speaking guide between scheduled tours because we would be leaving before the next scheduled opportunity. As I've mentioned before, I'm suspicious of attractions that are named in English when they are located in a non-English-speaking land – but this was definitely worth the price of admission.  In these caves we learned a lot about Orvieto's subterranean life, and about the ingenuity of its inhabitants.  First we were shown a map of the 700-or-so caves that have been discovered; we learned that cave-building in Orvieto was an ongoing activity from the time of the Etruscans until the middle of the twentieth century; and we were informed that the caves were fragile and would not have withstood the bombing in World War II, so Orvieto was declared a "no bomb" zone to protect its history.  And then we went in the caves and learned how, in the Middle Ages, the citizens of Orvieto figured out how they could withstand a longer siege by solving the problem of food:  They created shoebox-sized roosts in the cave walls to attract pigeons, which would fly out through windows, feed in the wild, and return to their roosts at night – such that the citizens of Orvieto were able to subsist off the pigeons and their eggs.

Finally, we toured the spectacular Duomo di Orvieto, which we entered as a service was being conducted in a side chapel.  The beautiful chanting from the service echoed throughout the enormous building and brought a good experience to the verge of perfection.  The building is an incredible monument, as prominent against the mountaintop town of Orvieto as that mountain is against the surrounding plain.  Really, it's completely out of scale for such a small town, and the explanation for its existence revolves around a Bohemian priest who stopped in nearby Bolsena on the way back from a pilgrimage to Rome.  This priest had doubted that the communion bread was really the body of Christ – but during a mass in Bolsena blood flowed from the consecrated bread, soaking the cloth underneath.  This cloth was brought to the pope, who was visiting Orvieto.  It took this pope and his successors sixty years to persuade the townspeople of Orvieto to sponsor the construction of a glorious duomo to commemorate the "miracolo del sangue" (miracle of the blood) – but when Orvieto finally undertook the project, it embarked on a journey that led to the preeminent example of Italian Gothic architecture.  And let me tell you, it is an utterly spectacular achievement.
