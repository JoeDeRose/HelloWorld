We started the day with breakfast at the hotel, then checked out, stowed our luggage, and joined the others at their hotel for some morning sightseeing in Florence.  The short walk from the Hotel Accademia to the Fenice Palace hotel took us past San Lorenzo and the Cappelle Medici (Medici Chapels).  These are a couple of sights that we did not give sufficient attention to on our previous trip, and – although we didn't go inside – I was glad to get the chance to walk past them and see more detail of the outside.

   

San Lorenzo and the Cappelle Medici
   

We wouldn't be able to check in to the villa until mid-afternoon, so we spent the morning doing some Florence sightseeing.  First, those who were in Florence for the first time split into one group to see David at the Accademia; Tim, J.R., Troy, Billy, and I went instead to the Museo di San Marco, a convent containing a lifetime of fresco work by Fra Angelico.  The highlight was the dormitory, where you can view the paintings in each monk's personal room, and see how Fra Angelico had shared his skill with the many brothers.  Unfortunately, pictures were forbidden inside.

After leaving Santa Croce, we walked past Dante's house and church, and then made our way to the Piazza della Repubblica.  Our intention had been to rub the nose of Il Porcinello, legend having it that those who rub the snout of this statue of a drooling boar will someday return to Florence.  It worked for us last time (as our presence here in Florence attested) – but we were misguided as to its location:  Il Porcinello is actually in the Mercato Nuovo a few blocks south.  J.R. had not shared the enthusiasm that the rest of us had for this task, and insisted at this point that we abandon the search and return to the Fenice Palace so they could check out.

I didn't put up much resistance on abandoning Il Porcinello because (1) I've really seen enough of Florence and don't need to come back and (2) it would give me a chance to climb the Campanile.  One of the nice things about revisiting a place is doing things that were regrettably missed on a previous trip:  In that vein I went to the top of the Campanile while the others went to check out of their hotel.  This endeavor finally gave me the unobstructed view of Brunelleschi's fantastic dome that I had long wanted, but had been unable to find on the previous trip.

We all drove south to Siena and checked into our villa, the spectacular Villa del Cielo. The villa is divided into two sections: the Cappella (Chapel) – which is a converted chapel with a living area in the nave and bedrooms in the transepts – and the Caminetto – a more traditional villa arranged around a central fireplace and an adjacent conservatory. The two sides are connected by a glass-lined walkway that looks out over the Tuscan hills.

Unfortunately, the villa was quite cold, especially the first night – a situation that impacted the joy of being in Tuscany for the entire week we spent there.  We were given the opportunity by the owner to have the heating turned off, turned on for a period of the day, or turned on all the time, with varying degrees of financial obligation.  A decision was made without my or Billy's involvement that the villa would be heated for six hours a day, half in the morning and half in the evening.  If you were to ask my opinion, this was inadequate – and I would have been happy to pay my share of a higher heating bill.  We had to wear winter clothes inside just to be comfortable while we were awake, but mattress warmers made sleeping enjoyable.  Aside from the temperature, however, the Villa del Cielo was wonderful; in warmer weather it would be perfect.

The villa is just up the hill from Siena – so, with much of the afternoon still available to us, we all went into Siena and checked out the Piazza del Campo.

Dinner was at a restaurant on the Campo, where I had a very nice gnocchi dish with a cream sauce and shards.  One of Mom's favorite meals on our previous trip was a similar gnocchi dish, and I had regretted getting some ordinary meal (pizza, I think) rather than enjoying gnocchi in Italy.  Thus, I was glad for this opportunity to make up that lapse, as it were.
