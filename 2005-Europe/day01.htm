I don't sleep well on planes.  With the combination of excitement, discomfort, and ambient activity, I just don't find it that easy to nod off, and estimate that I got only an hour's worth of rest on this nine-hour flight. Fortunately, the movie was Without a Paddle, which I enjoyed quite more than expected. My seat in the middle of the plane was miserable as anticipated, but I was next to a man from Geneva with whom I practiced my French.

As our plane landed, the sun was just coming up over the Alps. The amber glow behind the jagged peaks was a gorgeous welcome to Europe, but, unfortunately, a picture would have been impossible from seat 28D.

 
Notes on the Flag: Derived from a similar banner of the Holy Roman Empire.  The symbol of the International Red Cross, founded in Genève (Geneva), is the same flag with the colors reversed.  Indeed, a 1906 revision to the Geneva convention stated:  "To do homage to Switzerland, the heraldic arms of the Red Cross on a white field, which is formed by reversal of the Swiss Federal arms, shall be maintained as a distinctive emblem of the medical services of most armies."

Switzerland
Schweiz/Suisse/Svizzera/Svizra

So our plane landed with a precision of timing that would make any local watchmaker proud, and we set foot in a country which appeared on our tickets as Switzerland, but which is known officially as the Confoederatio Helvetica.  The question of "Why?" is a logical and frequent follow-up, and here is the answer:  Though the Swiss are famous for their ability to get along with each other – four languages and two major religions – it was not always so.  As recently as 1847, the Swiss were fighting the Sonderbund civil war of Catholics against Protestants, and upon the completion of this infighting drafted a new constitution to preserve the peace.  Loathe to piss off any of their constituencies by naming the country in a local language (Schweiz in German, Suisse in French, Svizzera in Italian, Svizra in Romansch), they instead selected the Latin name for their nation:  Confoederatio Helvetica.  It's an ironic twist, really, insofar as the Romans identified these people as adversaries.  The Swiss are fond of their country now, but time was that they burned their own villages as incentive and marched themselves in a conquering fit toward Gaul.  Only after the Romans crushed them at Bibacte did they retreat to the confines that we know today as Switzerland.

This term "Helvetica" comes up in many other places as well – most notably in the name of the Helvetica typeface created by Switzerland's Haas Foundry in Münchenstein in 1957.  I therefore here honor the Swiss with the use their typeface (or, actually, the shameless rip-off that Microsoft renamed "Arial") for the portions of this trip that were in the CH.

One of the things I most enjoy about visiting new countries is using new currencies.  Thus, the rise of the euro has been a bit of a disappointment for me (except the first time I saw it, of course).  Would that I had been able to use shillings in Austria and marks in Germany and lire in Italy and francs in France!  But Switzerland, at least, is not on the euro, so I did have the opportunity to enjoy its currency.  I'm almost embarrassed to report that I headed for an ATM as soon as we were past customs – not out of necessity, but out of excitement.  The coins are a tad plain, but the banknotes are among the most beautiful I have ever seen.  (Note that the coins all honor "Helvetia," a symbol of Switzerland, in image or by name or both.)

We enjoyed our introduction to Swiss efficiency in getting out of the airport and into the train station below it, and especially the fact that the luggage carts were designed to join us for the entire journey, even on the escalators and down to the train platform.  We bought tickets and took seats on the upper level of a double-decker train to downtown.  And then from the train station we took Tram 13 to from Bahnhofquai to Wipkingerplatz, so we could pick up the rental car on nearby Hönggerstrasse.

Ticket for the train to Zürich
  

I hate renting American-style cars in Europe – which usually means a Ford Focus – and hoped that this vehicle would be a European design. I was richly rewarded with a Citroën Picasso, the least American car I have ever driven (tying for the honor perhaps with the car I drove – on the left – in the United Kingdom and Ireland).

We headed east toward Liechtenstein and marveled at the mountains; Billy took dozens of pictures.

Driving in Switzerland provided an intriguing perspective on Swiss efficiency: The signs are very well organized and easy to follow. Repeater systems are in place on the most heavily traveled tunnels so radio signals are not interrupted. And the highways are laced at crucial points with checkerboard explosive patches so the road system can be obliterated in case of invasion.  I had heard about the explosive patches (which are presumably kept unloaded when Hannibal and the elephants are at a safe distance), but actually seeing them – feeling the rattle of the wheels passing over them – brings an entirely different level of awareness.

   

Hilltop church that we passed driving toward Liechtenstein
   
         

Liechtenstein

    Notes on the Flag:  This design was presented in 1937 with the following description:  "Blue is the color of a radiant sky, red the color of the embers in the fireplace during evening gatherings; gold of the crown shows that our people, our country and our princely House are united in heart and spirit."

After being so impressed with San Marino on our previous trip, I had high hopes for Liechtenstein – but I didn't swoon over it as I had hoped. It had a nice covered bridge to Switzerland, though.

Liechtenstein is an interesting place historically, though, if not scenically.  Sovereign since 1806, it was closely aligned with Austria through World War I – but the Austro-Hungarian Empire's poor performance in that conflict forced Liechtenstein to find a new master, and it turned like Janus to Switzerland.  Unfortunately, there's not much to say about Liechtenstein, except to hang out its dirty laundry.  But since that is so:  Liechtenstein's notoriously lax banking regulations attract "investors" for whom the Swiss numbered bank accounts provide too much transparency.  This has been a lucrative strategy for the world's sixth smallest country:  It has been said the attitude toward Switzerland of Liechtenstein – which uses the Swiss franc officially – is, "You have the currency; we have the wealth."  The rising tide has not necessarily lifted all boats, though:  About a quarter of the people living in Liechtenstein are not citizens of the country, and a substantial number of them are not citizens anywhere else, either:  Generation after generation of residents may be born in Liechtenstein, but without the Liechtensteiner bloodline, and therefore without citizenship.  In another area of disenfranshisement, it bears note that women in Liechtenstein got the vote in 1984.  Perhaps in this context you will find it interesting to know that the daily newspaper is the Liechtensteiner Vaterland.

"To thine own self be true," they say – and in this vein I must confess that I would never have bothered with Liechtenstein if it weren't an independent nation.  There's just something sexy about self-government, and especially so about the microstates of Europe.  I hasten to add, though, that Liechtenstein – currently 160 km2, or about 9/10 the size of Washington, DC – has aspirations of not being so small forever:  It disputes the ownership of a chunk of the Czech Republic ten times the size of Liechtenstein proper.  If this ... um ... disagreement were ever resolved in Liechtenstein's favor, the tiny nation would surge in size past Andorra to over half the area of Luxembourg!

Austria
Österreich

    Notes on the Flag:  One of the early rulers of Austria wore a white tunic in battle, and emerged from that battle entirely bloodstained except for the portion of his tunic covered by his belt.  This image was the source of the Austrian colors.

We continued north, back into Switzerland, then to Austria. You'd think from their jaunty logo that Austria's Gendarmerie wouldn't take themselves too seriously, but they apply themselves diligently and sternly to their job of protecting the Austrian border from the Swiss. The agent who met us scrutinized our faces as he held our passports, made me exit the car and open the trunk, appeared to deliberate a bit, and finally waved us through. It's easier to enter the U.S. from Canada. (Heck, it was easier to enter Switzerland from the U.S.)

We had lunch at a nice restaurant that we think was in Bregenz (but we were lost at the time, so who knows?).  The staff tried earnestly to help us overcome our language barrier; this was not entirely successful, but eventually money was exchanged for food and everyone had a pleasant expression.  What more can you hope for?  Billy had Wurstnudeln, an Austrian specialty of meat and buttery pasta, and I had Wienerschnitzel.

Germany
Deutschland

    Notes on the Flag:  This design has been used several times in German history, notably as the flag of the German Confederation, the Weimar Republic, West Germany, and the post-Soviet united Germany.  There is dispute over the origin of the colors, but the most common belief seems to be that they were taken from a German coat of arms under the Holy Roman Empire.

We entered Germany, and with this border crossing we had – in the last two hours – set foot in every country on earth that has German as its official language.  Here we drove for a little while on the Autobahn (failing to revel in the boundless possibility of a world without speed limits), and found a place to nap on the side of the road near Isny im Allgäu as fatigue had become an issue.

About an hour later, we continued to the town of Hohenschwangau to see about visiting Neuschwanstein. The ticket office had just closed, but we rode the last horse-and-buggy of the day up to look at the castle from the outside.  On the way up the hill, we passed Hohenschwangau Castle, where King Ludwig II spent much of his childhood.  Eventually, we arrived at the point, halfway up the mountain, where Neuschwanstein Castle rests.  King Ludwig II – often referred to as "Mad King Ludwig" – fancied the idea of an absolute monarchy under only the authority of God; as a constitutional monarch with limited power, he retreated into a fantasy world of which Neuschwanstein is the crowning (as it were) achievement.  (It must be noted whenever the subject of Neuschwanstein is raised that it is the castle upon which the Sleeping Beauty Castle at Disneyland was patterned.)

We drove south a few meters back into Austria and found a restaurant in the fading twilight. Here again the staff tried to help us communicate, and, amidst our broken English and broken German, even helped us find a hotel room – a wonderful and inexpensive hotel room at the Gästehaus Almrausch – in nearby Nasseireth. I had Münchner Weißwurst, which was very nice.

I have heard people say that they find people who speak English in the most remote corners of the world, and that communication is never a problem for them. They might want to have a go at western Austria: The people we encountered made an earnest effort to communicate with us – even trying to involve friends who they thought spoke English or apologizing that they had forgotten so much from long-ago lessons in the language – but, by-and-large, the world's lingua franca is simply not known in these parts.
