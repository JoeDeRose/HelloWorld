Italy     Siena

Sunday started with Billy and me making a reconnaissance trip to find a supermarket.  And then the plan was for both cars to make a return trip to do some shopping there before it closed at 1:30 PM. Tim had vague directions, but was following me to the supermarket to be certain he found it.  Somewhere between points A and B, however, he went in a different direction and left the shopping chore to me and Kyle with the text message:

We will meet you at the camp o text us when you get there.

Which led to what I can only assume was a communication breakdown.  Taken aback by the unforeseen division of labor, I wrote back,

Wait for us at a café. We don't want to miss sights as a result of being helpful.

To which I received the unexpected reply:

Go to hell

So we quickly grabbed a few supplies and then joined the others already assembled at the Piazza del Campo.  All that was said of the "Go to hell" message was Tim's claim to me that, "If you're going to be obnoxious, I'm going to be obnoxious."

I didn't quite understand, but I thought it best to let it go in the interest of minimizing conflict in our remaining six days together.  So we sat down for an amiable lunch at Spizzico, an Italian fast-food pizza chain.  Spizzico has the distinct virtue of selling American style pizza in Italy.  The Italians seem to like it, and so do I.  Generally in Italy, fast-food pizza involves a doughy imitation that was described vividly, accurately, and disgustingly by my friend Chris.  If you really must know what he said, I'll be glad to tell you – in person.

Piazza del Campo from the Torre del Mangia.
Here you can clearly see the nine patterns of brickwork that represent the Nove (or "Council of Nine") that once governed Siena.  This is the area that is cordoned off for the annual Palio, a rambunctious and dangerous horse race that circuits around these bricks.  The Fonte Gaia is at the top of the picture.
  

Fixture on post at the Piazza del Campo
(With a close-up of the bricks)

Our first task was to go inside the Palazzo Publico and get our bearings for climbing the Torre del Mangia and visiting the Museo Civico.

   

Courtyard of the Palazzo Publico
   

We found that the route up the tower was was a circuitous path up a set of steps so narrow that they had a traffic light to make sure no one had to pass; then a wide, modern staircase; then the ticket booth; then outside on the roof of the Palazzo Publico; then up the extremely long, narrow, steep stairs of the Torre del Mangia; then up a few more flights of stairs (ladders, really) to the very top.  The tower, by the way, takes its name from the appellation of its first bellringer, Giovanni di Duccio; apparently he was overweight and slovenly, and was therefore called "Mangiaguadagni" ("eat the profits").

When you climb one of these towers, you want to see interesting architecture on the climb, and a spectacular view at the top; the Torre del Mangia delivered in spades.

Then most of us went to the adjacent Museo Civico in the Palazzo Publico (photographs not permitted) and afterward took a moment to check out the Fonte Gaia (Gay Fountain), named for the joy the Sienese felt at taming the aqueducts to bring water to the center of their hilltop town.

For a final exploration, we went to the Museo di Santa Maria della Scala, which was a spectacular diversion, especially with its access to catacombs on the lowest level.  Unfortunately, this was one of those places where photographs were forbidden.

   

Ticket to the Museo di Santa Maria della Scala
   

Dinner was a repeat at the same restaurant as the night before, at which I finally got to try the pizza with a fried egg on it that I had envied Billy for ordering on our previous trip to Italy.  It's a great way to serve pizza – but practically unheard of in the United States.  The version I ordered also had bacon (chewy strips, not crunchy bits), which is also uncommon in the U.S.

Finally, on our way back down to the car, we stopped to explore a cistern that was fed (we must assume) by the subterranean system of aqueducts, or "bottini," that allowed Siena to rise to prominence despite its arid environment.  This network, stretching for 25 km and dating back to the thirteenth century, was so ingenious for its day that during a visit here Charles V declared the city underground to be more beautiful than the city "under the sun" above.  Apparently it is possible to tour this network, which I would have loved to do; unfortunately, I didn't know of the possibility until I returned home.  This is how things get on my "regrettably missed" list, and become fodder for future visits.
