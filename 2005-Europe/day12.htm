We departed early and drove Kyle up to Firenze, where he was to meet up with the others at the train station for their return via Roma (Rome).

Then Billy and I headed west toward France.  When we found ourselves near Pisa, we couldn't pass up the chance to climb the Leaning Tower again, as this had been such a spectacular and memorable part of our previous trip.  We arrived at 10:30 AM and were given a ticket for 11:00.  To pass the time, we also bought a combo ticket for the baptistry and the cemetery.  The baptistry failed to move me as it does some people – but the cemetery was definitely worth the time we gave it, and would have been worth even more.

Then – too soon for us really – 11:00 came and it was time to climb the tower.  It was as wonderful as we remembered, and I'm so glad they've reopened it for visits to the top.  By the time you're standing on that flat surface above the bells, you're accustomed to the lean and it doesn't feel as disturbing as it looks – but the stairs are an experience worth having as you list backward, then right, then forward, then left.  It's probably much like walking on the deck of one of the ships for which Pisa was once famous.

We stopped east of Genova (Genoa) and enjoyed an autostrada lunch of Parma ham and mozzarella di bufala.  It doesn't sound like much, but that simple dish of ham and mozzarella is the one that I have craved and missed more than any other meal since our return.

We got lost in Genova, which was a delight as the city is absolutely beautiful, with layers and layers of pastel buildings rising up every slope from the Ligurian Sea.  Though we didn't hear it spoken (to our knowledge), Genova apparently has a dialect distinctive enough that it is characterized as a separate language.  The Pater Noster in Genoese is:

Poæ nostro che sei nei çe seja santificao o vostro nomme o vostro regno vegna comme in çe cosi in tæra si faza a vostra voentæ. Deeme ancheu quotidiano o pane nostro, e perdonè a noiâtri i nostri debiti, comme noiâtri erdonemo i nostri debitoi no ci lasciè cadè ne tentazione ma liberateci da mä. Our Father which art in heaven, hallowed be thy name. Thy kingdom come. Thy will be done in earth as it is in heaven. Give us this day our daily bread. And forgive us our trespasses, as we forgive them that trespass against us. And lead us not into temptation. But deliver us from evil.
 

Seborga

After getting our bearings, we continued west and stopped in Seborga, which was an unexpectedly attractive climb from sea level. Seborga was founded as the first Cistercian state in history, and is currently engaged in seeking international recognition of its independence, which it bases on the a series of accidental omissions:

  1. Seborga was sold to Vittorio Amedeo II of Savoy, King of Sardinia – but this acquisition was never registered in Sardinia nor with the House of Savoy;
  2. In the 1748 Treaty of Aquisgrana, Seborga was not brought into the Republic of Genoa;
  3. Seborga was not cited as part of Sardinia in the Viennese Congress of 1815; and, finally,
  4. Seborga was not listed as a component of the Italian Act of Unification of 1861.

And according to Seborga, that settles it. Anyway, if it's a ploy to attract tourists, it worked on us.

   

Flags of Seborga
   

The official website of Seborga offers that "the Principality of Seborga is to the Principality of Monaco what Cancun is to the Belize," an assertion that I couldn't begin to dispute. The website also notes that Seborga is ruled by a "democratically elected Prince," whatever that may be. The town – er, nation – isn't much to look at, but we had a pleasant stroll up the main walkway and exchanged dollars for luigini, the local currency.  The luigino is pegged to the dollar – not the euro – at a rate of $6 to 1£, and I was surprised to find that it actually has a currency exchange code – SPL.  Luigini seem to be accepted not only in Seborga but in neighboring towns along the Riviera – but I just can't see it as a viable alternative: The smallest denomination is 10c£, equivalent to 60¢, which kinda makes it hard to buy a gumball.  Interestingly, the luigino that I bought for $6 would have cost me € 7, which made the dollar more valuable in this exchange at a time when it was actually worth about € 0.75; I wish I'd had the presence of mind to convert all of my dollars in my suitcase into luigini, and then into euro.

France

    Notes on the Flag:  Blue is the color of Saint Martin, a rich Gallo-Roman officer who ripped his blue coat with his sword to give one half of it to a poor beggar. White is the color of the Virgin Mary, to whom the Kingdom of France was consecrated by Louis XIII in the 17th century; it is also the color of Joan of Arc, under whose banner the English were finally driven out of the Kingdom. Red is the color of Saint Denis, the patron saint of Paris.

We entered France along the Riviera, but barely had time to appreciate being there before we changed countries again, this time into Monaco – the second smallest, and the most densely populated, nation on earth.

Monaco
 Monaco/Mùnegu

    Notes on the Flag:  Red and white are the heraldic colors of the Grimaldi family.

With Monaco, I hit a milestone in my efforts to see all the microstates of Europe:  Luxembourg (when I was 16), Vatican City (last year), San Marino (last year and this year), Liechtenstein (last week), and Monaco (today).  Only Andorra eludes me.  (Although, just to be thorough, I'd like to hit Malta and Gibraltar.)

Monaco is exactly what I want a microstate to be.  The surrounding French Riviera is, without doubt, a wonderful place – but Monaco is special in a way that is both different from, and an improvement on, the Riviera.  Even though we were there less than an hour, we could tell at an instant that Monaco was culturally, and even architecturally, distinct from its environs.  In short, when you go to Monaco, you know you're there.  I really wish we had given it more time, and it is definitely a place to which I want to go back.

By the way, I was standing in the same spot for both the picture of Monte Carlo and the picture of Monaco-Ville. The two "cities" are only about 800 m apart – closer than the White House is to the Washington Monument.  We would probably consider them the same municipality here, but Monaco is a different world.

The official language of Monaco is French, but the local indigenous language is Monegasque (or "le Monegù" as it calls itself).  Here – with a painfully poignant rhyme – is what it looks like in writing:

Sun Mùnegu suvr’ün schoeyu,
nun semenu, nun rachoeyu;
U vijin nun despoeyu,
E püra, vivè voeyu.

 I am Monaco on a rock,
I neither sow nor reap.
I do not covet my neighbor,
but yet I wish to live.

At a time, it seemed as if Monaco might not always live:  The understanding had long been that if the house of Grimaldi ever failed to produce an heir, Monaco would revert to France.  The two nations, however, signed a treaty in 2002 establishing that Monaco would remain an independent nation, without regard to a break the line of succession.  Monaco on a rock will live.

We returned to France and stopped for dinner in Nice, where I had foie gras at a seaside café, as the sun set over the Mediterranean just beyond the beaches of the Côte d'Azur.  Doesn't that just sound perfect?  (It was indeed spectacular – one of the most memorable meals of my life, I'm sure – but it was separated from perfect by about 20°F.)

   

War memorial in Nice
   

We made up for the almost perfect dinner by spending the night in an Hotel Mercure, an autoroute hotel just west of Aix-en-Provence that was so generic that it didn't even have a bidet. I would have preferred a typical European centre-ville hotel, but Aix looked kind of suburban from the autoroute and I didn't know how to find a place that would suit the demands of my imagination. Anyway, after the relentless cold of the (nonetheless beautiful) villa, American-style comfort in the form of a motorway hotel was not entirely unappreciated. The Hotel Mercure did provide some entertainment in the way it was accessed: We arrived in the westbound direction of travel, but the hotel was in the eastbound rest area; the challenge was addressed by having us walk across a bridge over the highway that housed a few restaurants and shops, registering at the hotel, returning across the bridge to the car, driving backward (!) at the rest area (on lanes that were marked for this purpose), buzzing in at a security gate, and following a circuitous 3 km private road through some pastures and across a bridge over the autoroute to the hotel.  By the time we got there we needed the rest.
