        San Gimignano

This entire day was given to San Gimignano.  Billy, Kyle, and an empty seat rode with me; J.R., Troy, John, and Rhonda packed in with Tim.

We arrived in San Gimignano at lunchtime and found a nice restaurant where I had pici (an unusual pasta of very thick hand-rolled spaghetti) al cinghiale (with wild boar).

After the fort, Kyle and I climbed the Torre Grossa while the others went shopping.  The Torre Grossa had a strange, modern, steel staircase and no sign of an original, stone staircase – but the views from its top were fantastic.  San Gimignano is famous as a city of towers, the result of a building frenzy in the Middle Ages wherein the status symbol of a noble family was to have the highest tower around.  There were at one time more than seventy of these erections, though now only about fourteen remain.  The Torre Grossa is the tallest of these towers, and it offered us two bonuses:  its adjacent art gallery, and the wonderful courtyard at its exit.  On our previous trip, I had noticed an attractive balcony and took a picture of it; on exiting the tower, I realized that this "balcony" was actually a bridge that led to the aforementioned courtyard – a courtyard that was lined with frescoes and a quaint well.

Surprisingly tired, we went grocery shopping at a nearby store and then returned to the villa where some of our housemates prepared a pasta dinner.

This evening provided an opportunity to familiarize myself with the art of using European washers and dryers, as well as to study the hieroglyphs in which their instructions are written.  Complicating matters was that the dryer was a convection dryer, which I had never heard of before – so the need to remove its water collector, and pour out the water therein, took me entirely by surprise.
