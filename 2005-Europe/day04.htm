And with the arrival of morning, things were definitely looking up: We awoke to a view out our window of the sun rising over the Matterhorn. Breakfast was delicious. And we learned that the paragliding service was operating and could take us immediately after we returned down the hill to Zermatt.

   

View from our hotel room of the sunrise over the Matterhorn
   

Since we had arrived after sunset, this morning was our first opportunity to get a look at our environment and this spectacular hotel.  Zermatt bills itself as a car-free environment, but Gornergrat is the real thing:  The only way to get there is by train or on skis.  There aren't even roads (unless you count the groomed ski slopes).  The Gornergrat just sits in the middle of a snow field, a short walk up the hill from its station.  The Hotel Kulm Gornergrat has the distinction of being the highest hotel in the Alps, at 3,089 m (10,135') above sea level.  A hotel was first built on this site, the Hotel Belvédère, by the Commune of Zermatt in 1896 – two years before the construction of the rack railway.  Exactly a hundred years later, the observation domes were added so that German and Italian astronomers could avail themselves of the crisp clear air. (Unfortunately, they are not open to hotel guests.)

We'd had the presence of mind to purchase a downhill ticket the night before, as our uphill trip had been covered by the one-day lift card.  This was a time-saver, as I had no idea how to purchase a ticket at the virtually unstaffed Gornergrat Station.  Billy and I boarded the train with our luggage and were glad finally to see the sights and vistas that has passed us by in darkness the night before (green line on the map).

Billy and I stowed our luggage at the main train station and met Daniel (our paragliding guide) at nearby Sunnegga Station, bought lift tickets to Rothorn, and rode the collection of vehicles (cable subway, cable car, and gondola) to the summit (yellow, orange, and red lines on the map). There we met up with Bruno and walked to the launch area where he and Daniel laid out the gliders and we all waited for a south wind. When the wind finally came we ran to the edge of the cliff for our flight to Zermatt 1,483 m (almost a mile) below. Bruno let me drive the paraglider briefly, showed me how the vehicle maintains its stability during a forced wing collapse, buzzed the ski slopes, and did some acrobatics and high-G turns (>4 G). Daniel flew Billy close to the rocky cliffs and did even more daring acrobatics that included going upside down.

Conveniently, we landed on the roof of the train station, where our luggage was waiting for us in lockers.  So, satisfied with our experience of Zermatt, we took the train to Täsch, retrieved the car, and started our drive south to Florence. In the interest of speed we ate at a McDonald's in Brig, where we found that Swiss McDonald's offer ChocoBalls (fried balls of surprisingly high quality chocolate).

We then made our way out of Switzerland across the Simplon Pass.  The road was well engineered and the transit was easy, which is a testament to the time, money, energy, and resources that went into constructing it, as this is not an easy set of mountains to get through.  Indeed, in an area where most major routes date back to the Romans, this passage was not even opened until Napoléon had a road constructed for ease of military movement at the beginning of the nineteenth century.  The mountains were spectacular, and we delayed ourselves a bit stopping to look at the most picturesque vistas and waterfalls.  If we had known this would be our last view of the Swiss snow-covered Alps (as our return to Switzerland would come after nightfall), I think we would have stopped even more.

Italy
Italia

    Notes on the Flag:  The origin of the colors is uncertain, but may have arisen from the civic militia of Milan.  The most common type of pizza in Italy – the Margherita – is designed to reflect the national colors, with basil, white cheese, and tomato sauce.

Our hope that been to meet our friends in time for supper in Florence.  We made good time approaching Genova, but after two horrendous fogs and an hour of being lost in Florence (which we could only resolve by paying a taxi to let us follow him), we had long missed the opportunity to join the others for dinner.  We finally made it to the Hotel Accademia at about 10:00 PM and checked in.

Finally at the hotel, we walked over to the Fenice Palace and joined Tim, J.R., Kyle, Troy, John, and Rhonda for a short while before returning to our room.
