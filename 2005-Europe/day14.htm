Notes on the Flag:  Despite various legends and back-formations, there is no official symbolism to the colors of the American flag.  The numbers are a different story, however.  As just about everyone reading this is already aware, there are thirteen stripes to represent the original thirteen colonies that formed the United States of America.  And the number of stars represents the number of current states, with stars being added as necessary.

United States of America


            

      

The seating was better on the flight back.  But the flight itself was a bit of an ordeal.  First, it was two hours longer than the eastbound flight – in part because of the extreme northerly route (across Norway, Scotland, Greenland, Québec, and Indiana) that our pilot had to fly.  And our entertainment options were limited by technological problems:  Catwoman (which actually seemed pretty interesting) was shredded when the flight attendants interrupted it to warn us about turbulence.  We got to watch Shark Tale through to the end, but our headphones stopped working about a half hour into Mr. 3000 (which didn't bother me so much).  The flight attendants made an earnest and thorough effort to solve the headphone problem or move us to different seats, but we didn't want to be separated into disparate seats on the plane, and there's only so much you can do when you're six-and-a-half miles above tundra.  It was all good anyway:  Billy had his laptop and I had my PDA book and Delta's Sky magazine.  I also had a window seat, and spent a couple of hours marveling at a landscape that I think was northern Québec, near Hudson Bay.

Finally back on terra firma, customs and immigration was an ordeal as well:  The lines were long and slow (especially the one we chose).  But the real wait was for our luggage:  Some problem in staffing or mechanics delayed the unloading of the plane, and we waited with the other passengers around a luggage carousel for well over an hour before the luggage started to appear.  Having arrived early at the airport, our suitcases were put in first, and therefore came out last.

Finally, though, fifteen hours after arriving at the Zürich airport, and after eleven hours in the air, we re-entered the U.S.A.  It was joyous for the freedom alone, but more so for what we had waiting.  I can find no better way to say it than to quote Lord Byron, whose elegy to his Newfoundland, "Boatswain," was reprinted in the copy of Sky magazine that I read so thoroughly on the eleven-hour flight.

Beauty without vanity, strength without insolence, courage without ferocity, and all the virtues of man without his vices.

Lord Byron would understand our joy.  Here's what welcomed us home:
