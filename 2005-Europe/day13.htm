Unfortunately, though, the Pont du Gard was the only significant thing we would see this day, as we faced a long, tedious trip back to Zürich. Our trip north through Provence, though, was pleasant if not exciting. Much of the drive was on a long low plain – but it was punctuated with ruins, towns, and rivers that gave it a strong sense of place.  Nonetheless, away from the autoroute there is so much to see in Provence; the misfortune that we were zooming past it at 130 km/h was not lost on us.

Continuing our trip back, we came to enjoy and appreciate fully the French highway system, which is expensive, but truly a delight to drive on:  First, its speed limit is 10 km/h more than most countries we visited. Second, the signage is spectacular, with symbols used as much as possible, attractive graphics highlighting a distinct attribute of each village through which the road passes, and explanatory messages about prominent and interesting features on the landscape. Third, the road design reflected cleverness, with dedicated truck lanes on steep hills and a striping pattern that is designed to help you gauge a safe following distance at the speed limit.  In less than a full day's driving, we paid € 49 in tolls, but it was probably worth it.  The radio, however, was less of a thrill:  In Switzerland and Italy, we had been able to find American music or enjoyable local pop, but in France neither was available.  I have heard that rumors of a French reverence for Jerry Lewis are inaccurate, but if the quality of French radio is a guide, such an obsession would not be entirely out of character.

Entering Switzerland on an autoroute near Genève (Geneva) we were actually waved through customs without interrogation – and even without being stopped.  Traffic was made to go slowly, however, so cars could be picked for more detailed review.  It seemed that the Swiss agents agreed with Austria's Gendarmerie:  We looked like we belonged in Switzerland.

Back in the CH, it felt like we were "almost there" – but our trip to Zürich was more challenging than expected:  The autoroute between Genève and Neuchâtel was not entirely finished, so for long periods we would be in long, slow lines of traffic moving between middling-sized towns.  And, unfortunately, we were entirely spoiled as to scenery:  At any other point in our lives, the surrounding mountains would have been stunning, and the Lac de Neuchâtel (the only major lake entirely inside Switzerland) would have been a beautiful attraction.  But we yearned for the snow-covered 4,000 m peaks we had seen in Zermatt, the glaciers, and the ice-laden streams.  There may have been such views as we got closer to Zürich, but the light of our last day in Europe was fading quickly, and had disappeared before any such vistas came into view.

Perhaps in an unconscious attempt to reintegrate ourselves to our own culture, we had eaten lunch at a McDonald's in France and now stopped for supper at the Cindy's Diner chain – a Swiss version of an American diner. The decor at Cindy's was highly authentic, but it was amusing to be surrounded by young, well-dressed people eating their burgers with forks and knives.  Jonesing for American – and by "American" I mean Mexican – food, I ordered a burger with guacamole.  The Swiss have not mastered guacamole.  I have only myself to blame.

We spent the night at the Mövenpick Hotel by the airport – a gorgeous hotel that seems to be the product of an enthusiastic – perhaps even obsessive – collaboration with Ikea. Our room had long banquettes of wood veneer, a quarter-circular seating area around a circular table by the window, angular floor-to-ceiling fixtures with built-in lights and speakers, and a bathroom that could double as the interior of a marble tomb.  From the front desk they had turned on the stereo and the lights, set the television controls to English, and placed an announcement on screen welcoming me by name.  Ah, the Swiss!

This, then, is how we left Switzerland:  Pleased and comforted at the shiny, attractive veneer, such that we didn't even wonder what might be underneath.  But as with us all, there is an "underneath."  On their face, they seem like a progressive people, perhaps because of all the Apple Macintosh architecture – but prick the surface and they bleed a red-state red:  Swiss women got the right to vote in 1971 in federal elections – 17 years after (for example) Pakistan.  (But with the canton of Appenzell Innerrhoden being the most notorious holdout, they didn't get universal suffrage from lake to shining lake until 1990.)  The Swiss are comfortable with firearms to an extent that is out of proportion (in my humble opinion) to their vulnerability:  As we traveled the country, for every single Swiss man (not woman) that we encountered – whether hotel clerk or waiter or gas station attendant or Daniel and Bruno the paragliding instructors – we could rest assured that somewhere he had a locked cabinet containing a fully automatic military assault rifle with seventy-two rounds of ammunition, as Swiss law mandates.  We could also rest assured as we looked up at the beautiful mountains that they were laced with concealed gun turrets from which the Swiss army could strafe the valleys and highways, and that every single Swiss man (not woman) knew exactly which one to report to in case of invasion.  In some circles Switzerland's claim to fame is not natural beauty, but a banking infrastructure that is world famous for obfuscatory protections that have not always been used for good.  And they're unfailingly neutral (except as protector of Liechtenstein and the Holy See), which may be a virtue most of the time, but is decidedly less so when six million Jews are being gassed next door.

Let me say this then:  I adore Switzerland.  And let me say this, also:  I am a progressive person who remains fond of his native region despite its sketchy, and often indefensible, history.  I suspect the Swiss know how I feel.
