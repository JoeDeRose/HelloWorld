I think I’ve got French capitalization in titles right, but I’ll welcome correction if it’s warranted. My understanding of the system is thus:

  • The first word is always capitalized – « Pâtisserie de la cité ».
  • Proper nouns are always capitalized – « Pâtisserie David ».
  • If the first word is not a noun, then the first noun and all preceding adjectives are capitalized – « La Grande et Vielle Pâtisserie de la cité ».
  • Equal pairs in a title, linked by a conjunction, are capitalized independently according to these rules – « Boulangerie de la cité et La Grande Pâtisserie ».

In the 1990s, Dean Kamen was working on a brilliant and noble project – a wheelchair with such a powerful combination of computers, sensors, and mechanics, that it would revolutionize the relationship between wheelchair users and the world to which they were all too often merely bystanders➴.

 
➴ Lest I be accused of overdramatization, note that even the language that describes their exclusion, excludes them.

This phenomenal new wheelchair would have the same four wheels that one finds on powered chairs already – but these would have a central chassis that would allow the wheels to rotate over each other, letting the user climb curbs, and even stairs. It would be rugged enough that the user could go “off-road,” joining friends on dirt trails or sand, and even go through as much as of standing water. And most amazingly, the wheels could align themselves vertically, thus raising the user to the point that she or he could have an eye-level conversation with someone, or reach a high shelf without assistance. Key to some of these innovations was a sophisticated gyroscopic feedback unit that enabled the wheelchair to balance, and even respond to the user’s subtle positional changes – whether they be deliberate or accidental.

The wheelchair was code-named “Fred.” And when Dean Kamen had the idea of marketing a version of this vehicle for use in a standing position, that companion project was code-named “Ginger” – an homage in naming to the unparalleled footwork of Fred Astaire and Ginger Rogers. “Fred” danced into the marketplace as the iBOT Mobility System. And a few years later, “Ginger” whirled in, as the Segway HT➴.

 
➴ “Human Transporter”; the current model is the Segway PT, with the abbreviation standing for “Personal Transporter.”

Our focus was then drawn to the itself. Originally called and then sporting a tall statue of that monarch in its center, it was renamed during the bloody Reign of Terror, when, in the center of the square, Louis XV’s statue was pulled down and a guillotine was put up – a device that was given such enthusiastic usage that for some time it averaged more than 40 beheadings a day. This is where countless French citizens passed into eternity – notable among them:

  • Louis XVI: Louis-Auguste de France was king, of course, when the square carried the name of his grandfather, but he was introduced to the screaming crowds as . Louis’s conviction of treason was unambiguous, with 693 votes (out of 721) confirming his guilt. But his sentence for death was surprisingly close: 288 votes against the death penalty; 72 in favor, but with delays and conditions; 361 for immediate execution. The majority, though slim, had spoken; so on , he was brought to the Place de la Révolution for the sentence to be carried out. And just as the National Convention was somewhat equivocal in its judgment, so was the guillotine itself: The blade did not cut through on the first try, and had to be raised for a second attempt before the crowd was allowed to rush forward and dip their handkerchiefs in the blood of Citoyen Louis Capet➴.
 
➴ Louis’s name, by the way, was a complex insult: He was an heir to the House of Bourbon, with the House of Capet being only distant ancestors. The use of this name was meant to suggest that the House of Bourbon had been merely pretenders to the French throne.
  • Marie Antoinette: was, like most women until very recent decades, given guilt without responsibility, blame without self-determination. A daughter in the Austrian royal family, Marie was married, in absentia, to Louis at the age of 14; she met him for the first time a month later, only to discover his deep prejudice against her country – a view that put her at a disadvantage with her family in Austria (as she was expected to influence French policy to their favor) as well as her new family in France. Never interested in politics, and shut out of them even if she had been, she was still the target of sniping in Versailles, which led to wild accusations that were also taken up by the revolutionaries as the monarchy unraveled: that she had affairs with people who were in fact enemies, and that she sexually abused her own son (a charge that she challenged with such ferocity that the mob was briefly won over to her side). She famously commented to her hairdresser as her enemies mounted in the Second and Third Estates, “Come, Léonard, dress my hair, I must go like an actress, exhibit myself to a public that may hiss at me.” But her biggest mistake was that she refused to escape: During the Revolution, she wouldn’t hear of any plan that would separate her from her husband. And after Louis XVI’s execution – when she was referred to simply as Prisoner No. 280 – she still refused to cooperate with any of several attempts to extract her to safety. She was taken to the Place de la Révolution on for her date with the guillotine. In mounting the platform, she accidentally stepped on the executioner’s foot, and even amidst these circumstances was at pains to let him know that it was not intentional; the last words of were, .
  • Antoine Lavoisier: Antoine-Laurent de Lavoisier, one of France’s most brilliant scientific minds, was probably less a victim of the Revolution than of his own personality – having angered Jean-Paul Marat➴ before the Revolution, and falling victim to his vengeance after. An appeal was made to allow him to continue his experiments, but was rejected summarily by the courts, who opined, “The Republic needs neither scientists nor chemists; the course of justice cannot be delayed.” The sentence was carried out on . A year and a half after his decapitation, Lavoisier was exonerated by the French government, and his personal effects were returned to his widow with a brief note acknowledging that he had been falsely convicted. Robespierre, below), “I killed one man to save 100,000.” She was executed in the Place de la Révolution on .
  • Maximilien Robespierre: Maximilien François Marie Isidore de Robespierre learned that those who live by the sword, die by the sword. And, like nearly 100% of the people who learn this lesson, he learned it too late to do him any good. Originally an opponent of the death penalty, he argued that, in one specific case, Louis XVI must die so that a hundred thousand virtuous citizens may live. Later, he became a prime architect of , solid in his belief that swiftness in judgment was far, far more important than certainty, and that terror was a form of virtue because without it virtue is impotent. Political winds changed, and Robespierre was declared an outlaw. While hiding from the National Convention, he shattered his jaw in a suicide attempt with a pistol, and then was held in the same cell that Marie Antoinette had been locked in while she awaited the identical fate. The next morning, on , he was taken to Place de la Révolution without a trial and presented to the executioner.

One person who didn’t die in the Place de la Révolution, however, was Joseph-Ignace Guillotin, inventor of the device. It is often said that his demise was effected by his most famous invention, and indeed he was imprisoned during the Reign of Terror, on a trajectory that would have led him, in a tumbrel, to the Place de la Révolution. But when Ropespiere fell from power, and found his own date on that square, Dr. Guillotin was freed. He invented the device, by the way, because of his steadfast conviction against any form of capital punishment: He believed that by creating a quick and painless mechanism for execution, that he could move his country in the direction of abolishing capital punishment entirely. His wishes came to fruition almost 200 years later, on October 9, 1981, when France became the last nation in Western Europe to abolish the death penalty. The guillotine was the mechanism of execution to the very end, having been used as recently as September 10, 1977 in the execution of Hamida Djandoubi for the torture-murder of his former girlfriend.

As I read back over my text above, I can hear John’s voice in my head: “You’ve painted a sympathetic picture of the monarchy, and a harsh picture of Revolution. Just what political perspective are you going for?”

That’s a good question, John. The French aristocracy were undoubtedly tyrants, although the same could be said for the early leaders of the Revolution. Indeed, Louis-Sébastien Mercier referred to Robespierre as a “Sanguinocrat” (from the French word for blood). But I will acknowledge – and defend – the position that the French Revolution, despite some early hiccups, was in general an enormous force for good, both for France and for the world.

But to answer John’s question (and don’t doubt me; it would have been John’s question): My primary perspective is not really political at all – just a conviction that every person’s life is a story that’s worth telling. But there is a political aspect as well in my distrust of capital punishment, as it cuts away the endings of those stories. I simply cannot bring myself to believe that any person is completely irredeemable. And even when we fail to bring the criminal to redemption, when we let her or him fade away in a prison hospital because trust could never be restored, we at least learn something of the process of redemption in the failure – we learn the result of our interactions; we learn what didn’t work; we advance our understanding in the context of the scientific method.

So I think Guillotin had it wrong: By making capital punishment quick and painless, we don’t move in the direction of abolition at all. Rather, in doing so, we make it all too easy to give up hope for a certain criminal’s redemption. And just as it’s far easier to buy ground beef in a supermarket package than it is to bleed the animal and scrape its bones, it’s also far easier to put someone to death when the execution isn’t punctuated by the criminal’s screams. But (in both grocery shopping and capital punishment) ease of our task shouldn’t be our foremost consideration. By cutting away the individual, we cut away a little of ourselves in the process. And by making it easy, we cut away a little more.

Nine hundred years ago, the Louvre was a fortress of . Four hundred years ago, it was the palace of the House of Bourbon. Now have you heard about the rule of thirds? In short, it holds that one should mentally divide an image into a uniform 3×3 tic-tac-toe grid, and should compose the image so that major elements fall along the dividing lines (and especially at the intersections). For portraits, it is generally held that the subject’s eyes should line up with the upper horizontal line, and that the horizon should also be on that line or on the lower one.

Leonardo da Vinci hadn’t heard of it (because it wasn’t invented yet). And that’s why he painted the Mona Lisa in a pyramidic compositional style that would have earned, at best, scrutiny in a twenty-first-century art class. But despite having violated one of the most basic precepts of composition, da Vinci managed to scratch out a picture that some think is the most beautiful in the world.

The Mona Lisa, it should be noted, has been through a lot, and has emerged in remarkably good shape considering its history. In 1911 it was stolen by an Italian nationalist and Louvre employee, Vincenzo Peruggia, who felt that it should be repatriated to Italy➴. Peruggia hid in a closet on a Sunday, and simply walked out with the painting under his coat (after extracting it from its frame) on the following Monday, when the museum was closed with only a small number of employees on the grounds – a strategy that was possible because of the painting’s relatively small size at , but not as easy as it might have been, since the Mona Lisa is painted on wood rather than canvas. It was only when he tried to sell it to the Uffizi that he was discovered and prosecuted. The painting’s return to France, however, was delayed while it was exhibited triumphantly throughout Italy – but in 1913 the Mona Lisa hung again in the Louvre. Peruggia served a short sentence (having been extended leniency in the Italian courts because of his nationalistic goals); eventually he married and returned to France where he operated a paint store (which sounds ironic, but probably isn’t). The painting’s worst year to date was 1956, when it was damaged first by a vandal who doused it with acid, then by another who chipped its paint with a rock. Now it sits behind bulletproof glass, which has protected it from more recent attacks: spray paint in 1974 when it was on loan to the Tokyo National Museum, and, exactly one month before our visit, an attack with a terra cotta teacup that cracked the case but left the painting undamaged (making you wonder exactly how “bulletproof” that glass really is).

I hope you’ll allow me to digress and rant for a bit (and frankly, you wouldn’t have made it this far if either of those were a problem): This is one of the reasons that the metric system bugs me. Now, don’t get me wrong: I try to use it in tandem with the U.S. system just to keep my mind nimble, and I can even understand the temperature in Celsius as well as Fahrenheit without a calculator. But at the end of the day, the metric system is built on a shaky foundation. And, while it is nonetheless brilliant in concept, people on the street don’t really comprehend its brilliance.

Let’s take those in reverse order: If you’re standing at the Eiffel Tower and you ask how far away the Gare du nord is, you’ll be told by the knowledgeable person that it is about 6 km away. But if you respond, “How far is that in hectometers? Or decameters?,” you’ll be met with a blank stare. The answers, of course, are 60 hm and 600 dam, respectively – but average people are just as challenged with the conversion from kilometers to centimeters as we are with a conversion from miles to inches. The fact that you can do it in your head if you know what the prefixes mean has absolutely no relevance when most of the people in the metric world don’t know what the prefixes mean.

And they also don’t know what the distances mean: It became a running joke when Mom, Billy, and I were in Italy, that every time we asked directions, the destination was invariably 50 meters away. Actual distances ranged from about ten meters to (more commonly) several hundred, but in much of the metric world (and Italy is not the only example, just the first that we encountered) “50 meters” means “a ways up.”

But the more controversial of my assertions is that the metric system is built on a shaky foundation. Maybe you’ll think that I can’t defend that point – but at least let me try by drawing your attention to that foundation. For every unit of measure, the base unit can be preceded by one from a set of prefixes that establish the multiplier of that unit:

For the first three levels adjacent to the base unit (shaded above), the multipliers advance by a factor of ten. But thereafter, they advance by a factor of 1,000. This helps streamline things when the base unit (a byte, for example) is woefully out of scope with the practical application of that unit (a 300 GB hard drive, for example). But a little more finesse near the base units would make sense, in my opinion. And metric time is a perfect example of why: Let’s assume that the base unit of time is the “day”: If so, then decimal minutes are “centidays.” But there is no single word that reflects the measurement indicated by a decimal second; rather, it would either be 1/10 of a “milliday” or 100 “microdays.” The inverse is true as well: If the base unit is the “decimal second,” then decimal minutes are “hectoseconds,” but hours are 10 “kiloseconds,” lacking a word of their own. The only way it would work for both organization and casual reference would be if the decimal minute were the base unit (second = “centiminutes”; hours = “hectominutes”) – but the framework of the metric system, that base units should have some natural practicality, is violated in such a schema; really the base unit should be the day, as that is based on observable phenomena directly related to the planet earth (although in modern science it’s actually the second).

Anyway, I think the metric system is pretty cool, and I think it’s better than our system. But, at the end of the day, it leaves room for improvement.
