Whenever I come home from a trip, there are several people among my friends with whom I’m eager to share the details. For me, the trip itself is just about one-third of the fun of traveling; the remaining two-thirds are in the excitement of planning, and in the joy of recounting the stories to family and friends.

But one friend in particular has been a most delightful person with whom to share mutual tales of conquest. Traveling together was out of the question – his style tended toward investigating the minutiae of museums both famous and obscure, while my style leans more toward The Amazing Race – but the conversations we shared about the places and the people and the politics were deeply cherished.

Unfortunately, they were also over: On Thursday evening, when I had planned to be packing for the trip, I was instead standing outside of John Speaks’s home with various neighbors and friends with whom he had missed appointments in the previous couple of days. When a police officer arrived to supervise our entry into his house (where his car was in the driveway and several lights were on), the front door key was handed to me. (I don’t know why I was given this solemn and terrible responsibility, but I’m honored that I was.) I opened the door to find John in his foyer where he had apparently died from a heart attack sometime in the previous two days.

John would not have wanted his death to ruin my trip, and I hope that he somehow is able to know that it didn’t. But the things I would have shared with him upon my return resonated all that much more strongly in my mind knowing that he wouldn’t be there to hear my stories, and to tell me his own, when I got back to Atlanta in September.

The presence of John was deeply felt on this trip, so much so that it is essential to the story that I embed it just as emphatically here. To this end, I will be marking issues that I suspect would be meaningful to John with boxes like this:

As one who hungers for new experiences, I was pleased to have a chance to experience one of the new millimeter wave scanners when I passed through airport security. They were not part of the standard protocol (being used, rather, for secondary screening when needed), but when I asked if the booth was one of the new devices, the TSA agent confirmed that it was and invited me to try it if I wanted. Some people are horrified at the idea of a TSA employee at a different location seeing her or his quasi-naked image flash quickly on screen among a series of hundreds or thousands of quasi-naked images. I find those concerns dangerously absurd, and eagerly accepted the invitation. My only disappointment is that the machines take so long: I had to step into the booth and stand with my feet apart and my arms down by my side (but not touching my sides), wait for a scan that took a couple of seconds, shift my orientation in the machine by a quarter turn, keep the same stance except this time raise my arms above my head, wait another couple of seconds for the scan, and then step out and wait about 10 seconds for the sequestered analyst to communicate by phone with the supervising TSA agent. As for results, though, it was good: Obviously, I wasn’t carrying anything inappropriate, but he knew that there was a dense item in my front left pocket (my passport) that merited further investigation. If they can get enough of these machines, or can improve the speed issues, I’d love to see them replace the ancient metal detectors.

Waiting for the plane was a bit Kafkaesque – or, at least, the parts of it inside my mind were a bit Kafkaesque. I was still shell-shocked from the sad and gruesome events of the night before. And I was also a bit self-conscious that I was heading to Amsterdam without telling my well-liked former neighbor, Aaron. This was an important point because he had family in Amsterdam and had long encouraged me to see the city, and to make arrangements to stay with his family while there. But our time was so short in Amsterdam that I hadn’t pursued this opportunity, a decision that I rationalized because I hadn’t seen Aaron very frequently since he had moved away after a divorce, as his schedule for visiting his children across the street from my house didn’t overlap very often with the times I was at home. Still, I felt a tinge of guilt that I hadn’t tried harder to include Aaron’s generous offer in our plans, and hoped that I would be able to dodge mention of it long enough after our return that he wouldn’t take offense. I think, in fact, that I was mulling this awkward anxiety at the exact moment I noticed that Aaron’s niece was one of our fellow passengers – which was also the exact moment that she noticed me. She was on her way to Addis Ababa, just transferring in Amsterdam, so with such a long agenda ahead of her, I hope that it won’t cross her mind to (accidentally, of course) rat us out. I would hate for Aaron to think that I didn’t appreciate his offer enormously, merely because I neglected to act on it.

As for the flight itself, I thought KLM was superb. Except for the seat size, coach on KLM was better than Business Class on Delta on our previous trip – especially with regard to the things that matter to me: food and entertainment. Indeed, the entertainment options were head and shoulders above what we had in BusinessElite in 2007: The seatback console provided games, language lessons, and a multitude of movies. I studied a little Dutch, thinking I wouldn’t need it. (Boy was I mistaken! This was an enormously productive use of my time.) And I watched Angels & Demons. I wanted to stay up all night watching movies, as the selection coincided quite strongly with my movie-watching agenda – but my common sense got the better of my excitement, so I took a reluctant pass on Frost/Nixon, Man on Wire, Star Trek XI, The Curious Case of Benjamin Button, and The Watchmen.

Also, I considered it auspicious that the plane was named after an important site at the central destination of this trip: We flew to Amsterdam on the Place de la Concorde - Paris.

John chided me for what he considered a habit of visiting a country just to “check it off my list.” His assessment was wrong (and I think he knew so), rooted in the fact that I traveled more quickly than he would, and that I considered myself to have visited a nation (although certainly not to have explored it) even if I had just transited through on the way to somewhere else.

He would not have made this accusation about the Netherlands, as I was there for three days (though this was probably a bare minimum in his eyes) – yet for the Netherlands, the accusation may have been true. It had been sitting there taunting me as a void on my map of Europe: As all the countries around it were filled in (even the microstates), the Netherlands just languished as a boring, untraveled, gray. This influenced my interest in going there at least as much as the opportunities that lay within.

One could go mad trying to look for a moral lesson in the history of the Netherlands; there is simply too much conflicting data. The best observation I can give you is that the Netherlands offers something for everyone, and has listed to the extremes from its distant past to the present day. Now the Netherlands is viewed as a tight-knit society intent on improving its quality of life while serving as an example for the world; in the not-so-distant past, it was simply a tight-knit society intent on improving its quality of life.

Now known as exhibiting extraordinary open-mindedness, the original exposure of the Dutch to other cultures came in the context of their nation’s expansion as a colonial power. And with this expansion came the usual set of moral compromises:

  • The Dutch may have developed a near-utopia back home, but they sowed the foundation for Apartheid with the engagement of the in .
  • Many persons of African descent in the U.S. and the Caribbean have ancestors who came to the New World in the cargo holds of Dutch slave ships.
  • In the weeks before Christmas, the Dutch roll out not only the cross-cultural seasonal icon, (their version, BTW, spends the off-season in Spain rather than the North Pole), but also their own specific overlay to the holiday pantheon in the form of Sinterklaas’s lackey, , whom Dutch teenagers emulate by costuming themselves in blackface, afro wigs, and garish red lipstick. You can see an example of Zwarte Piet in the image to the right.
  • Only 1% of English words are of Dutch origin, but the worst racial epithet of our language is among them.

The Netherlands is highly respected by politicians on the left as an example of what can be achieved by socialist ideals, and as an emblem of the most positive, most nurturing connotations of the term “welfare state.” Yet, in reality, the Netherlands ranks #13 (77%) on the so-called “Index of Economic Freedom” (in which the most capitalist economies are at the top), just 3.7 percentage points behind the U.S. (#6 at 80.7%), and well above reputedly ultra-capitalist Japan (#19 at 72.8%). And let us not forget that Holland was the site of , in which the Dutch became so consumed by greed, speculation, and the “greater fool” theory that a single tulip bulb sold in 1636 for 2,500ƒ➴.

I’ll concede that much of my criticism of the Netherlands is nitpicking, being either ancient history, or not that important. But one legitimate gripe about the country is in its environmental commitment: The Netherlands was an early signatory of the Kyoto Protocol on greenhouse gas emissions, yet environmental consequence at (and below) sea level hasn’t been treated with the same priority for this, the lowest of the Low Countries: Indeed, while we in Georgia (hardly a bulwark of environmental responsibility) agonize over whether water releases from Lake Lanier are adequate to preserve the mussel populations in the Apalachicola, the Netherlands has been terraforming its north for so long that a specific geographic coordinate cannot be described as land, lake, or sea without bringing in the variable of time: What was known as in Roman times later became the much larger, salt-water , and then, through a massive series of dams, was turned into the fresh-water . Amsterdam is a major center for oceangoing ships, yet its port is fresh water and unaffected by tides. The Ĳ, Amsterdam’s waterfront, is described in Wikipedia as “a river, formerly a bay.” And the vast majority of Dutch windmills are not for electric power generation, but simply for moving the water back up the hill to the sea.

There is a story of uncertain veracity about : that he had his throne set on a beach, and sitting there he commanded the tides to halt. When the waters impassively rose up to cover his feet and wet his robes, he spoke with a rehearsed humility to his subjects: “Let all men know how empty and worthless is the power of kings, for there is none worthy of the name, but He whom heaven, earth, and sea obey by eternal laws.” Yet an entirely different scene has played out in Holland: The people who couldn’t hold back the Nazis for more than five days have been holding back the ocean for nearly a millennium. The English long ago made an observation that has stuck through the ages: God made the world, but the Dutch made Holland.

Yet the Dutch are nothing if not self-aware about their physical circumstances. One of the best quotes from a regent who was a font of wonderful quotes, came when Queen Wilhelmina visited Germany’s Kaiser Wilhelm II prior to the hostilities of the First World War➴. Kaiser Wilhelm tried to intimidate the queen, saying “My guards are seven feet tall and yours are only shoulder-high to them.” Queen Wilhelmina replied, “Quite true, Your Majesty, your guards are seven feet tall. But when we open our dikes, the water is ten feet deep!”

 
➴ Queen Wilhelmina remained in power also during World War II, a conflict in which she was unable to sidestep German aggression as she had in World War I. Taking refuge in London at the invitation of Winston Churchill, she took an aggressive lead in communicating with her people by radio; supporting the resistance; and even sacking the prime minister, Dirk Jan de Geer, for being open to negotiation with the Germans. Because of her strength of will and character, Churchill described her in 1946 as “the only man in the Dutch government.”

So please understand that it is not my intention to disparage the Dutch, but rather simply to offer a “reality check” on our external perceptions of this country. I could never begin to dispute the underlying assumption: By and large, the Dutch people, and their governmental policies, are of a stock that the rest of the world would do well to emulate. Indeed, I think the Dutch might collectively set an example for the United States as to how a nation that functions as a colonialist superpower, and serves its own aggrandizement, can later learn from its mistakes, restrain its reach, tighten its focus, and come to serve the world as an example of peace rather than as an instrument of war. But let us, and them, never forget how much blood, and how many tears, they went through to get where they are.

Our plane was scheduled to arrive at 7:25 AM at (the word “Amsterdam“ is omitted in the Dutch version of the name➴; I have no idea why). In fact, it landed nearly an hour earlier after catching a tail wind; this fact seemed to delight our pilot, but resulted in a bit of frustration for us. We were already a bit uncertain what to do with ourselves showing up in Amsterdam at the crack of dawn (several hours before check-in opened at our hotel). An extra hour meant we had more time to wander the streets like zombies, and less to nap on the plane.

 
➴ And, by the way, why doesn’t the official airport logo carry the Dutch version of the name? I’ll go on a rant farther down about the lack of written material in English, but my wish is simply for some convenient translations, not for the Dutch to forsake their own language.

Schiphol is supposed to be one of the finest airports in the world. I was excited to see what that was like after spending the last vacation at the other end of the bell curve in Larnaca, Amman, Cairo, and Luxor. Schiphol was nice (much nicer than those four, certainly) – but I’ve still never seen an airport that caused me to think, “I want to make a special effort to come/transit/avoid here just because of this facility.” One of the things that’s supposed to put Schiphol above its peers is that it has shower facilities available for travelers, but we put a lot of effort into into finding them and came up empty. The people at tourist information told us they had never heard of the showers and had never received that question before (and regarded us with such a mixture of amusement and skepticism that the honesty of their response was not in doubt).

Finally, we gave up and decided to go into town, which gave us more opportunity (indeed, more opportunity than we wanted) to explore the offerings of the airport: First the ING ATMs wouldn’t accept my card (who knows why? I worried that it was the beginning of a long string of money woes, but it wasn’t). Then the ticket machines for the train to insisted on my entering my credit card PIN. I had actually documented my PIN, but saved it in a protected Access database, which required finding a place to look up the file on Billy’s laptop, which required finding a power outlet after his use of the machine on the long flight, which proved difficult (we had the European plugs, but found ourselves wanting for a socket). Finally, I remembered a second password file in my PDA, found the PIN, got the tickets, and got us on the train. Maybe it’s just ’cause we were so groggy, but it all seemed more difficult than it should have been.

The name of the airport, by the way, means “Ship Hole” in English, which sounds inauspicious, but is actually rooted in history: Like much of the Netherlands, the point where Schiphol sits used to be open water until it was drained in 1852 to form the Haarlemmermeer polder. The lake at that site was prone to violent weather that made it perilous for ships, giving it a name that has survived over the decades from sea to land to air. The airport is entirely below sea level, with some of the runways being as much as below the , which is the slightly fuzzy approximation of sea level used in official documentation for the Netherlands.

We made our way into town by train, helped by a hip American ex-pat who didn’t pay the fare (because “they never check on this line”) and who told us that Amsterdam was more conservative than people think (and getting even more so), that the pot is better in California, and that it wasn’t really a party town because the bars and transit close early. She also confirmed what we had heard elsewhere: Despite a “touristy” reputation, the canal cruises are pretty interesting.

Hotel check-in didn’t open until 11:00 AM, so we tried to waste time walking around Amsterdam, and found ourselves instantly, by chance, in the Red Light District. I’ve got a section on it below from my return after nightfall, but suffice it to say now that sunlight did the place no favors: If you don’t find a prostitute enticing at dusk on a crowded street that smells like beer, you won’t find her enticing in the glare of the morning on an empty street that smells like vomit.

Billy and I just walked around Amsterdam getting our bearings. First we discovered a nice Jewish memorial along the Amstel River. Then, to avoid a rain shower, we ducked under the , a beautiful tower with a weird history of possibly-unnecessary rebuilding: The original was built around 1487 and survived a fire in 1618 without major damage. Still, it was renovated in 1620, and then torn down in 1885 to make way for the current version. An underpass was added in 1939, and it was there that we hid from the rain. The tower’s name comes from the fact that it was used to mint coins during the , in which both France and England declared war on the . It rises above the , where the Amstel River ends➴.

 
➴ What, you’ve never heard of a river just “ending,” in a crowded (non-aqueous) public square? This is not even the most cunning of the tricks the Dutch have played on their watery environs. The Amstel used to flow through town, past the dam at , from which Amsterdam (formerly “Amsteldam,” and before that “Amstelredam”) took its name.

Now, however, the last leg of the Amstel has been filled in and is a major street known as the Rokin, taken from the Dutch word, . The water doesn’t stop, of course, just because the river does; it gets past downtown via the and through a set of pipes under the Rokin.

The Amstel, by the way, is only long, which may partially account for another quirk of its geography: Even in such a waterlogged environment, the Amstel has only one island, tiny in the town of Amstelveen.

One of the most interesting sites that Billy and I passed was the Bloemenmarkt, a floating flower market along the Singel, Amsterdam’s oldest canal. I really wish I’d spend more time in the Bloemenmarkt when I was there, but I was exhausted from the flight, didn’t realize it floated, didn’t recognize it as one of the few places that might have sold wooden shoes, and wasn’t aware of how unique it was in the street life of the city. I’m lucky that I saw it at all, but disappointed that I didn’t see it better.

And one joy of Amsterdam was simply the architecture. It’s not the brutish look of the British Isles, which can leave one with the impression that they threw up their buildings with leftover road- and rail-building supplies. And it’s not the garish rococo of France, which often looks like angels barfed on the side of the building. Instead, it’s just perfect: Every building is ornamented in exactly the right proportion. Large undecorated walls serve as a counterpoint to detailed crenellations at their peaks. The mandatory pulley rings that jut from every attic (as required by law) give Amsterdam an iconic and unique sense of place. The distances between the canals and the canal houses is a celebration of both form and function. Windows have such an ideal relationship to the size of the building that one’s heart is immediately filled with hope. And, indeed, hope is called for, as Amsterdam gives the appearance of being in the beginning stages of a domino-like collapse, with houses leaning, often at precipitous angles, into their neighbors.

Filled with such hope, we considered McDonalds for lunch; Billy and I had great experiences with McDonald’s restaurants in Italy and Switzerland, where they have unique architecture and unusual additions to the menu. Indeed, while it wasn’t fine food by any stretch of the imagination, McDonald’s in Italy was one of the few opportunities to eat something that Italians enjoyed, and that was completely and absolutely unavailable in the U.S. But efforts to duplicate this discovery in other areas have been unsuccessful to say the least: McDonald’s in Mexico, France, and Greece were the same lard-and-salt factories that we knew from home. And this McDonald’s appeared to trend toward the Mexico/France/Greece model. Also, the service was – to be generous – unexceptional. So we left and ducked into a sandwich shop where we had rosti (toasted sandwiches).

And speaking of Amsterdam, here’s a way to start arguments if you’re of that particular bent: Find a group people with a middling understanding of European geography, and ask them whether Amsterdam is the Dutch capital. The situation is so murky that Wikipedia actually carries an article titled “Capital of the Netherlands” to thoroughly flesh out the subject. The answer to the question is that Amsterdam is the capital of the Netherlands, based on a single reference in the constitution that the monarch should be coronated (but not live) in . But since 1588, the country’s seat of government has been in , with the Dutch parliament, judicial system, royal offices, and all foreign embassies being located there.

While Amsterdam’s role as capital of the Netherlands is confusing, it’s role as capital of the is beyond dispute: It isn’t. That honor goes to Haarlem, just up the road.

And here’s another tidbit about Amsterdam, to balance the snarking I did in the previous section. The city’s motto is . Americans reading this (and that’s pretty much all of you) will initially take this as bullshit civic boosterism, but that’s not how it goes down in the Netherlands. The motto was bestowed on the city by Queen Wilhelmina in 1947 to acknowledge the actions of the citizens of Amsterdam during World War II, and especially a single event that overwhelmed her with emotion when she learned of it during her self-imposed exile in London: In February 1941, the citizens of Amsterdam went on strike to protest Nazi persecution of the Jews – the first time that a gentile population rose up against the anti-Semitism of the Third Reich.

One amazing aspect of Amsterdam is the bicycles, the sheer quantity of which is amazing (reputedly almost one per citizen). But as someone who uses a bicycle as his primary mode of transportation in a much less bike-friendly city, I’ve got to say that I wouldn’t want to do it as the Dutch do:

  • Most significantly, their bikes are behemoths – old, heavy, one-speed clunkers with handlebars that come up nearly to shoulder height and seem designed to train the rider in good posture as a side benefit to the commute. They’re such tanks that people struggle to pedal them up even the small inclines flanking the numerous canal bridges. Also, they’re almost universally in poor maintenance, with half-flat (or completely flat) tires that make the cobblestones of the commute all that much more unbearable. And strikingly for a culture that is generally so sensible, no one wears a helmet.
  • Theft is widespread, affecting about one of every eight bikes each year. It’s facilitated by a lack of serial numbers on the bikes and a bureaucratic system of bike repatriation that requires the victim to get to a rural depository (not on public transit), describe the bike (which is probably pretty easy as 95% of them look about the same), and then search for the individual bike in a yard of tens of thousands of recovered bicycles.
  • There’s no place to park. Even with a multi-story parking deck for bicycles just outside the main train station, and bicycle parking barges scattered around the city, most every railing throughout Amsterdam to which a bike could be chained has, indeed, a bike chained to it. It is not uncommon to see bikes dangling over the canals by their security chains, having been heaved over the railing by someone who needed the space.
  • Most visitors to Amsterdam comment that “every street has a bike lane.” It does give that appearance, but the truth of the matter is that a lot of streets don’t have a bike lane, and that bikes are not allowed on streets (except very small streets) that lack a bike lane. Even where the lanes exist, they run a circuitous path around the canals, crossing streets at odd angles and merging into pedestrian zones. And the shared environment has its challenges: In theory, cars, bicycles, and pedestrians have their separate spaces and crossing signals. In practice, scooters are consigned to bicycle lanes (and forbidden, like bikes, from the main roadways), so there is a constant speed differential to navigate in the bike environment. The pedestrian areas are often so narrow that pedestrians are forced into the bike lanes. And crossing major intersections can be time-consuming as every mode of transportation is separately given its turn. In some ways it works undeniably, though: Amsterdam takes it as a point of pride that more people die through murder than through traffic fatalities (26 as compared to 18 in 2007).
  • Unlike most transit systems in the U.S., they have not implemented a strategy for allowing bikes on buses and trams.

If I lived in Amsterdam, I would do things completely differently: I would get a folding bike so I could take it on buses and trams and so I could avoid parking and theft problems by taking it inside buildings. I would have a bike with multiple gears and a broad gear ratio so I could approximate the speed of the scooters and get over the bridges easily. I would keep the tires inflated (as I do at home). I would always, always, always wear a helmet (as I do at home).

And if I had the power to affect public policy in Amsterdam, I would start from scratch: I’d keep the bike lanes, but require bikes to merge into traffic lanes for left turns➴ (which would speed up the intersections, a benefit to traffic and to pedestrians). I would invest in a program like Vélib’ in Paris, where people can establish memberships in the program, rent bikes when they need them, return them to any location, and be assured of an available bike and an available spot to drop it off. I would start getting rid of the clunker bikes by establishing a disincentive to owning one through parking restrictions and by letting anyone who turned one in have a free membership for five years in the shared bike program. I’d put bike racks on the buses and trams. And I’d pass a helmet law.

 
➴ But incremental improvement isn’t the plan. Instead the Dutch are experimenting with (singular is ), in which cars are not banned, but instead move at a crawl amidst whatever non-automative activity may be found – pedestrians, bicycles, planters, benches, children’s playgrounds, and the like.

But such is the tragedy of innovation: The place that pioneers something doesn’t have the opportunity to learn from the mistakes of others.

I’ve used this line before and I’ll almost certainly need to use it again: Traveling teaches you that everything everyone says is wrong. Our first hour in Amsterdam brought us face-to-face with two challenges to our assumptions.

First, I had heard friends wax on and on about the drainage in Amsterdam. The promise – described in the telling as literal, not figurative – was that the Dutch were such masters of drainage that every square centimeter of Amsterdam was inclined just perfectly to channel the rainwater through rivulets between the cobblestones, and back into the canals, such that standing puddles were unheard of. Now I’m not going to say that the Dutch don’t have a way with water management, but this seemed from the start to be too good to be true. And it was. We arrived after a shower and let me tell you: puddles abounded.

But the second false promise was even more of a challenge. I had been assured that was a phrase I didn’t even need to know – that the Dutch (all of whom, like the children of Lake Wobegon, are above average) spoke English fluently and unfailingly, and might even take offense if questioned on this point. One website cautioned that the Netherlands is the only place in the world where it might be considered rude to ask, “Do you speak English?,” as this could imply that their education was lacking. It’s complete BS: A lot of people speak English there, especially in the tourist trade – but a lot of them don’t. And it seems to catch them off-guard if you just walk up and start speaking English.

I will concede that more people spoke English in Amsterdam than in any other non-English-speaking place I’ve ever visited. But I did find it striking that very little was written in English. Our train tickets to Paris were indecipherable to us, being entirely in Dutch; restaurant menus were often only in Dutch; public announcements might be bilingual, but written warnings were often just in Dutch; even the emergency diagram at our hotel was exclusively in Dutch. Finally, seeing it in place gave me a sense of the “skankiness” of the transaction: I had imagined the windowfronts as sales and negotiation zones, with private apartments upstairs where the deed would be done – but these street level rooms are the entire place of business: Behind each of the windows is a curtain (red, usually), a bed, and a sink. Little could Musical Youth imagine what the Dutch government might later do to undermine their efforts at purity: Fourteen years after their song was climbing the charts, the Netherlands established its , which revised the guidelines related to its Opium Act: Going forward, the arrest and prosecution of persons possessing small quantities of drugs for personal use was not a priority. And with that a flag was planted on the slippery slope.

It is still illegal, though not enforced, to consume marijuana in the Netherlands. (The Dutch government explains this inconsistency by stating that it is a party to several European treaties that forbid it to legalize marijuana, so instead the country has established a policy that the laws will not be enforced in limited situations [i.e., personal consumption in a reasonably private environment].) It is still illegal and enforced that you cannot grow marijuana in the Netherlands, cannot import it, and cannot trade it in more than personal-consumption amounts. Yet somehow, the “Coffeeshops” (when the name appears in English, it is a sign that marijuana is sold there) are full of it. (The Dutch government does not explain this inconsistency, but the obvious answer is that you can’t have one without the other.)

The situation now is such that phrase “Dutch system” is how diplomats describe a policy of turning a blind eye to casual drug use. And “Dutchie” has entered the English language as a type of marijuana cigar. It is not an exaggeration to say that nearly every single person among my friends, family, and co-workers, upon hearing that I had planned a trip to Amsterdam, asked as the first or second question, “Are you going to smoke pot?” And in a series of engagements that challenged my assumptions about age and religious conviction, many of them had stories, first- or second-hand, of marijuana consumption in Holland.

So here’s the answer: I went there thinking I might try it, as I don’t have a strong moral objection to marijuana use – but the Coffeeshops were so seedy and unappealing that I immediately changed my mind. I never even walked inside of one. Yet I was not unaffected: Exactly opposite the (reported) experience of Bill Clinton, I didn’t smoke, but I did inhale. The streets of Amsterdam are littered with Coffeeshops and the pot smoke billows constantly out of the doors and open windows of these establishments. And the effect was cumulative: By the third night, I found myself tossing and turning in bed, imagining every scenario of disaster for the remainder of our vacation; it was only when I realized that I was experiencing the paranoia of cannabis exposure that I finally relaxed enough to fall asleep.

Frankly, by the end of our stay there, I got quite exasperated with the figurative and literal pot cloud hanging over Amsterdam. It’s hard to find a T-shirt that doesn’t have some drug reference on it. And I searched high and low, without success, for a souvenir pair of wooden shoes. But if I’d wanted a bong – even a bong shaped like wooden shoes – thousands of choices lay before me (and that is not hyperbole). And again, in ways that challenged my assumptions about age and religious conviction, I noticed the staff in these bong emporia: senior citizens, and women in headscarves, not being uncommon among them.
