It is actually for this reason that a tiny chunk of what is now Belgium, , almost became – possibly to be renamed as – the world’s first jurisdiction in which Esperanto was the official language. Neutral Moresnet was a tiny mining community, just about twice the size of Monaco at , the existence of which reflected a hodge-podge of compromises that apparently caused the local populace no end of frustration: It was jointly administered by Belgium and (and not by the of which Prussia was at that time a component); it used the French Franc as its currency even though it did not border with, or have any ties to, France; and it was largely left to its own devices, except when Belgium and Prussia (usually Belgium) thought it was getting out of line, such as when it began to produce its own stamps, set up a casino, and welcome people fleeing military service obligations. The foray into Esperanto was the brainchild of a local booster, Dr. Wilhelm Molly, and actually gained some traction in the early 20th century, with Neutral Moresnet being declared by the in 1908 as the capital of the Esperanto community. Unfortunately, the German tide that washed over the continent in World War I scuttled the ambitions of Neutral Moresnet and Esperantists, with the territory being absorbed into Germany when they were winning, and into Belgium after they had lost. Neutral Moresnet is also of interest to cartographers, being one of the components of the only international quadripoint ever to exist, at the point where the Netherlands, Belgium, Prussia, and Neutral Moresnet came together (the same point where the Netherlands, Belgium, and Germany now come together).

I know I’ve given a different, less elaborate, and somewhat contradictory description of the French flag in a previous trip report. All I can tell you is that the following is what I have now: The definitive heritage of the tricolor is unknown, but the colors have been associated with France since the Middle Ages. White is often associated with the monarchy, and some interpret the flag as an overlay of the monarchy’s white on the blue and red, a long-time symbol of Paris. Others take blue to represent the bourgeoisie, white to represent the clergy, and red to represent the nobility (with blue coming first, in keeping with the ideals of the French Revolution). Yet others say that blue comes from the House of Valois, white from Joan of Arc, and red from either St. Martin or St. Denis (or both). Whatever its history, the flag is an inviolable symbol of France: When a royalist majority was voted in at the beginning of the Third Republic, the parliament made preparations to re-establish the monarchy, and even went so far as to offer the throne to Henri, comte de Chambord. He accepted on the condition that the tricolor be replaced by a white flag. The demand was refused, and neither Henri, nor anyone else, was ever again named King of France.        Apparently the thing you see above is not a coat of arms – at least not according to the anal-retentives who decide these things – but, rather, a “national emblem.” The difference, apparently, is whether the governmental force that it represents is democratically elected (national emblem) or hereditary (coat of arms). First, in the dates at the top of each page, I show not only the standard Gregorian date, but also the Republican Date, as in the . This system of timekeeping arose in the same context in which the French adopted➴ the .

 
➴ It is widely believed that the French invented the metric system after their revolution, to remove anthropic measurements based on the size of the monarch’s foot, thumb, etc. This is actually not true, as the metric system was conceived over a century prior to the French Revolution as an aid to international commerce (the “Euro” of its day, as it were), and the path to its eventual adoption in France was, in fact, rooted in the work of Louis XVI, the very king that the Revolution so famously beheaded in 1793. But even though hatred of monarchy didn’t play too much a role in establishing the metric system as the new standard, it did affect the measurements themselves: Originally, “gram” was just a shorthand term for a thousandth of a “grave,” the latter being the base unit of measurement of mass. But was also the word for the title of nobility that translates to English as . Because any reminder of the nobility was out of favor in revolutionary France, the base term for units of mass became the gram, and the mass represented by the term “grave” would forever be just a thousand grams, or a “kilogram.” But even though this change affected the base term, it did not affect the base unit. And that is why, at the , there exists, entombed under two nested bell jars, not the “international prototype of the gram,” but, rather, the “international prototype of the kilogram.”

The French did, however, create the Republican calendar out of whole cloth after the Revolution in the same spirit of enthusiastic change around new systems of measurements. And interestingly, it was the calendar that came first, having been adopted in ; the process toward metrication began the same year, but the system wasn’t wholly adopted in France until six years later, in .

The second design element that is worth mentioning is the location indicators for Paris, such as you see at the beginning of the “Gare du nord” section, immediately below. This design, now over 150 years old, is inextricably tied to Paris, as much an emblem of France’s capital as the Eiffel Tower, the Louvre, and the Arc de Triomphe. Although different designs are employed in real life, depending on the age of the sign, the importance of the location, and the length of the name, the one I have emulated is the most iconic, with the name of the arrondissement at the top and the name of the location in bold below (with smaller letters for connecting words). The colors are enshrined by law as “azur blue background” and “green bronze framing” (and there’s also some stuff about size and shadowing). If you actually want to make one, and get it right, the very precise, but not very romantic, substance description is also in the legal code: blue copper phtalocyanate (C32H16N8Cu) and green chrome oxide (Cr2O3).

Interestingly, one of the things that saved Paris from destruction in World War II was this complicated station system. Much as all roads are said to lead to Rome, all railroads lead to – but not through – Paris. Many European cities faced immense destruction around their train stations as the Allies or the Axis bombed those areas to interrupt the delivery of supplies. But Paris, with only terminal stations serving its long-distance routes, was not capable of serving as a transit point, and therefore was not a strategic military target➴ for either side.

I have always found the French delightful – cheerful, eager to help, and accommodating. The vast majority of French people with whom I have interacted have switched the conversation to English as soon as they recognized my nationality (even while apologizing, unnecessarily, for their own lack of skill in English), have found something nice to say about Americans and America (even in the most politically trying of times), and have gone out their way to assist me if I were lost or confused. I have never understood the prejudice that some Americans have about the French, as I have never seen even one single example of the stereotypical rude Parisian, and have seen countless counterexamples. If I were forced to come up with a complaint, it is that they don’t give me a chance to practice my French, as they are so unfailingly eager to switch to English if they know even just a little of it. No one in any country was mean-spirited to us on this trip, but one person made an anti-American jab in the Netherlands (“You’re from America; well, we all have our handicaps”), as did one couple in Ireland (“The seas are too rough for a landing? Blame the Americans [looking at us] for the global warming”) – so I think it’s important to note that we never heard such grating comments while in France. This is even more revealing in light of the timeline: We were in the Netherlands and Ireland for three days each, while we spent six days in Paris alone. And to boot, we interacted with more locals in Paris than in either of the other two countries. And that, in part, that’s why you won’t hear an anti-French commentary from me.

I like the Paris Métro quite a bit, but, unfortunately, the trip to Charles de Gaulle put me on the RER – – Paris’s commuter rail system, which is much more extensive than the Métro (hence my need to use it) and, in my opinion, much more cumbersome. Part of my frustration with the RER, I will concede, was simple lack of familiarity. And most of my frustration had to do with Line C (the first leg of my trip), as Line B, with its airport traffic, had enhancements that made travel easier on the unfamiliar tourist.

The problem with RER Line C is that it is a “line” in the same sense that an octopus is a “line.” On the RER map, Line C is everything colored in yellow, a mass-transit conglomeration that encompasses seven terminal stations (some of which are also intermediary stations, served apparently by different tracks, on other branches of Line C), in three major constituents:

  • Saint-Quentin-en-Yvelines (C7) to either
    • Dourdan-la-Forêt (C4) or
    • Saint-Martin d’Étampes (C6);
  • Versailles – Rive Gauche (C5) to Versailles – Chantiers (C8); and
  • Pontoise (C1) to Massy – Palaiseau (C2)

One thing you’ll notice is that end-point C3 is missing. Apparently it was once associated with the station Argenteuil on a line that’s no longer supported (an odd withdrawal for a system only thirty years old) – but the code may be reinstated as part of an expansion plan deeper into the suburbs, which will not only add C3 at a place across town from the original C3, but which will also also remove C8. So much like the octopus to which I’ve already compared it, Line C can have a leg cut off, and simply re-grow it in a different direction with no damage.

But what’s really confusing about Line C is these component lines listed above. For the second one, the end point stations are literally down the street from each other just over distant, both in the way-suburban 4 (out of 6 such zones), but covering an enormous loop through central Paris between them; you could walk on your knees between the stations faster than you could take a train from one to the other. But other extremes of the line are at vast distances: The route from Pontoise (C1) to Saint-Martin d’Étampes (C6) (which, I acknowledge, would require a transfer) is . The upshot of all this branching is that RER Line C is an enormous complex that comprises 84 stations on of track, but only 11 of those stations are on a segment that all the trains share.

So one faces a challenge in knowing which train will go to one’s destination (more so since some trains skip some stations). The governing authority has tried to decrypt these arcana in a way that can only be described as Kafkaesque: Each train has a four-letter . The first letter of the name signifies the train’s destination; the second letter indicates which group of stations it serves (“O” means “omnibus,” and signifies that all stations are covered); the third and fourth letters are arbitrarily selected to complete the word (usually matching a person’s name, but, oddly, often reflecting English-language name forms, such as “VICK,” or English words, such as “FOOT”). I didn’t understand the system at the time, and I’m still not certain whether I got on the appropriate trains by luck (although, if so, on the busy segment that I was traveling the odds were heavily in my favor) or if every single train serves the two stations I was using. But while I’m complaining about the organizational management of the , I have to praise their aesthetics. The RAPT has what I consider to be one of the best logos in the world. It echoes the shape of the Seine River, transformed into a face and looking optimistically upward, framed by a circle that can represent the sun, the moon, or the almost-circular city limits of Paris.

On the way back, I had the chance to chat with a local woman and to ask her about the word “lès,” which had confused me when I noticed it in the name of the end-point station, Saint-Rémy-lès-Chevreuse (B4). Perhaps this is a good time for my confession that I’m not a huge fan of the sound of French (although I am very please that I am able to speak it). The nearly universal opinion is that it is at or near the top of the list for the world’s most beautiful languages, but mine is the vote that keeps that opinion from being unanimous. I adore the French, their nation, and their food – but their language to my ears sounds like a malfunctioning contraption in a Dr. Seuss book. Among the languages I speak, I’d put its euphony only slightly above German.

Finally, I made it back to the apartment and turned my attention to deciphering the shower mechanism. Luckily for me, Billy had been at this for two hours, alternately freezing and scalding his skin, and was able to help me use it with minimal injury and discomfort.

After I got myself cleaned up, we went out to see the neighborhood – around the Eiffel Tower toward the western end of the seventh . The arrondissements of Paris are, on their surface, simple administrative divisions that (among other things) localize the postal code➴. But they are core to the self-perception, and external perception, of Paris. And even on a map they are somewhat romanticized – spiraling outward from the Louvre like the widening gyre of a falcon taking flight, or like the golden spiral of the shell of a perfectly formed snail. On the ground you might have no clue when you cross from the 15th arrondissement into the 7th – but when you’re making your hotel reservation, you’d never get them confused.

 
➴ In Paris, the first three digits of the postal code are always “750,” and the last two digits match the arrondissement. Thus, Mom’s postal code was “75007.”
