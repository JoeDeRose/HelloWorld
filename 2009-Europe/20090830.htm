5 April 1944		  

Billy and I had breakfast on the Vita Nova, and then went immediately to the along the . As promised, the line was both long and quick-moving, and we were inside within half an hour. And it was actually a very pleasant half-hour, as we made the acquaintance of a Briton and her teenage daughter, with whom we discussed our travel histories and aspirations. (The mother confessed that one of her more embarrassing experiences was during a tour of the Grand Canyon, when she asked when they would get to the area with the president’s heads.)

The house is remembered, and named, for its its most famous resident – but it is really a testament to her father, Otto Frank, who was cunning and shrewd➴ enough to get his family out of Germany at the start of the Nazi rise there, and then to protect them for most of the Nazi occupation of the Netherlands.

 
➴ I write these words knowing that “cunning” and “shrewd” are among the negative stereotypes foisted on Jews of that day, and of this day. It is a classic strategy of an intimidator – whether it be a schoolyard bully or the Third Reich – to intimidate and harass the victim, and then to belittle the same victim’s survival strategies. Cunning and shrewdness were called for, and the Frank family was fortunate to have a patriarch that embodied the traits that could keep them safe for so long.

But Otto’s logistical skills were apparently equaled by his people skills. Over the years of running two companies, Opekta (a gelling product) and Pectacon (spicing products), Otto earned the love and trust of his employees, four of whom felt enough of a bond that they agreed to his request (apparently even without hesitation) that they risk their lives to shelter him and his family from the Nazi advance.

With those commitments in hand, Otto transferred ownership of his companies to one of these associates, Johannes Kleiman, and resigned his directorship, to avoid allowing the companies to be seized as “Jewish-owned.” When his daughter, Margot, received a summons to appear for transport to a work camp, he activated his plan: The family left their home in a disheveled state (to suggest a hasty departure); left notes implying an escape to Switzerland (where they had family); and even left behind Anne’s pet cat, Moortje. They wore several layers of clothes (because suitcases would have aroused suspicion), and walked in the summer heat (because Jews were forbidden on public transit) to Prinsengracht 263. There they stepped through a hole behind a bookcase into the , and into history.

Because of Otto’s position and planning, the achterhuis➴ was much more comfortable than most hiding places used by Jews in Amsterdam, so much so that he was able to bring in a total of eight people: his family of four, the three members of the van Pels family (referred to as the “van Daan” family in the diary), and Fritz Pfeffer (whom Anne detested, naming him “Alfred ” in the diary). And because of Otto’s brilliance, it was ingeniously concealed: Prinsengracht 263 had less depth than most houses on its block, but the addition of the achterhuis made the rear of the property align with the neighboring structures. Thus, the four helpers were able to keep an active factory in operation without most people in the having any awareness of the additional structure. And even for those workers who needed to enter the lower two floors of the achterhuis, there was no connection to the area in which the refugees were sequestered. From the back, there was nothing to suggest a separation of structures that the authorities might wish to investigate. And from the street level it was impossible to see the achterhuis at all.

 
➴ The “achterhuis” is the correct term for what became known in English translations of the diary as the “Secret Annexe.” While it would be nice to imagine Anne converting this tragic situation in her mind to a game or a spy novel, she never used the term “Secret Annexe” (or its Dutch or German equivalents) in her writing. This phrase was simply used in the English version to help an audience unfamiliar with Dutch architectural terms.

The house and museum are well laid out for telling the story of Anne and her family (as long as you’re not in a wheelchair, as is unfortunately the case for most historic structures). The visit winds first through the front offices, then through the hidden entrance that the family passed through sixty-seven years ago – first to the lower floor of their living quarters, and then to the upper. The attic was not accessible, but the stairs were open and a mirror had been placed to allow a view of the struggling , a horse-chestnut, set behind the house, the sight of which gave Anne much pleasure during her confinement.

At one point, Anne wrote in her diary that her fondest wish upon being freed from hiding would be to go back to school. Anne never got that wish, as she died sick, alone, and far from home at the hands of people who hated and brutalized her for nothing more than her genealogy. Perhaps it would bring her some comfort to know that, even though she never returned to the classroom, she has become required reading for schoolchildren around the globe.

I don’t know why this story hits me so hard. Around six million Jews had their lives snuffed out in the Holocaust, and the suffering of each one of them was just as much a sacrilege as the intimidation, hunting, torture, and murder of Anne Frank and her family. And I’ve never even read The Diary of a Young Girl (the official English name of her memoir). But in researching this section I’ve easily shed as many tears for Anne as I did for John. The story is utterly heartbreaking to me.

 

John Speaks

b. April 27, 1945
d. August 27, 2009
John would be honored by this juxtaposition, and he deserves the honor.

As a ceaseless advocate for equality and fairness, John would brook no form of intolerance in his life. We never spoke, to my recollection, about Anne Frank, but I know from our other conversations that he viewed injustice toward anyone as a dreadful weight carried by us all.

But his ties to this story are even closer: John was not Jewish, but he had married a Jewish woman – Linda – with whom he raised their children in her faith and traditions. Though he never converted, John came to be very thoroughly versed in the rituals and practices of Jewish life, and maintained many of these traditions even after Linda had passed, and the children had moved, away. It was in John’s home that I celebrated my first Seder. It was with him that I built my first Sukkah. It was among him and his friends that last year I welcomed Rosh Hashanah with apples and honey.

And it was at his gravesite that I first laid a pebble in remembrance.

I think there are several embers that cause the fire of this story to sear at me so unrelentingly: As reflected in her diary, Anne was very much the mix of optimism and pragmatism and introspection that I try to balance in my own life: She gets angry at people (as I do), and then rebukes herself for her anger (as I do), and then tries to see things from a different perspective (as I do), and experiences a mixture of success and failure (as I do). And beyond all of these issues, she was absolutely full of hope, and I so want to believe that an innocent fifteen-year-old girl could be saved from a murderous mob by hope – as would be the case in Hollywood, a place of glamour and hope that filled Anne’s daydreams.

But most tragic aspect to me is that, at every turn, they almost made it. The timeline is as heartbreaking as the story.

Anne and Margot, of course, never did return.

With nowhere else to go, Otto returned to Prinsengracht 263, where he resumed his work with earnest determination in a building that must, in many ways, have haunted him. The diary was published in 1947, and soon thereafter Otto resigned in deference to the publicity needs in sharing with the world his daughter’s opus. It may be true that Otto and the four helpers survived the war, but in some ways their lives were still cut short by the world’s need for them to act, not on their own, but as a vehicle for telling the story of Anne Frank. It is a testament to their integrity, a mitzvah, that they all did so without complaint.

As of this writing, only the secretary, Miep Gies, still remains alive. At 100 years old, she is reportedly still alert, still follows the news, and still spends every August 4 in mourning.

After leaving the Anne Frank House, it was nearing lunchtime, and an opportunity to sit and reflect was something we both wanted and needed. We found an adorable café with wicker chairs on a bridge over the (Amsterdam’s oldest canal, which – by the way – isn’t a circle). On the day we sat there it was windy and a little chilly, and the food was filling but not very special; in my memory, as I write this several months later, it was perfect. Billy had eggs with ham and cheese over toast (a Dutch specialty according to the waiter, although not substantially different from an Egg McMuffin); I had mushrooms in cream sauce over bread (which seemed unusual to me, and quite tasty, but which according to the waiter was not a Dutch specialty, but rather just food).

And after lunch, we backtracked to the to see the Homomonument, one of the most unique monuments in the world, both in form as well as in function – and, indeed, one that was especially meaningful to us.

The conception of the Homomonument, commemorating all of those who have been persecuted for their homosexuality, came in 1970 when gay activists were arrested and denounced for attempting to place a lavender wreath at the at Amsterdam’s Dam Square. Construction of the monument advanced fairly quickly, and in 1980 a jury selected the design by Karin Daan for the Homomonument. The design is a large triangle, with smaller triangles at its points. Each of the points has a time period, a height, a direction, and (for lack of a better term) a shtick:

  • The side by the canal represents the present, and dips down to nearly the level of the water. It points (perhaps accusingly) toward the National Memorial, where the whole thing started, and (perhaps for this reason, or perhaps simply because it is the most striking point of the monument) it is where wreaths of remembrance are typically placed.
  • Moving clockwise, the next point represents the future. It is raised above the ground level, and points in the direction of COC Nederland➴. Because it is slightly higher than the plaza level, this point is frequently used as a podium.

  • The remaining triangle represents the past and rests at ground level. It points in the direction of the Anne Frank House. And if I may further editorialize, it strikes me that the Dutch are entirely too comfortable with the word “etc.” As you’ll remember from my background on the Netherlands in yesterday’s write-up, the Queen’s official titles actually carry the word “etc.” three times at the end. And now this ostensible love poem presents us not with love, but instead with “etc.” I’m left with the impression that if one asked directions to Paris, the reply might be, “Take the road toward Utrecht, etc.” Or that Dutch recipes trail off with instructions like, “Separate the yolks from 3 eggs, etc.”

Jan Wolkers, 1977		 Designing a memorial for the spot where the urn containing the ashes of victims of Auschwitz rests in Dutch soil, seems an impossible task. How do you find a form to mark a crime you know will never be erased – not in two, or even two thousand centuries from now, when our planet has ceased to exist? You rack your brains to conceive of an image that might convey the ignominy and suffering. You look up at the sky and can’t understand how that blue firmament could have stretched so serenely and indifferently above such horror, as though it were nothing but a field of flowers. And in a vision of justice, you see cracks appearing in that impassive blueness, as if the atrocities committed on the earth below her have violated eternity forever. That is what gave me the idea of placing broken mirrors on the small patch of earth above the urn of ashes. Never again, on this spot, will the sky be whole.

Jan Wolkers, 1977

After the memorial, I reconnoitered with Billy for supper. Having discovered the convenience of Amsterdam’s public transit infrastructure, we had picked up 48-hour transit passes as soon as yesterday’s 24-hour passes expired, to carry us through the end of our stay in Amsterdam. Realizing how dependent we had become on the trams after just one single day in the city, we immediately felt a deeper appreciation for the emphasis given in the Anne Frank House about Jews being barred from the trams and buses.

Most of our tram trips had been on lines 1, 2, and 5, and had passed through a neighborhood called Leidseplein, which we had remarked might be a nice place to come back to for dinner. So we hopped back on the tram and made our way up there.

I first had to hunt for money. If I’d been listening attentively, I’d have realized the ATM I needed was directly across the street. Fortunately, my listening skills were below par that day, which gave us a chance to explore the neighborhood.

I was quite amused to find a pissoir on the streets of Amsterdam. These public urinals have been all-but-entirely removed from the streets of Paris, where most Americans became familiar with them. But they remain a fixture of the streets of Amsterdam (at least, according to Wikipedia; this, however, is the only one that I saw). They leave very little to the imagination – hiding neither the identity of the person inside, nor the activity he is performing; generally, only the span from torso to knees is concealed from public scrutiny. In Islam, nudity occurs between the naval and the knees – and if the Netherlands weren’t so relentlessly Islamophobic, I would wonder wonder at the coincidence.

Just past the pissoir was a cool sculpture of iguanas, made all the more intriguing by a cat wandering in their midst.

And finally, we encountered a crowd watching a game of being played on one of those giant chessboards – the first time that I’ve seen on of those giant boards actually in use.

And at last I found the ATM and took out some euro, and we found a restaurant. In this case, we wanted to try , which have a diameter similar to a crêpe, and a thickness between a crêpe and an American pancake. Only Billy followed through on the experiment, though, selecting a ham-and-cheese pannenkoeke. I saw mussels on the menu and remembered something from KLM’s in-flight guide to Amsterdam about mussels being a specialty that we should look for. It’s hard to say who chose most wisely: The pannenkoeke was both delicious and unusual (he let me taste it), but the mussels were both spectacular and plentiful, coming in a deep pot that I wasn’t even sure I could finish off (yet I did). For dessert I had , a Dutch treat that is not unlike a beignet (but maybe a bit more buttery). Billy punted and had apple pie.

Speaking of Dutch Islamophobia, our waiter was Muslim. As our visit coincided with Ramadan, I commented on this fact and wished him “Ramadan Mubarak,” the standard greeting for the holy month. He was astonished, and enormously pleased, that we had even heard of Ramadan, let alone that we knew the greeting and chose to express it to him. I explained that in America it was fairly commonplace to know the rhythms of other religions, which was a piece of good news that took him completely by surprise. I think we made good ambassadors in this interaction.

After dinner, we took the tram back to Station Centraal, walked back over to the Vita Nova, and watched movies on Billy’s laptop until we were ready to fall asleep.
