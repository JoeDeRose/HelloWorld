Are you ever going to finish this trip report?

Well, obviously, yes.

Okay, fine, but why the hell did it take so long?

It was hard for a lot of reasons, the enumeration of which skews heavily toward whining. In short:

  1. Life Was Hard: When I came back, I had missed a week (as planned) of a Chinese class in which I had enrolled at Emory, and was struggling to catch up. Then Pepper (our dog) fell ill, eventually dying of that illness. This led me to withdraw from the Chinese class, which I had never done before with a language class, and which I took pretty hard. And my sadness over Pepper reminded me of my sadness over John Speaks (who had died just before the trip began). With all of this working together, my comfort zone was to enjoy new things: diving into a big new project at work that I really was enjoying; setting up my new Windows 7 computer (which I received from Mom after she no longer needed it upon her return, and on which I wrote this report); taking minor trips with friends; and exploring more interesting aspects of the restaurant scene in Atlanta. I just didn’t want to write in the weeks after we got back – but, unfortunately, this is when the memories were most fresh.
  2. The Trip Was Hard to Report: A lot of people really have enjoyed my previous trip reports, and I enjoy hearing that so much that I wanted to do well by them. But for the other trips, I’ve gone places that other people aren’t familiar with. Just to tell you that I set foot in Orkney or Llanfairpwll➴ or San Marino or Bagno Vignoni or Ek Balam or Petra or Wadi Rum was exotic enough without an excruciating detail of the history of those places. But Amsterdam, Paris, and Ireland are well on the beaten path, and a lot of people I know are already pretty familiar with these places. I felt like I really had to dig in to the places we saw if I wanted to bring any insight to them.
 
➴ That’s the short local name for Llanfairpwllgwyngyllgogerychwyrndrobwllllantysiliogogogoch.

Really, I very nearly decided not to finish it several times – but I kept coming back to our old trip reports, and experienced the flood of nostalgia that came from reading through them. So I knew I needed to finish. I set myself an absolute deadline that this report would be finished before Mom and I departed on the Israel trip, and – by the skin of my teeth – it was.

Why the potty mouth?

I don’t believe that any words are inherently inappropriate – so I choose the ones that will best help me convey an idea. Sometimes that word is “assignation”; sometimes it’s “fuck.”

How did you choose the color scheme?

In older reports, this wasn’t something I thought much about – but with the 2007 trip to the Middle East, I picked a color scheme that reminded me of Petra, the destination around which that entire trip was planned. And I knew I wanted to give the design some attention here as well.

My original color scheme revolved around blue – but I wasn’t satisfied with it (and neither were others to whom I showed it; this means you, Randy). Finally, I realized that the slate brown really spoke to my memories of the trip: It is the color of the canals of Amsterdam, the Eiffel Tower, the balustrades along the Seine, the sculptures in the Stravinsky Fountain, the Rock of Cashel, the Cliffs of Moher, and the Skelligs. In many ways it is, in my mind, the color of Europe. Some may view it as dingy, but to me it is stolid, impressive, and ancient. Once the decision was made, I felt very good about this color choice.

You’ll also see a hint of orange in some places relating to portions of the trip that were in the Netherlands; blue in Paris; green in Ireland.

And what’s up with the quotation marks?

I’m delighted that you noticed! I always try to reflect the proper form of punctuation when I am presenting information in a non-English language, but quotation marks were especially complicated on this trip.

  • In American English➴, of course, we used left and right double quotes to frame quotations: . Irish follows the American English system: .
  • Dutch closes quotes the same way we do, but opens them with a lower double quote: .
  • German opens quotes as Dutch does, but closes them with what we in English-speaking countries think of as as left double quote: .
  • With Latin, I took a little liberty, giving it the “Latin quotes” or “angle quotes” that are common in Romance languages derived from it: . I did this in part because of the tell-tale name “Latin quotes” and in part because of the similarity of the languages – but quote marks were not used historically in Latin, and Latin-language articles on Wikipedia carry marks similar to those in English. Other references, however, say that Latin quotation marks are the same as Greek (without describing them), while Wikipedia confirms that Greek uses angle quotes.
  • Italian uses angle quotes: .
  • French uses angle quotes, but differs from other languages in that it includes a 0.25em non-breaking space between the quote marks and the text: .
  • Old Norse is Old Norse, of course, course, and no one wrote quotes in Old Norse, of course – at least not in the era that I’ve quoted here. But since it evolved into most of the Scandinavian languages, I took those as my model. I really wanted to use the Danish system, which reverses the Latin quotes, as in , or the Swedish system that points them both toward the right, as in . But at the end of the game, this seemed disingenuous: The area that now includes Denmark and Sweden spoke the Old East Norse dialect, while the Old West Norse dialect was spoken in what is now Norway, Iceland, the Faroes and northern Scotland, and a scattering of communities around Wales and Ireland (including the Dublin area). Indeed, the Old West Norse dialect is, apparently, the only one that had any influence in Ireland. So, since the only areas of this trip in which I had an Old Norse frame of reference were in Ireland, I went with the Latin quotes as would be used in Norwegian: , but I could have used the German system – – as that is the scheme used in Iceland. (Whatever the hell they do in the Faroes is beyond the reach even of Wikipedia.)
 
➴ In case you’re wondering about this distinction, American English frames the main quotation with double quotes, and an inner quotation with single quotes: She remarked, “The British term for ‘French fries’ is ‘chips.’” (It also puts closing periods and commas inside the quotation mark(s), as in this example.)

By contrast, British English reverses this structure (and puts periods and commas outside, unless they are part of the quote): She remarked, ‘The American term for “chips” is “French fries”.’

It hurts – physically and psychologically – for me to say this, but the British system is immensely superior.
