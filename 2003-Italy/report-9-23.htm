  • Mom and Meridel chose to spend this day catching up on needed rest, so Billy and I left early to return the rental car in Sorrento in the (successful) hope of avoiding a late fee for arriving in town after closing hour the previous evening.

  • When Avis opened at 8:30, we returned the car, walked to the train station, checked the schedule in preparation for our return the same evening, taxied to the marina, and took the first hydrofoil to Capri.

Hydrofoil Ticket from Sorrento to Capri

  
  • We knew Capri was amazing even from the boat:  The island jutted from the Tyrrhenian Sea like the fist of Poseidon, capped by 570-meter Monte Solaro.

Capri

  
  • We made it our first task to ascend Monte Solaro, which was an unexpectedly involved process for such a small island:  We began from the marina on the funivia (cable car), which took us up a short but steep hill to Capri town (brown and black line on the map).  There we boarded a bus for Anacapri, which made its journey along the most shockingly precarious of cliffside roadways and switchbacks (yellow and black line on the map).  Finally, we took the seggiovia, a twelve-minute chair lift, to top of Monte Solaro (red and black line on the map).  This bus, though!  I must speak more about the bus:  The road to Anacapri was appended to the side of a mountain as one might tack up a shelf in a workroom to hold items that are neither fragile nor precious; we tried to get pictures, but in the photographs it was impossible to distinguish the tiny roadway from the gargantuan mountain where it hung.  And it was narrow!  On the ride up, Billy and I both had seats on the right side of the bus (there were only single seats on each side of the aisle), and at times we couldn't see the roadway's protective barrier; just the quarter-mile drop into the sea.  On the way back, the bus was more crowded, and as I stood pushed against the back window I saw cars and busses shooting past us in the other direction, but could not bring my mind to comprehend how a vehicle of any size was passing us in the few inches available to do so.  By the end of the journey, we had ridden to the top of a 570-meter (1,990-foot) mountain with our feet dangling from a seat attached to a thin cable – but Billy and I both agreed that the scariest part was the bus.

  • After Monte Solaro, we made our way back down to sea level as we had come and set out to see the Grotta Azzurra (Blue Grotto), a stunning cave where sunlight illuminates the water from underneath a wall that does not reach all the way to the sea floor.  This procedure, too, was surprisingly complex:  First, one purchased transportation on a motorboat to the grotto (meaning transportation to the water just outside the grotto), but there was no opportunity to make any further arrangement at this point.  Once near the grotto, rowboats approached the motorboat and took on a few passengers at a time.  These boats were then rowed to another boat where two more transactions were made (in the rocking sea, jammed into a rowboat) – one to pay for the services of the rowboat and another to pay for admission to the grotto.  Finally, all financial disbursement having been completed, the rowboat operator paddled to the entrance, waited for the water to recede between waves, pulled the boat in on a chain, circled the cave singing loudly (to showcase the natural acoustics), and then returned us to the motorboat.

  • Satisfied with our time in Capri, and having been returned conveniently to the Marina, Billy and I purchased ferry tickets to Naples and returned to the mainland.  (We couldn't have asked for better timing; each time we showed up at a dock, we were able to purchase tickets for a boat leaving in the next few minutes.)
Ferry Ticket from Capri to Naples
  • We were girded for the worst in Naples, having been warned (even by Italians) of lawlessness and filth – but we found Naples to be quite pleasant on our brief visit.  Our route was from the marina to the train station, two areas of any city that tend to the seedy.  But Naples presented us with clean streets, attractive buildings, and gleaming modern subway stations.  Our progress was slowed because I didn't ask for directions properly (the funicolare and the metro are not the same thing even though they are run by the same agency and you can transfer freely between them) and because the Naples subway was much less frequent than other transit systems we had used.  In our efforts to find the subway we stumbled past two beautiful structures, the Castel Nuovo and the Galleria Umberto I.

  • The ride took more than an hour (as we knew to expect), so when we finally reached Sorrento and taxied back to the hotel, we were quite ready for supper.  We met up with Mom and Meridel at the hotel restaurant, and I had the good fortune to order zuppa Pavese (Pavese soup) – an egg-on-toast-soup, for lack of a better description.  A victory comparable to ordering this splendid dish was finding the recipe for it upon my return home.  Even better was learning the story behind the soup:  King Francis I, defeated by Emperor Charles V, roamed the countryside in Pavese desperate for a meal.  A peasant prepared soup for him – but as she prepared to ladle the broth over toast as was the custom, it occurred to her that the dish was not appropriate for a king, or even a former king.  So she broke an egg over the toast first, and then ladled on the broth.

Pavese Soup

Serves 6

6 T unsalted butter
2 T olive oil
6 slices Italian style bread, 1 inch slices, fried
6 cups chicken or beef broth
6 eggs
Salt and freshly ground white pepper
1 1/4 cup freshly grated Parmesan cheese

Heat the butter and olive oil in a skillet and sauté the bread, browning on both sides.  Remove and place on paper towels. Meanwhile bring the broth to a simmer and keep it there.  Heat individual soup plates or bowls in an oven.  Place a slice of bread in each bowl or plate.  Break an egg onto the bread, being careful not to break the yolk.  Sprinkle with Parmesan, salt and pepper to taste.  Once all eggs are placed on the toast carefully ladle the very hot broth onto the eggs in the bowls.  The heat of the broth should cook the eggs.  If the eggs are not cooked to your taste, place in a hot oven for a minute or two.  Serve with extra Parmesan at table.
