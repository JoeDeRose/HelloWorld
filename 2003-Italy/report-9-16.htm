  • We started the morning with a walk past the Cappelle Medici (Medici Chapels) and San Lorenzo on our way to the Galleria dell'Accademia.  At the Accademia we saw Michelangelo's David – my mother in tears.  Seeing this statue had been one of her goals for as long as I can remember.  David was covered on his rear and left flank with scaffolding, but was amazing nonetheless.  Pictures in the Accademia, of course, were forbidden.

Admission Ticket to the Galleria dell'Accademia

  
  • After leaving Mom and Meridel at the Accademia, Billy and I went to climb to the Cupola of the Duomo.  The view was spectacular, but I wished it had been possible to get a better sense of Bruneleschi's innovative structural design.

Entrance Ticket to the Cupola of the Duomo in Florence

  • After lunch we met up with Mom and Meridel again to visit the Galleria degli Uffizi.  (Again, no pictures – but it's nothing you can't find reproduced in any book of the greatest art of Europe of the last millennium.)

Admission Ticket to the Galleria degli Uffizi

  
  • Again leaving Mom and Meridel in the Museum, Billy and I retreated to the streets of Florence to wander along the Arno River for the remainder of the afternoon.

  • We rested a bit at the hotel, and then went out in the evening to get our last look at the sights of Florence, giving more scrutiny to the the Loggia dei Lanzi in the Piazza della Signoria – which was built in the twelfth century for assemblies and which now houses a portion of Florence's embarrassing wealth of great sculpture.

  • And lastly we patted the nose of Il Porcinello – a bronze statue of a drooling boar at the Mercato Nuovo; legend holds that those who touch his nose will return to Florence.

Il Porcellino Statue at the Mercato Nuovo

 
  • Billy and I finished the day at Stazione Santa Maria Novella to buy our next day's tickets to Milan.  I used the automatic ticket machine and was done in ten minutes.
