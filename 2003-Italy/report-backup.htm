  • Then we took the express train from into Rome from Leonardo da Vinci airport in Fiumicino, and then the Rome subway (the "Met. Ro." for "Metropolitana Romana") to our hotel, one stop away at the Repubblica station.  The subway was efficient and popular, but it was clear that the trains were left parked overnight in an unsecured lot:  Their exteriors were covered ceiling to wheels in graffiti, so much so that it was occasionally difficult to tell where the door was until the train stopped and it opened.  We very quickly adjusted to the Italian system of buying a ticket before boarding, having it validated in a machine at the entrance, and showing it (if requested) to a transit system official; by the end of the trip we were well accustomed to looking for a tabacchi (tobacco shop) or newsstand for the ticket purchase before any trip on public transit.  It rained a little on the train, but was dry by the time we arrived at Roma Termini station.  The weather stayed sunny and dry for the next fifteen days.

  • We were surprised to find that the Hotel Adler did not occupy its entire building, but rather was a tenant on one floor of an office building.  We later learned that this is a fairly common structure for Italian hotels.  We also were introduced to another quirk of Italian guesthouses:  that the room key is left at the desk whenever one departs, and retrieved when one returns.  The elevator was one of the traditional European kinds, with outer and inner doors that must both be opened manually.  The Adler had a great breakfast, and rooms that looked out over a quiet courtyard; the towels were made of the material that we tend to reserve in America for tablecloths, but we came to love the place over the course of our four days in Rome.  We stayed at hotels that we liked better, but none – I think – of which we were more fond.
  • After arrival, Billy and I immediately made for the Colosseum.  This was our first introduction to the layers of antiquity and ruins in Rome, and I think we may have been walking with our mouths open.  We came first to the Forum, and then to the Colosseum itself

  • It was still early in the day, so Billy and I walked through one of Rome's nicer shopping districts to the Pantheon and the Trevi Fountain.  After having struggled to frame a good photograph of Bernini's Colonnade, and now finding it impossible to frame a good picture of the Pantheon, I realized why one so rarely sees good pictures of these famous buildings, yet finds them so attractive in drawings:  Their size and position simply make them all-but-impossible to photograph well.

  • From the Trevi Fountain, we went toward Via Veneto, a park-like area of  Rome, to see the Capuchin Crypt at Santa Maria della Concezione.  Here the resident Capuchin monks assembled, over the course of 300 years, art from the bones of their deceased brothers:  Skeletons hang from ceilings; bones are piled ceiling-high along the walls to form mausoleums; skulls decorate the doors and walkways.  Unfortunately, photographs are forbidden here.
  • At Via Veneto, we checked out the Piazza del Barberini (with the Fontana del Tritone) and saw – or, at least, noticed – our first of Rome's many drinking fountains:  the Fontana dei Api (Fountain of the Bees), so named because it is decorated with the bees that were a symbol of this area's influential Barberini family.
  • Then we made our way to the Spanish Steps and saw both the Piazza d'Espagna and the Fontana della Barcaccia.  Many of Rome's fountains, like Trevi and this one, are "mostre" – places where an aqueduct terminates.  The aqueduct that feeds the Fontana della Barcaccia, however, is of very low pressure, so instead of a grand fountain it is designed as a sinking boat.  At the Piazza d'Espagna, we also discovered an incredibly large and interesting McDonald's restaurant while searching for a restroom:  One enters through a long passage, in which archeological finds are evident in the walls; and the dining room is enormous in comparison to what we find familiar in the U.S.

  • We took a taxi to our hotel, the Pensione Centrale.  At first the enormous dark staircase leading to the hotel seemed grimy and foreboding, but when we learned that the building was a converted palace it took on a whole new aura.  The rooms were huge, the locks still used skeleton keys, some of the rooms still had their painted ceilings, and our room had a view of the Medici Chapels.  We thought the elevator was a little subpar:  It was tiny, and it only took us to the registration level, one floor below our room.  Little did we know it was the last one we would see until we were back in Rome.

Pensione Centrale Room Key

  
  • We started at the Duomo, Santa Maria del Fiore to be precise, the spectacular cathedral of Florence.  Experts will say that the most noteworthy part of the cathedral is Filippo Bruneleschi's dome, and that the façade is just a nineteenth-century revision.  The dome does make a great story:  The cathedral was left unfinished, Florence betting that someday the technological advancement necessary to complete the structure would come (and we see in three dimensions that their faith in the future was well placed).  But it is the façade that brings this building to life.  We then looked at the Duomo's interior (surprisingly drab for having such a beautiful skin) and at Bruneleschi's tomb in the crypt.

  • After the Duomo, we wandered through the pedestrian area of Florence, seeing places such as the Piazza della Repubblica (ringed with souvenir stands), the Mercato Nuovo (covered with souvenir stands), and, especially, the Ponte Vecchio, Florence's most famous bridge (lined with souvenir stands).  The Ponte Vecchio (Old Bridge) is lined with shops and capped with a portion of the Vasari Corridor, the overhead walkway that the Medicis used to get between the Uffizi and the Pitti Palace.  The Ponte Vecchio was the only Florentine bridge to have been spared by the Germans when they retreated from Florence near the end of World War II.  I have always thought that the Ponte Vecchio was singularly unattractive in pictures, but in person, in context, I can attest that it is a thing of beauty.

  • The entire reason that we were in Milan was to see Leonardo da Vinci's The Last Supper, so we started out early for Santa Maria delle Grazie to meet our reserved showing.  (It was only after our return to the U.S. that some confusion about the name of this masterpiece was resolved:  In Italian it is referred to alternately as L'Ultima Cena and Il Cenacolo; the former means "the last supper"; the latter is the name of the room in which the Biblical story of the last supper took place.)  One hears about the faded masterpiece of light pastels that has suffered over time through incompetent restoration attempts in previous centuries, the contempt of Napoleon Bonaparte's troops who used the refectory in which it is placed as a stable, and the destruction of the room (but not this wall, nor Donato Montorfano's Crucifixion on the facing wall) by Allied bombs in World War II.  In the context of such preparation, we were delighted and astounded to see that the restoration presented us with a vibrant and deep painting, which we were assured was a restoration and not a recreation, that could be thoroughly seen and experienced (thoroughly, that is, except for the door through Jesus's feet that was installed in 1652 to give access to the kitchen).  Much effect was created with an extremely dim room and specific illumination of the painting – but this effect was brilliant for its simplicity and brought The Last Supper to life.

  • After The Last Supper, we took a tram back to the Speronari to retrieve our luggage and depart.  I had long thought that European-style streetcars would be the salvation of American cities; it was Milan that changed my mind on this subject.  A single line in a grassy median, such as the St. Charles streetcar in New Orleans, is attractive and pleasant.  A jumble of overhead wires, and clusters of trams clogging the streets, is much less appealing.  It did give Milan a certain urban feel that was nice in its place, but that would be to the detriment of any city in which it is newly installed.

Pavese Soup

Serves 6

6 T unsalted butter
2 T olive oil
6 slices Italian style bread, 1 inch slices, fried
6 cups chicken or beef broth
6 eggs
Salt and freshly ground white pepper
1 1/4 cup freshly grated Parmesan cheese

Heat the butter and olive oil in a skillet and sauté the bread browning on both sides. Remove and place on paper towels. Meanwhile bring the broth to a simmer and keep it there. Heat individual soup plates or bowls in an oven. Place a slice of bread in each bowl or plate. Break an egg onto the bread, being careful not to break the yolk. Sprinkle with Parmesan, salt and pepper to taste. Once all eggs are placed on the toast carefully ladle the very hot broth onto the eggs in the bowls. The heat of the broth should cook the eggs. If the eggs are not cooked to your taste, place in a hot oven for a minute or two. Serve with extra Parmesan at table.

  • I had scheduled the train in the hope of getting to Rome in time to see the Cappella Sistina (Sistine Chapel).  It was going to be close, but I hoped I could make it.  As soon as we found the hotel and submitted our passports, I dashed out the door and grabbed a taxi to the Musei Vaticani (Vatican Museums) without even seeing the hotel room.  I made it:  I paid my admission, hurried past tourists who had a greater appreciation of art than I, skipped the Rafael Rooms, and walked down the stairs to the awe-inspiring chapel.  I found Michelangelo's Last Judgment on the altar wall more compelling than the Creation of Adam on the ceiling, but at least I was finally seeing it for myself.  Pictures were forbidden in the chapel, but were permitted in other areas of the museums, so I circuited through again, visiting the Rafael Rooms this time, and taking some of those pictures of other ceilings.

  • So my coin in the Trevi Fountain worked:  It had given me the chance to see the Sistine Chapel much sooner than I had hoped.  I had time, but I didn't return to the Trevi Fountain; I'll leave my next visit to Rome to chance.
  • I returned to meet Mom, Meridel, and Billy at the Hotel Italia.  I had selected a different hotel for the last night in Rome, in case we had disliked the Adler and dreaded returning to it.  The Italia was a fine hotel, but I was in a nostalgic mood and I really missed the Adler.
  • We went to dinner at the Ristorante Barocco, where we had enjoyed our first dinner in Italy two weeks before.  I ordered the Pizza Frutti di Mare again; it was delicious again.  The sand was running out of the hourglass.
  • Billy and I walked down the Via Nazionale one last time, and he bought a leather jacket as he had wanted to do since our arrival.  We had seen a huge structure several times on our previous days in Rome, and this time decided to see what it was.  It turned out to be the Monumento Vittorio Emanuele, a tremendous and imposing monument to the unification of Italy.

  • And we did wake up at 3:45 AM.  The car to the airport passed some of the sights we had seen before, notably the Bocca della Verità and the Circus Maximus.
  • Safely on Alitalia, we passed out of Italy as we had entered it:  across the majestic Alps.  On this pass, I saw a glacier!

Alps

  
  • If the trip was not perfect, it was at least ideal:  We all enjoyed the sights and each other's company, the time spent in each city allowed us to see our full itinerary without rushing and without getting bored, we enjoyed beautiful weather and delicious food, and trip plans went without a hitch.
  • None of the caveats that were issued regarding Italy seemed accurate to me:  Everything was efficiently handled exactly on schedule (I could set my watch by the Italian trains); no one made any move (that we could discern at least) to deceive us or steal from us; while there were some beggars, no gypsy children (or anyone) reached in our pockets – the beggars were all, in fact, adults; we were never, so far as I know, shortchanged (and, indeed, I miscalculated on a couple of occasions the change to expect and was surprised to receive more than I anticipated); driving was orderly, and while some Italian drivers are aggressive, others are as timid and slow as we have at home; crossing the streets in Rome and other large cities was not a matter of skill and courage, but was governed by crossing signals and pedestrian right-of-ways that were largely obeyed by people on the sidewalk and vehicles in the street.
