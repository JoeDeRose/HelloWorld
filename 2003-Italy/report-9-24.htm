  • We started the day with sad news:  We learned that the wonderful Pensione La Minervetta, our beautiful cliffside hotel, was to close at the end of the month as it had been informed that its lease on this property was not being renewed.  I'm sure the space will reopen (perhaps with an elevator) as a much more expensive hotel.  And it has every right to be a much more expensive hotel.  But I hate to see the La Minervetta go.
  • Billy and I showed Mom and Meridel to the Circumvesuviana, and the four of us made our way to Stazione Napoli Centrale, and then back to Rome.  Billy and I felt like experts on Roman geography when the train passed the Acquedotto Claudio (which we had visited on the vespa), and we knew where we were.
Train Ticket from Naples to Rome
  • I had scheduled the train in the hope of getting to Rome in time to see the Cappella Sistina (Sistine Chapel).  It was going to be close, but I hoped I could make it.  As soon as we found the hotel and submitted our passports, I dashed out the door and grabbed a taxi to the Musei Vaticani (Vatican Museums) without even seeing the hotel room.  I made it:  I paid my admission, hurried past tourists who had a greater appreciation of art than I, skipped the Rafael Rooms, and walked down the stairs to the awe-inspiring chapel.  I found Michelangelo's Last Judgment on the altar wall more compelling than the Creation of Adam on the ceiling, but at least I was finally seeing it for myself.  (I was, apparently, in disagreement with Guy de Maupassant; according to Dan Brown in Angels & Demons, he commented that the Last Judgment "looks like a canvas of a fair, painted for a wrestling booth by an ignorant coal heaver.")  Pictures were forbidden in the chapel, but were permitted in other areas of the museums, so I circuited through again, visiting the Rafael Rooms this time, and taking some of those pictures of other ceilings.

  • Then I walked around the Vatican City walls to the Basilica San Pietro to climb to the cupola.  I paid the surcharge for the elevator, but seeing the line I opted to walk up instead.  Highlights included seeing the mosaic art up close (all of the art in St. Peter's is mosaics, but it is difficult to discern this from ground level) and looking out over the Piazza San Pietro.

  • So my coin in the Trevi Fountain worked:  It had given me the chance to see the Sistine Chapel much sooner than I had hoped.  I had time, but I didn't return to the Trevi Fountain; I'll leave my next visit to Rome to chance.
  • I returned to meet Mom, Meridel, and Billy at the Hotel Italia.  I had selected a different hotel for the last night in Rome, in case we had disliked the Adler and dreaded returning to it.  The Italia was a fine hotel – but I was in a nostalgic mood and I really missed the Adler.
  • We went to dinner at the Ristorante Barocco, where we had enjoyed our first dinner in Italy two weeks before.  I ordered the Pizza Frutti di Mare again.  And it was delicious again.  I wished we had sat at the same table; I wished we had the same waiter; I wished we were starting over.
  • Billy and I walked down the Via Nazionale one last time, and he bought a leather jacket as he had wanted to do since our arrival.  We had seen a huge structure at the end of this street several times on our previous days in Rome, and this time decided to find out what it was.  It turned out to be the Monumento Vittorio Emanuele, a tremendous and imposing monument to the unification of Italy.  On our previous days here, the monument's white marble had gleamed like a beacon, but Rome was dark and slippery after a brief afternoon downpour and the monument, like everything else, was dulled.  Two weeks of bright blue skies were ending; the sun was setting on our holiday, and it was back to the world of rain.

  • With nothing really to do, and an early flight, we returned to the hotel.  The sand had run out of the hourglass.
