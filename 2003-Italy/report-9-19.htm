  • Mom, Meridel, Billy, and I started the day at the Campanile, the reconstruction of an identical bell tower – dating to 1514 – that collapsed in 1902.  The loss of the original tower was a tremendous blow to Venice, and "com'era e dov'era" ("as it was and where it was") became a rallying cry for its reconstruction.

Campanile in Venice

       

Entrance to the Campanile

  
  • After taking time to enjoy views of Venice from the top of the Campanile, the four of us made our way to the Palazzo Ducale (Doge's Palace).  The Ponte dei Sospiri (Bridge of Sighs) was the money shot, but they walked us through a lot of rooms on the way to it – some interesting and gorgeous; some less so.  (Pictures of the interior were forbidden, but photos of the courtyard were allowed, as were pictures of the outside through windows.)

Venice
  • In the course of our wanderings, we found an attractive restaurant just off the Campo San Cassiano, with an arbor-covered terrace near a minor canal.  We told Mom and Meridel about it, and the four of us returned there for dinner – Mom and Meridel picking up the tab to thank us for handling so much of the planning.  It must have been an authentic stop, as the wait staff spoke almost no English whatsoever, which would have been unusual in any large Italian city, but was especially so in Venice (where customers are often greeted first in English because, as one clerk told me, "It's practical").  The meal was delicious (I had a first course of spaghetti with seafood and a second course of cuttlefish in squid ink sauce with polenta), and greatly appreciated.
  • At the end of the evening, we wandered back to the hotel in the Venetian twilight.
