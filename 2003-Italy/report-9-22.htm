  • We then headed south toward Sorrento.  After a day of driving the mountain roads of Le Marche, this day's driving down the Autostrada at 140 km/h (20 km/h over the speed limit) was a dream.  We stopped for lunch at a service area where we were amused to see that the kitsch for sale included replicas of U.S. license plates.
  • Then we arrived at Pompei (or, in English, Pompeii).  The most astonishing thing about Pompei was how accessible it was.  This is a fragile ruin of international significance – indeed, a World Heritage Site – and with little exception we were allowed to touch, lean against, walk on, and take flash photographs of, the ruins.  The other surprising thing was how big it was:  Only about a third of the estimated extent of the city has been excavated, and we covered only a fraction of the excavated part. Suffering a bit of loneliness at the long separation from Prince and Pepper, we enjoyed petting the friendly stray dogs of which Pompei has many.

  • After Pompei, we drove to Sorrento via the Costiera Amalfitana (Amalfi Coast), which was beautiful beyond words.  This was, I think, the curviest road I have ever seen (the title heretofore being held by the road I drove on the previous day), and took us through the gorgeous hillside villages of Maiori, Amalfi, Praiano, and Positano.  The cliffs were there, just like in the roads of Le Marche – but this time they were stuffed with buildings carved into the face or set under overhangs, and this time our convoluted road wasn't on the cliffs; rather, it tracked a tiny ribbon of asphalt between the cliffs and the Mediterranean.  And here there were busses:  huge lumbering busses that brought traffic to a complete stop for minutes at a time as they tried to pass each other on hairpin turns.  Unfortunately, we were rushing to Sorrento to return the rental car by a deadline (which we missed anyway), so we didn't take time to explore or photograph this area that so richly deserved it.  My biggest regret of the trip is that I didn't just pull to the side of the road in Maiori, lean against a rock, and nibble at a loaf of bread while staring out at the beautiful sea.
  • We checked into the Pensione La Minervetta, a spectacular hotel set on the edge of a seaside cliff, and descended (the entrance is at the top of the cliff) to our room overlooking the Bay of Naples and Vesuvius.  Even the bathroom had a balcony with a view.  We left our balcony doors open at night to enjoy the Mediterranean breeze.  And we marveled at how our hotels had gotten better and better in each city – with the exception, of course, of the Hotel Speroni in Milan (which really wasn't terrible, just a departure from the trend).
