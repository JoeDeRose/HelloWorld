  • The entire reason that we were in Milan was to see Leonardo da Vinci's The Last Supper, so we started out early for Santa Maria delle Grazie to meet our reserved showing.  (It was only after our return to the U.S. that some confusion about the name of this masterpiece was resolved:  In Italian it is referred to alternately as L'Ultima Cena and Il Cenacolo; the former means "the last supper"; the latter is the name of the room in which the Biblical story of the last supper took place.)  One hears about the faded masterpiece of light pastels that has suffered over time through incompetent restoration attempts in previous centuries, the contempt of Napoleon Bonaparte's troops who used its refectory home as a stable, and the destruction of the room (but not this wall, nor Donato Montorfano's Crucifixion on the facing wall) by Allied bombs in World War II.  In the context of such preparation, we were delighted and astounded to see that the restoration presented us with a vibrant and deep painting, which we were assured was a restoration and not a recreation, that could be thoroughly seen and experienced (thoroughly, that is, except for the door through Jesus's feet that was installed in 1652 to give access to the kitchen).  Much effect was created with an extremely dim room and specific illumination of the painting – but this effect was brilliant for its simplicity and brought The Last Supper to life.

  • After The Last Supper, we took a tram back to the Speronari to retrieve our luggage and depart.  I had long thought that European-style streetcars would be the salvation of American cities; it was Milan that changed my mind on this subject.  A single line in a grassy median, such as the St. Charles streetcar in New Orleans, is unique and pleasant.  A jumble of overhead wires and clusters of trams clogging the streets is much less appealing.  It did give Milan a certain urban feel that was nice in its place, but that would be to the detriment of any city in which it is newly installed.

Milan Tram Ticket

  
  • Our work in Milan done, we made our way to Milan's Stazione Centrale for our train ride to Venice.  Although the four of us had arrived here the previous day, and Billy and I had come again that evening to purchase our tickets for today's trip, this was the first opportunity we had taken to look at the station and enjoy its architecture.  Milano Centrale, brought to its current design in 1931, is described as one of the finest examples of fascist architecture; it was indeed an amazing building to behold.

Detail of Façade of Stazione Milano Centrale

       

Fountain

  
  • Not only was the station enjoyable, the train ride was a delight as well.  We were helped by the fact that we embarked at the end of the line, but Milano Centrale was efficient, and we had climbed the learning curve.  The journey was pure relaxation as we skirted the foothills of the Italian Alps and crossed the Veneto Plain, alongside Lake Garda and through stops at Brescia, Verona, Vicenza, Padova, and Venezia Mestre on our way to Stazione Venezia Santa Lucia.  Crossing the Venetian Causeway was like entering someone else's dream.
Train Ticket from Milan to Venice

Venice
  • We arrived in Venice in good order and immediately made for the vaporetto (the waterbus that one uses to travel around the city) and traveled half the length of the Canal Grande (Grand Canal) to the Ponte Rialto (Rialto Bridge) stop near our hotel.  (N.B.:  Although one generally, and properly, refers to riding the waterbus system as "taking a vaporetto" no matter what type of boat one boards, precision demands awareness that "vaporetto" is the word for one of three very distinct types of boats that comprise this system, the other two being the motoscafo and the motonave.)

Venice Vaporetto Ticket

  
  • The Locanda ai Bareteri is extremely well located, halfway between the Ponte Rialto and Piazza San Marco (St. Mark's Square); it is a great hotel (with real human-sized bathrooms we were pleased to find).  I could have jumped, blindfolded and drunk, to the balcony across the street – but that's the charm of Venice.  Our room at the top of the hotel was a ninety-one-step climb from the street, and worth every huff.  Interestingly, the hotel considered our room at the top of this five-story climb to be on the fourth floor of the establishment:  The entrance level was floor zero in Italy, of course – but floor zero here was one flight up from the street.

  • Billy and I got out in the afternoon and just walked around the sestieri (Venetian districts) of San Marco and Dorsoduro, marveling at tiny streets and attractive bridges.  We made our way over to Santa Maria della Salute, the grand church the Venetians built to pray for (or give thanks for, depending on the version) an end to the plague.

  • We had dinner at a nice pizzaria overlooking the Canal Grande at the foot of the Ponte Accademia (Accademia Bridge); then we returned to our hotel for the evening.  After the charcoal overcoat of Rome, the sepia tones of Florence, and the gray stone of Milan, the vibrant colors of Venice were stunning, and so much more welcome than if we had taken these cities in reverse order.
