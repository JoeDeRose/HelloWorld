  • On the day of our departure from Venice I set out early to see Piazza San Marco one last time and take a last couple of pictures in the Serenissima (the "very serene one"), as Venice once called itself.  I came to Venice wanting to photograph every bridge in in the city; then it became every canal, then every street, then every gondola, then every doorway, then every window – then I had to give up.  Venice is the type of place that can drive you mad trying to reproduce it in colored beads or macaroni, just to have a sense of it still with you after you leave.

  • I went back to the hotel and the four of us then took the vaporetto to the Piazzale Roma to pick up our rental car, a Ford Mondeo station wagon (huge for the Italian roads, but just right for our group and luggage).  We drove across the Ponte della Libertà that connects Venice to the mainland; we were on our way to Ravenna.
  • Ravenna provided us with a nice diversion and a nice lunch.  I especially liked the way the old section was structured with a discreet (so discreet that we didn't understand it at first) bike lane of white bricks in the center of each street.

  • We made our way from Ravenna to San Marino, the world's smallest republic (Vatican City is a theocracy; Monaco is a kingdom).  After making an attempt to park near the "Centro" of the city of San Marino, the Sammarinese capital at the top of the mountain, we realized the folly of our hopes and returned halfway down to park and take the funivia (cable car) from Borgo Maggiore to the top.  San Marino is one of two independent nations completely surrounded by Italy (the other, of course, being Vatican City).

  • Near the top of the mountain, we decided we had seen as much of this picturesque country as we needed to, and started heading back down to the car without making our way fully to the Prima Torre.  Halfway back to the funivia station, I had a change of heart and asked Mom, Meridel, and Billy if they would mind waiting while I jogged up the hill to see it.  I'm so glad I changed my mind.

  • Finally after I returned from my exploration of the Prima Torre, we returned to the funivia and, in turn, the car, and drove westward toward Assisi through the Le Marche area of Italy.
  • One of the most amazing places we passed through was San Leo, high on a mountaintop with Gradara Fortress at the pinnacle against sheer vertical cliffs.  We didn't even know at the time what this structure was (we thought it might be a monastery), but learned after our return that it was a fortress, and indeed one that had impressed Dante Alighieri sufficiently that it was mentioned in The Divine Comedy.
Vassi in Sanleo e discendesi in Noli,
montasi su in Bismantova 'n Cacume
con esso i piè; ma qui convien ch'om voli;
dico con l'ale snelle e con le piume
del gran disio, di retro a quel condotto
che speranza mi dava e facea lume.
 
    
     San Leo's fortressed height
And Noli's steep descent a man may tread
With feet unaided, or the crest attain
Of Bismantova, but the peaks ahead
That faced us now, must very wings require,
- The wings, at least, of hot and swift desire
To follow closely where my Master led.
  • Driving through Le Marche, we found some of the curviest, steepest roads I have ever driven – or even seen.  Some hairpin turns were so abrupt that a mirror was mounted to look for oncoming traffic; other turns were so sharp that a roundabout was provided at the hairpin because the inside turn was too sharp without this assistance.  These are the roads that they use for car commercials:  From some vantage points the roadway looked like a seismograph reading.  Unfortunately, there was never a place we could safely stop to photograph it.
  • The approach to Assisi was gorgeous, and we reached the Hotel Ideale just in time to see the sun set behind the mountains, parking the car as instructed under the branches of an olive grove adjacent to the hotel.  Our rooms at the Ideale had balconies with glorious views of the town of Assisi; we left those balcony doors open all night and enjoyed the cooling Umbrian breeze.

  • Finally, we addressed ourselves to the task of having supper, and walked halfway down the hill on which Assisi is built, through tiny streets that were magical at dusk, to the Piazza Comune.  We tried to eat in one restaurant that was hopelessly understaffed, and waited there without menu or acknowledgement for half an hour before deciding to look elsewhere.  We found a great little trattoria around the corner where I was fortunate enough to order a locally made pasta with the most truffle-generous truffle sauce I have ever tasted.
