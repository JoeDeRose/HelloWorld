  • On our second full day in Venice, we started the day again with Mom and Meridel, again in Piazza San Marco – this time to see the Basilica San Marco.  After a long but fast-moving line, we entered the basilica and walked the slow progression through the sanctuary.  Venice is built on a foundation of upright tree trunks, and some references say that over a million trunks have been set under the basilica's foundation, the sturdiest around the perimeter as is typical; the difference in quality of wood from area to area was apparent as we shuffled our way across the floor, which ramped up and down noticeably as a result of decay.  (Pictures were forbidden inside the sanctuary.)

  • While inside the basilica we went in to see the Pala d'Oro, the ornate gold altarpiece speckled with precious gems.  It was not as big as I expected, but many times more ornate and beautiful than I had imagined.

  • Finally, on our way out of the basilica, we went up to the Galleria to see the Horses of St. Mark – both the originals, protected inside the basilica, and the replicas, facing the piazza.

  • Billy and I had set aside the rest of the day to explore the lagoon islands, especially Burano and Murano.  But our vaporetto only ran once an hour and we had just missed it, so we wasted time wandering nearby areas in the sestieri of San Marco and Castello, eventually making our way to the Arsenale.

  • In due course we arrived at Burano, which was a delight.  Burano is known for three things:  lace, its colorful houses, and its leaning bell tower.  We purchased a lace souvenir for Mom, and walked among the colorful houses looking for good views of the bell tower.  If Venice can be said to rise above the drab color scheme that one finds in other cities of north Italy, Burano actively rebels against it.

  • After exploring Burano, we hopped back on the vaporetto to go to Murano, the island known for glass manufacturing, the glass industry having been moved to this island from Venice to avoid soot and the risk of fire.  On the way, I gave up my seat to a woman who lives on Burano, and she chattered to Billy and me at length in Italian; I understood less than half of what she told me, but she and I both enjoyed talking.  (Periodically, she would turn to Billy and say, in Italian, "You don't understand a word I'm saying."  And he would smile and shrug amiably.)  She showed us an old fort that we passed and told me that the colorful house painting tradition on Burano started to help fishers find their way home after a long day fishing and a long night carousing.  We were a bit disappointed in Murano.  By this point in the trip, we had come to distrust on instinct any warnings we received about a place, as so few of them proved accurate – but this one was true:  The good glass is in Venice.

  • And to cap our tour of the lagoon islands, we stopped at San Michele, the cemetery island.  It wasn't nearly as morbid and creepy as I imagined (and, frankly, hoped), but the different styles of graves that represented different fashions and cultures in burial were interesting.

  • Finally, we returned to Venice proper on a motoscafo (which meant we had used each of the three types of boats in the vaporetto system), and allowed ourselves to get lost in the alleys of the sestiere of Cannaregio before making our way back to the hotel.  With this we had covered all six of Venice's sestieri.  To my eyes, there was little difference between them, but I was glad to have explored all six.

  • Most everything we were told about Venice was wrong.  It is not smelly; it is not more expensive than other Italian cities; it is not bereft of children and families; the Basilica is not so dim that it is difficult to see inside; and, as we learned this evening, it is not impossible to find a good, cheap meal near Piazza San Marco:  We had a great pasta dinner al fresco (I use this as an English term; al fresco in Italian is an idiom meaning "in prison") for less than € 10 at a restaurant just off the piazza.  Later we found the cheapest soft drinks we had yet seen in Italy (€ 0.83 for a can, € 1.80 for a 1.5 liter bottle).  If only we had discovered this shop before our last night in Venice!
  • We stopped in at an Internet café across the Ponte Rialto from our hotel.  Then, to cap the evening, Billy and I finally got to ride a traghetto – one of the gondola ferries that provides transportation across the Canal Grande for € 0.40 per ride – to return to the Bareteri for the night.
