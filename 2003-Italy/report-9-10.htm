  • I didn't sleep as well as I wanted on the flight from Atlanta to Brussels, and stayed awake of necessity for our confusing trip through the enormous Brussels airport.  (We eventually learned that we had to exit customs on the B terminal, and re-enter security on the A terminal to get to our Alitalia transfer.)  I did take time to get euros from an ATM (bancomat) in Brussels, which was a bit of an ordeal; each time I asked directions in French, I was met with, "I'm sorry, I don't understand; do you speak English."

Flag of Belgium

  
  • On the flight from Brussels to Rome, though, I went out like a light.  (I even slept through the takeoff.)  But I did wake up long enough to see the Alps as we passed over Switzerland!

  • We arrived in good order in Rome (or, technically, in Fiumicino) at Leonardo da Vinci Airport, got our bags, and walked through the almost non-existent customs barrier.

Rome

Flag of Italy

  
  • Then we took the Leonardo da Vinci Express train from the airport to Roma Termini, followed the endless progression of moving sidewalks to the main part of the station, and rode the subway (the "Met. Ro." for "Metropolitana Romana") one stop down to the Repubblica station, near our hotel.  The subway was efficient and popular, but the trains must be left parked overnight in an unsecured lot:  Their exteriors are covered ceiling to wheels in graffiti, so much so that it is occasionally difficult to tell where the doors are until the train stops and the doors open.  We very quickly adjusted to the Italian system of buying tickets in advance at tabacchi (tobacco shops) or newsstands before boarding, having them validated in machines at the entrance, and showing them (if requested) to transit system officials.  It rained a little on the train, but was dry by the time we arrived at Roma Termini station.  The weather stayed sunny and dry for the rest of the trip.

  • We were surprised to find that the Hotel Adler did not occupy its entire building, but rather was a tenant on one floor of an office building.  We later learned that this is a fairly common structure for Italian hotels.  We also were introduced to another quirk of Italian guesthouses:  that the room key is left at the desk whenever one departs, and retrieved when one returns.  The elevator was one of the traditional European kinds, with outer and inner doors that must both be opened manually.  The Adler had a great breakfast, and rooms that looked out over a quiet courtyard; the towels were made of the material that we tend to reserve in America for tablecloths, but we came to love the place over the course of our four days in Rome.  We stayed at hotels that we liked better, but none – I think – of which we were more fond.
  • After arrival, Billy and I immediately made for the Colosseo (Colosseum).  This was our first introduction to the layers of antiquity and ruins in Rome, and I think we may have been walking with our mouths open.  We came first to the Foro (Forum), and then to the Colosseum itself

  • For dinner our first night, we went out to a restaurant near the Adler, Ristorante Barocco; I ordered and devoured the Pizza Frutti di Mare (seafood pizza), prominent with calamari.
  • Billy and I really enjoyed just walking up and down the Via Nazionale (which was the route we had taken to the Colosseum), merely looking at the buildings and the ruins and the people and the shops.  We walked up the street at least three times that day, once at night where we enjoyed the night lighting on some of the ancient buildings.
