  • We started out in the morning on bus 64 with Mom and Meridel to Vatican City, arriving just off Piazza San Pietro (St. Peter's Square).  I have always been fascinated with the tiny states of Europe, and getting my chance to cross Bernini's colonnade into Piazza San Pietro and enter the tiniest of them this morning was one of the things I had anticipated with the most eagerness on this trip.  Bernini's colonnade unambiguously belongs to Vatican City, but the Piazza San Pietro is claimed by Rome.  Crossing the colonnade, I was struck that this may be the only place I will ever see where a good athlete with a running start can leap entirely across a nation.

  • Basilica San Pietro (St. Peter's Basilica), however, was a disappointment to me.  From the piazza, it looks like an office building.  And the interior, while gorgeous in its opulence, lacks the soaring majesty of the gothic cathedrals; it came across to me as an ornate gymnasium.

  • Billy and I split from Mom and Meridel early in the Basilica San Pietro, and made our way to Castel Sant'Angelo, adjacent to the Vatican.  This amazing structure – which has been used as a tomb for Hadrian and other emperors, a fortress, a refuge for popes, a prison, and now a museum – was the highlight of our day with its ramps and bridges and underground passageways; the view from the top was spectacular in every direction.

  • It was still early in the day, so Billy and I walked through one of Rome's nicer shopping districts to the Pantheon and the Trevi Fountain.  After having struggled to frame a good photograph of Bernini's Colonnade, and now finding it impossible to frame a good picture of the Pantheon, I realized why one so rarely sees good pictures of these famous buildings, yet finds them so attractive in drawings:  Their size and position simply make them all-but-impossible to photograph well.

Pantheon

  
             
Trevi Fountain
  • From the Trevi Fountain, we went toward Via Veneto, a park-like area of Rome, to see the Capuchin Crypt at Santa Maria della Concezione.  Here the resident Capuchin monks assembled, over the course of 300 years, art from the bones of their deceased brothers:  Skeletons hang from ceilings; bones are piled ceiling-high along the walls to form mausoleums; skulls decorate the doors and walkways.  Unfortunately, photographs are forbidden here.
  • At Via Veneto, we checked out the Piazza del Barberini (with the Fontana del Tritone) and saw – or, at least, noticed – our first of Rome's many drinking fountains:  the Fontana dei Api (Fountain of the Bees), so named because it is decorated with the bees that were a symbol of this area's influential Barberini family.
  • Then we made our way to the Spanish Steps and saw both the Piazza d'Espagna and the Fontana della Barcaccia.  Many of Rome's fountains, like Trevi and this one, are "mostre" – places where an aqueduct terminates.  The aqueduct that feeds the Fontana della Barcaccia, however, is of very low pressure, so instead of a grand fountain it is designed as a sinking boat.  At the Piazza d'Espagna, we also discovered an incredibly large and interesting McDonald's restaurant while searching for a restroom:  One enters through a long passage, in which archeological finds are evident in the walls; and the dining room is enormous in comparison to what we find familiar in the U.S.

  • In comparing notes with Mom and Meridel, I was ashamed to realize that I had forgotten the Sistine Chapel existed.  We never even tried to see it!  I hoped that there would be another opportunity before we were out of Rome.
  • At the end of this long day, we found our first Internet café and struggled to check our e-mail and various websites without knowing how to enter the "@" and "~", which are respectively obscure and absent on Italian keyboards.
