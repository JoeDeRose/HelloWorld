  • The Italian train system offered still more learning opportunities:  I mistook the big "2" – meaning "Second Class" – for the little "2" – meaning the carriage in which we our seats were assigned.  So we got on the wrong end of the train to Florence and spent the first half hour of the trip clambering over people and luggage as we made our way through eight carriages to our seats.  Still, the train took us quite comfortably on our journey from Roma Termini to Firenze Santa Maria Novella.
Train Ticket from Rome to Florence

Florence
  • We took a taxi to our hotel, the Pensione Centrale.  At first the enormous dark staircase leading to the hotel seemed grimy and foreboding, but when we learned that the building was a converted palace it took on a whole new aura.  The rooms were huge, the locks still used skeleton keys, some of the rooms still had their painted ceilings, and our room had a view of the Capelle Medici (Medici Chapels).  We thought the elevator was a little subpar:  It was tiny, and it only took us to the registration level, one floor below our room.  Little did we know it was the last one we would see until we were back in Rome.

Pensione Centrale Room Key

  
  • We started at the Duomo, Santa Maria del Fiore to be precise, the spectacular cathedral of Florence.  Experts will say that the most noteworthy part of the cathedral is Filippo Bruneleschi's dome, and that the façade is just a nineteenth-century revision.  The dome does make a great story:  The cathedral was left unfinished, Florentines betting that someday the technological advancement necessary to complete the structure would come (and we see in three dimensions that their faith in the future was well placed).  But it is the façade that brings this building to life.  We then looked at the Duomo's interior (surprisingly drab for having such a beautiful skin) and at Bruneleschi's tomb in the crypt.

Florence Duomo

       

Florence Duomo, Baptistry, and Campanile

  
  • After the Duomo, we wandered through the pedestrian area of Florence, seeing places such as the Piazza della Repubblica (ringed with souvenir stands), the Mercato Nuovo (covered with souvenir stands), and, especially, the Ponte Vecchio (Old Bridge), Florence's most famous bridge (lined with souvenir stands).  In addition to the souvenir stands, the Ponte Vecchio is lined with jewelry shops and capped with a portion of the Vasari Corridor, the overhead walkway that the Medicis used to get between the Uffizi and the Pitti Palace.  The Ponte Vecchio was the only Florentine bridge to have been spared by the Germans when they retreated from Florence near the end of World War II.  I have always thought that the Ponte Vecchio was singularly unattractive in pictures, but in person, in context, I can attest that it is a thing of beauty.

  • After the Ponte Vecchio, Billy and I wandered around the Arno River and Altrarno (the neighborhood across the river); we also saw the Pitti Palace, which was so ugly I didn't even photograph it.

  • In walking around Florence, we had our introduction to a curiosity of Italian food shops wherein one browses the merchandise, goes to a clerk to describe the desired item, pays, gets a receipt, and then returns to the display case with the receipt to pick up the item from a different clerk.  It would be a bit cumbersome even without a language barrier, and I wondered what problems it solved that it was worth the confusion to outsiders.
  • Our day was made spectacular by the discovery that there was an Internet café adjacent to the hotel where one could buy cheap soft drinks and American candy bars.
