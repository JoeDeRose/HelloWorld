Frequently Asked Questions

How did it go?

If the trip was not perfect, it was at least ideal:  We all enjoyed the sights and each other's company.  The time spent in each city allowed us to see our full itinerary without rushing and without getting bored.  We enjoyed beautiful weather and delicious food.  And trip plans went without a hitch.

Mom and I had been planning a trip to Italy for years, but had eventually decided that we would get on each other's nerves with our different paces and itineraries.  It was for this reason that Mom invited Meridel, and I invited Billy.  The four of us always traveled between cities together, and would typically start the day in the same place.  But then we would split up as our divergent interests led us – and would meet up later, often for supper, to sit and discuss the things we had seen.

Were things bad/difficult/confusing/disorganized in Italy?

None of the caveats that were issued regarding Italy seemed accurate to me.  Among the most common allegations that my visit failed to support were:

  • Schedules will be disrupted and reservations will be lost:  Everything was handled efficiently, exactly on schedule.  Never was a reservation lost, and never was a room or car or whatever not ready as soon as we arrived.  I think I could set my watch by the Italian trains.  Even my SkyMiles incentives for the rental cars were deposited promptly after our return – which is a lot better than my previous experience (in other places) had led me to anticipate.
  • Pickpockets are rampant:  No one made any move (that we could discern at least) to deceive us or steal from us.  While there were some beggars, no "gypsy children" (or anyone) reached in our pockets – in fact, the beggars were all adults or (very occasionally) teenagers.
  • Shopkeepers will deliberately shortchange you:  We were never, so far as I know, shortchanged.  And, indeed, I miscalculated on a couple of occasions the change to expect and was surprised to receive more than I anticipated.  (I wonder if this prejudice about Italian shopkeepers comes from American impatience intersecting with the Italian custom of assembling the change, bit by bit, in a change tray – and then signaling that the transaction is concluded by placing the receipt, which is required by law, in that change tray – while in America the receipt is often omitted and change is counted out into the clerk's hand and presented all at once.)
  • Driving is chaotic:  Driving was orderly and predictable.  Italian drivers ran the same gamut (in the same general proportion) that we find in America, from aggressive and fast to timid and slow.  I continue to think that one of America's greatest contributions to civilization is the quality of our road system and the signs that provide direction on it; Italy was not as easy as the U.S. (and I think I would have the same impression even without a lifetime of experience on U.S. roadways), but I found Italy about as easy to navigate as the United Kingdom, and a lot easier than Ireland.  And Italy did a great job with providing direction within cities – once we got accustomed to the type of signs to look for.
  • Crosswalks are frightening:  Crossing the streets in Rome and other large Italian cities was not a matter of skill and courage, but was governed by crossing signals and pedestrian right-of-ways that were largely obeyed by people on the sidewalk and by vehicles in the street.  I felt safer crossing the road there than I do at home.
How did you get around?

Mostly by train, but we rented a car in Florence to drive around Tuscany, and we rented another car to drive from Venice to Sorrento.  In the course of the trip, though, we used a surprising number of different forms of transportation, notably:

  • airplane (Atlanta-Brussels-Rome, Rome-Brussels-Atlanta),
  • train (Rome-Florence, Florence-Milan, Milan-Venice, Naples-Rome),
  • narrow gauge railway (Circumvesuviana) (Naples-Sorrento, Sorrento-Naples),
  • subway (Atlanta, Rome, Milan, Naples),
  • people mover (automated subway) (Atlanta airport),
  • tram (Milan),
  • funicolare (cable railway) (Naples, Capri),
  • bus (Rome, Capri),
  • motorbike (Rome),
  • private car (Tuscany, Venice-Assisi-Sorrento),
  • taxi (Rome, Florence, Milan, Assisi, Sorrento),
  • ferry (Capri-Naples),
  • hydrofoil (Sorrento-Capri),
  • vaporetto (Venice),
  • motoscafo (Venice),
  • motonave (Venice),
  • motorboat (Capri),
  • rowboat (Capri),
  • gondola (traghetto) (Venice),
  • funivia (cable car) (San Marino),
  • seggiovia (chair lift) (Capri),
  • moving sidewalk (Brussels, Rome, Naples),
  • elevator,
  • escalator, and, of course,
  • our own feet.

Do you think you took enough pictures?

Actually, no.

First, there are a lot of things I couldn't photograph because I simply didn't have the camera with me.

Then there were the things that I either chose not to photograph or didn't think to photograph, that I now wish I had documented if for no other reason than to facilitate the storytelling of the trip.  Notable among these omissions are the graffiti-covered subway trains of Rome, the enormous closed door of the Vatican Museums when I tried to visit the Sistine Chapel after closing, the "Treni in Partenza" boards at the stations, the second-class sign that confused me on the train ride, the cramped passageways through the domes of the cathedrals, and some of the "No Photographs" signs that we saw in a lot of the most interesting places.

And of course there were those things that I was forbidden to photograph.  If only I could show you the ornate tombs inside the Catacombs of San Sebastiano or the skulls and skeletons in the Capuchin Crypt or the Pala d'Oro at the Basilica San Marco!

But I did take a lot of pictures, 329 to be exact.  That's 200 MB of memory (at 1600×1200 with fine resolution).  And in doing so, I went though six sets of camera batteries.

What was your favorite place?

Wouldn't you rather know which is my favorite eye?

I would have been impoverished by missing any of the places we saw, but I guess I have to give the nod to Venice.  I've always dreamed of seeing Venice, and it met my expectations – and more.

What was the best meal?

Cuttlefish in squid ink sauce at a restaurant in Venice.  And a close second was the Pavese soup I had at the Ristorante La Minervetta in Sorrento.

Is there anything you regret not seeing?

There's nothing I regret omitting from our plans, but there's a lot more I wish I could have seen.  On a return trip to Italy, I would definitely want to explore the Dolomites and Calabria and Sicily and the Cinque Terre.  In Florence, I wish we had arranged to see the Vasari Corridor.  It would have been wonderful if time had permitted us to see the Paestum and Ercolano (Herculaneum).  On the Amalfi Coast, I wish we had just stopped to enjoy it instead of racing to Sorrento to return the car on time.  And I wish our visit to Naples had not coincided with the day that the Archeological Museum is closed.

How long did it take you to prepare this report?

Longer than the trip.  But in my defense, I was also holding down a job, caring for my dog, and cleaning my house during that same period – tasks which others were handling in my absence during the trip.

What did it cost?

For Billy and me together, it was $4,624.32, over $1,000 more than I had planned – and that didn't include airfare (for which we used SkyMiles), the long-distance charges for our reservations, my dog-sitting payment, or picture developing.  We were hurt by a bad dollar-to-euro exchange rate and by my optimistic assumptions about the cost of food, but it was worth every penny.  I would have spent more, and I would do it again.
