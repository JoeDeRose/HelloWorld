Aktun Tunichil Muknal

Every moment of every day is an opportunity for learning.  And one of the many things  I learned on this day was:  When you get off the beaten path, the path beats back.  This was the day we reported for a much-anticipated event  the Aktun Tunichil Muknal cave hike, known in short as "ATM.".

First let me say that spelunking is not going to be my sport:  This is an activity that I am thrilled to have done.  But it was not an activity that I was thrilled to be doing.  That said, ATM was spectacular.  It had been known to local farmers and the chicle harvesters that frequented a certain sapodilla tree for some time, but was only revealed in 1989 to archaeologists.  Nine years later a tour policy was established that now grants two tour agencies permission to lead in visitors under very specific restrictions.  It is almost universally believed that the Belize Department of Archaeology will soon clap a gate on the entrance to this cave, as they have done on others, allowing entry only to archaeologists researching the site.  We are lucky that our trip coincided with the window in which the general public is allowed to see these astonishing relics.

It's not easy to get to, though:  Our tour started at Eva's Restaurant, from which we departed on the first leg of the trip in:  an hour's drive with with ten other visitors and two guides.  Five of us were with Francisco, and seven others were with Patrick  but until we were in the cave we pretty much stayed together as one group.

Francisco, our guide

Our van crossed rivers a few times on bridges, and twice without the benefit of bridges.  The first of these through-the-river crossings was what I think was the Eastern Branch Macal River, and was on a submerged concrete dam (of a type that I have also seen occasionally for crossings in the U.S., albeit in places where the water flow was much lower than here).  The second crossing may have been over Roaring Creek, with which we would become intimately familiar over the long day, and was completely undeveloped:  The van just plunged itself slowly into the current and nudged its way across.  We stopped for approval at the ranger's station (which was also his home).  Then we parked and hiked forty-five minutes through the jungle, trudging across Roaring Creek three times as we followed the path that had been preserved from among the old Maya routes.  Patrick led the way, using his machete to hack away any plants that had grown across the path in the last day.  (Which led me to wonder how much traffic these old Maya paths get, that they are still in existence though they require this much effort to maintain.)  Francisco kept the rear, hacking more enthusiastically to facilitate travels for others coming behind us and for us on the return.  As hard as this trek was for me, I have to give proper recognition to Patrick and Francisco.  Not only did they do the same thing we were doing  but they did it while hauling heavy packs that included their supplies, emergency items, and some of our supplies.  That latter fact, by the way, explains the paucity of pictures on this page:  Because of the risk of damage, our cameras were locked in "wet bags" carried by the guides, to protect them as were were making our way across, and up, the creek.  At important junctures, Francisco would retrieve the cameras for us  but this was too involved a process for him to do it at every interesting thing along the way.

On the hike, our guides introduced us a bit to the local flora and fauna:  On thing was saw as was an enormous termite nest on the side of a nearby tree at chest level.  We were told that the termites were edible, but didn't get a sample; I know that some tours actually let hikers taste the termites  but, unfortunately, either the practice has stopped or our guides skipped that event.  We were warned against straying from the path or touching any plants, because "some of them make poison ivy seem like a rose."  A highlight was collecting a couple of cohune nuts just before one river crossing, and having Francisco smash them on a rock at the other side of the river, allowing us to eat the coconut-like interior.  Patrick also showed us a cacao tree ("we send you this seed, and you send us back little Kisses with almonds in them") and the sapodilla tree, with its diagonal slashes, that had been responsible for the rediscovery of this cave.

At the end of the jungle portion of the hike, we enjoyed a tremendously generous lunch that we had hauled in with us, and availed ourselves of the last opportunity to use a toilet without carrying out our produce in a baggy.  (They're really serious about protecting this cave.)  Unfortunately, the toilet was a wooden plank with a square hole carved in it, completely open to the view of others who might come looking for it in the woods.  Fortunately, I did not find that I required its services.

Finally we donned our helmets and miner's lights and plunged ourselves into the cave from which Roaring Creek spills.  The Maya know their underworld as Xibalba, and their ancestors clearly had a great reverence for this cave, considering the ceremonial and sacrificial items that have been found in it.

Our hike inside the cave involved two hours of immersion in chilly water, sometimes to our knees, other times over our heads, that took us about a mile into the underworld.  We slogged on, wearing just our bathing suits and our shoes, fighting our way through strong currents, hauling ourselves up jagged waterfalls, and ducking under low overhangs, all with no more illumination than our LED miner's lights could afford (which, in fairness, was actually decent illumination).  In case you have not had a chance for empirical research in this arena, I can report that swimming in shoes is more difficult than it would seem  but the shoes were a blessing against the rocks at the bottom that we pushed ourselves against for most of the route.  We were told that we might see cave spiders as big as our outspread hands, and that we should not fear them, even though they did bite and were poisonous (but not lethal), because they were blind and would not jump at us.  I tried not to fear them  a task that was made easier by the fact that we did not encounter them.

To get to the dry section where the artifacts lay we climbed a rock and squirmed across and over an eight-foot drop to an overhanging ledge. This was just as horrifying as it sounds, more so because of the distance and effort that separated us from resources for medical care.  Unfortunately, for this portion of the route our cameras were locked in the wet bag, so I cannot show you how unnerving it was.

Here we removed our waterlogged shoes, as  to preserve the relics  the dry chambers may be explored only in sock feet.  Here I can report further that, while swimming in shoes may be uncomfortable, walking in a cave without them is even more so.  Anyway, this is how it must be done, so this is how we did it.  Shoeless, we climbed up a steep incline which elevated us another ten feet or so, and then were given our education on how to walk in the cave and why:  The cave has not been disturbed and never will be, so they will not investigate any pools of water to see what they contain.  But they assume that some of the shallow pools must contain pottery fragments of some sort; thus, to ensure that these are not ruined by tourism, we were not to place our feet in the water at any time.  We could only step in places where it was visually obvious that we weren't stepping on anything historic; we were to move slowly, so we didn't lose our balance and fall into a pot or a skeleton; we were to walk on the natural dams that the sedimentary process has left adjacent to the pools; we were to follow our guide's instructions without fail as to where and when we may step.  I came to believe that Francisco was prepared to kill me on the spot if it appeared I was about to blunder into an artifact.

There were inevitable missteps, but we were all careful and nothing was broken.  This owed mostly to our obedience and intelligence, but some luck came into play as well, and I found myself thinking that limiting visits to archaeologists might actually not be such a bad idea.  Francisco showed us some examples of pots broken by tour groups, and I was thankful by the end that we hadn't been responsible for any such desecration.  Anyway, with our education in place, we were ready for the heart of the visit.

Now my experience of going in caves is extremely limited  off the top of my head I can name only three:  Ruby Falls, the Lost Sea, and Carlsbad Caverns (and all of those can be visited by people in wheelchairs!).  I knew that this would be a different experience, but one thing took me by surprise:  One thing in common about those other caves I've visited is that the stalactites, stalagmites, columns, and formations are the "money shots" of the cave, and are vigorously protected.  They are also, in many cases, decorated with lights and names that lean substantially toward cheesy.  This cave was not about all that.  We pulled ourselves along by stalactites when necessary, and were rarely asked not to touch a natural formation.  Only once did Francisco shine his flashlight at a cave feature and say "nativity scene"  and on that occasion it really did look like a nativity scene (I thought it even before he said it).  No, the reason were were in this cave was archaeology, not geology  but that is not to say the natural features were anything less than stunning:  Some of the petrified flows along walls in the cave were absolutely breathtaking, more amazing than any I had seen before.  And the water seepage was not the dripping from a stalactite that one ordinarily sees; rather, it was in some places a torrent spilling from overhead with about the force of a typical household shower.

But, again, we were there for the Maya, not for Xibalba.  So first we were led to an area where some pottery fragments lay on the floor of the cave.  Not knowing what was ahead, we marveled at these; any museum would make them a centerpiece, but they paled against what came later.

The first pottery fragments we saw

Then Franscisco showed us a tableau that is, indeed, one of the major finds in the cave.  It is believed to be an altar of sorts  arranged ceremonially.  Specifically, the archaeologists believe that the arrangement of the pots is highly significant.  Little of this was explained in detail, and much of it would go over my head  but, in part, the upright pots were to evoke "filling" (which they sometimes did under the drip of stalactites), the tilted ones were to evoke "spilling," and the upturned ones were to evoke "emptying."  Another example was a formation that appeared much like the Maya goddess Ix Chel; the archaeologists are uncertain whether this is a purely natural formation or one that has been modified artificially  but they aver that the Maya considered this spot sacred because of that particular formation.

The tableau of pottery
    

The Ix Chel formation:  her nose and chin are to the right; her ceremonial headpiece is to the upper-left

We saw fire pits where the charcoal blocks they used were still visible after centuries.  And Francisco told us that this was the place where the Maya royalty would come to burn their incense (a corn incense that had been shared with us at a few other places), take their hallucinogenic drugs, and collect their blood in sacrifice to the gods (predominantly by piercing their tongues and genitals and by putting wooden splints under their fingernails).

Also, Francisco told us about the ritual destruction of the pottery:  As part of the rites, pots would be rendered unusable  so one will never find a purely intact pot or urn.  Four mechanisms for destruction were used:  shattering, breaking (removing a piece), chipping (removing a layer from the surface), and drilling.

An example of a drill hole in a pot.

Further in, we were shown the "monkey pot."  The monkey motif is quite rare, and poorly understood.  But the fact that such a pot was found in this cave is of great significance.

The Monkey Pot
    

A closer view of the Monkey Pot:  The monkey can barely be discerned in the front center.  The ceremonial breaking can be seen at the top.
    

The monkey image

Then we came to the first of the skeletal remains.  The archaeologists have done a superb job (assuming they are accurate) in deducing information about these remains without lifting them or removing them for study in a better environment  their ages, their status in Maya society, and whether they were ritually sacrificed by their own tribe (which was usually an honor) or by a warring faction.

The first skull we saw; known to be of the dominant tribe because his forehead slopes only in the front; thus, was probably sacrificed as an honor

The first part of the dry area has not been so bad to walk on because of the flat sedimentary flow.  But as we went back further, the rocks became rougher and the passages became tighter.  At one point we had to climb a ladder (the only modern artificial item we saw in our entire walk) up to a higher level.  (I am at pains  literally  to describe which is more uncomfortable:  ladders in socks, or swimming in shoes.)  This was worth the misery, though:  At the top of the ladder, and back a short distance across a small opening was the woman's skeleton lying in the chamber  the "stone sepulcher"  from which the cave takes its name.

"stone sepulcher"

At this point, all we had to do was the return:  We made our way back down the ladder, back past the skeletons, back past the pottery, back to our shoes.  Our cameras were locked in the wet bags.  Our shoes were back on our feet.  We slid down the incline to the precipice (over the frightening eight-foot drop) and strained to reach the pointy rock below with our toes.  All of our group made it without injury and we plunged ourselves back into Roaring Creek to follow its currents out of Xibalba.

Getting out was much easier than getting in, mostly due to the fact that the current was helping us rather than working against us.  In fact, the only times we had any trouble at all were when we had to clamber down waterfalls without going so fast that we might hurt ourselves.

We emerged from the cave to the picnic area near the sapodilla tree.  Francisco offered to get out our cameras if we would like to take pictures on the route back.  Having more familiarity with the river crossings, I felt certain I could keep my camera dry  but no one else was interested and Francisco remembered that my camera was at the bottom of the bag and recommended not worrying about it.  He had been so generous in carrying our supplies as well as his own that I could hardly complain.

Illness II

It was probably just as well, anyway.  I was showing early signs of some illness that manifested itself as  how to put this  discomfort and stupidity.  At first I thought I was merely dehydrated, as I noticed I had only drunk half of the water I had brought in.  This may have entered into it, but I feel certain there was more:  Perhaps, there was some bacterium in the water.  Perhaps I had brushed against the wrong plant or been bitten or stung by the wrong insect.  Or perhaps it was not nearly so exotic; perhaps the wrong person had just sneezed above my food.  In any event, I was mightily tired walking back  not such that I slowed us down, but enough that I really needed to stop when we got back to the van.  There, after changing my clothes, I almost forgot my camera as I hung it on on a limb by a screen set up for changing clothes; this near-disaster was averted by Billy noticing the camera and bringing it to me.  I tried to sleep on the van ride back, but the "roads" made this difficult.  And when finally back at the Parrot Nest, I kept craving things that didn't seem to help much:  a shower, then food, then a chance to lay down.  With a splitting headache, I thought I was taking two ibuprofen, but I realized the next morning I had taken two allergy pills instead.  Finally, I excused myself from dinner (reluctantly, as this was our only dinner at Parrot Nest, and dessert hadn't even been served), and went to lay in my bed.  In tropical Belize, I wore long pants, a long-sleeve shirt, and covered myself with two blankets, and literally shivered myself to sleep.

In the night, though, my body righted itself and we were able to continue with only a little more rest than I ordinarily require.  It could have been a lot worse.
