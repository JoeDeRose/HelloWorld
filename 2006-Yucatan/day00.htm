Ground Rush

Everyone who knows me is aware that skydiving in 1996 was among my seminal experiences because it was a really cool, once-in-a-lifetime (I hope) experience.  But scholars of my life (wherever they may be hiding) will note that it was also important because it introduced me to "ground rush," both as a concept and as a vocabulary term.  Skydivers enjoy the free fall – but to enjoy the sport a second time you have to keep focused on your altitude:  For a long time, as the seconds go by like hours, the ground seems (and is) miles away.  But you have to pull the chute while the ground seems like it is still tremendously distant, because if you wait until the ground seems close, you will – to put it gently – overshoot your target.  In many arenas – not only skydiving – the transition from comfortably remote to alarmingly imminent is instantaneous.  "Ground rush" describes the realization that one has traversed the barrier.

I begin this report nine days before departure, as I realize the ground rush has begun.  It was all so far away until a couple of weeks ago, and now it feels like tomorrow.  And all of the obsessive advance planning I've done for months suddenly seems woefully inadequate:

  • The Hotel:  Our first three nights are at the Rancho Sak-Ok in Puerto Morelos.  Rancho was hit hard by hurricane Wilma and is still rebuilding.  Just a few days ago, I got an e-mail from them saying that power was intermittent, the big palapa was still being rebuilt, and they are still removing the wreckage of a ship from the beach in front of the hotel.  They offered to let us cancel without penalty, but I curried the situation into a two-for-one room deal; whether or not I am an idiot for keeping the reservation remains to be seen.
  • The Car:  All seems to be in order with the Fox car rental.  Their website still indicates that they have no restrictions on driving area (making them a singular exception to the rule, and our only lifeline to a travel itinerary that takes us across two national borders).  Insurance plagues me still:  I can (and will) waive CDW and have my American Express card cover it for the whole trip.  I can purchase liability, which will cover me in Mexico.  At the Belize border I can purchase liability that will cover me in Belize.  But at the Guatemala border I cannot purchase insurance and have ascertained that my personal liability does not cover me – so in Guatemala I'll be flying without a net.
  • The Country:  Spanish is my best foreign language.  If I can't reasonably conduct simple tourist business in Spanish, I'm a failure as a linguist.  But throughout the planning process I've had to ask (repeatedly!), «Lo siento, pero yo no puedo entenderle. ¿Habla Ud. Inglés?»  This does not bode well.

I've pulled the ripcord.  Now all I can do is wait to see how far away the ground is.
