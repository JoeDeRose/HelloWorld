Vallalodid

Valente was pleased, I think, that we had taken his advice and modified our plans to see Ek Balam:  Over breakfast he showed us a book with some images of the portal and an overview of the site so we would know the most interesting places to look.

We weren't in a rush, for once, as Ek Balam was not too far away  so around mid-morning we started northward toward Cancún, and then westward on the toll road.  I had read that the toll roads are a blessing in Mexico, as they are of high quality and blessedly free of speed bumps.  But I found it a bit boring.  I was missing the Maya villages.  I was also missing the 180 pesos they charged me to drive on the thing.

Estado de Yucatán
State of Yucatán
 
 

Our trip this day took us from the state of Quintana Roo to the state of Yucatán, and one interesting thing we found in passing between the states was that they had a customs station at the border  with a red light/green light system just as at the airport.  We weren't selected for a search, but it was interesting that there was even the possibility.

Eventually, we came to our exit  which was the first exit in almost an hour-and-a-half.  Ek Balam was to the right, but we first went left to Vallalodid, one of the Spanish colonial cities and the third largest city in the peninsula.  I liked Vallalodid well enough, but I found myself glad that we hadn't planned to spend much time in the colonial cities; they were nice, but the Maya areas were breathtaking and unique.  Anyway, Billy found a nice restaurant for us on the square in Vallalodid, and we had lunch there.  I had Conchinita Pibil, a Yucateca dish of pork cooked in banana leaves.

After our meal, we went north to Ek Balam.  First we stumbled into the Maya village of the same name (which, while friendly enough, was clearly not a tourist destination).  Then we found the ruins.

Even before we came to the portal, El Balam was something special.  Mind you, our threshold of what constituted an amazing Maya carving was going to skyrocket  within the hour  but even the "ordinary" carvings at Ek Balam were among the best preserved and most detailed we had seen.  And while we aren't experts, it was clear even to the casual observer that the ornamentation on the buildings at Ek Balam was more intricate than in many other sites.

Unlike in most places we had seen, the main structure at Ek Balam was not a pyramid, but rather an elongated acropolis.  It is, in fact, the most massive structure yet unearthed in the Maya world  150 meters long by 75 meters across, and 30 meters high (as tall as El Castillo at Chichén Itzá).

You've probably already glanced down at the pictures  and even if not, I don't know how I can begin to set the stage.  Just know that the top-left picture in the previous set was not a typical example of what we had seen before.  Rather, it was an exquisite example.  We had spend the previous seven days looking at stelae and carvings, and considering ourselves generously rewarded if we could simply make our a portion of an image here or there on the otherwise weathered stone.  The one richly detailed example we had seen (in Xunantunich) was was actually a fiberglass overlay.

Valente had shared with us his views on reconstruction at the ancient sites:  He felt that it might be okay to replace a stone here or there, if you see that stone on the ground and a void of the identical shape just above it.  But a reconstruction requires imagination and assumption, and he felt that such work, no matter how lovingly it may have been done, inevitably detracts from one's understanding of the place and its history.  These opinions had come to guide how we felt about the places and things were were seeing, and we craved the authentic as we enhanced our discovery of the Maya world.  And here we found not just an astonishing site, but a genuine one:  Valente had assured us that the portal at Ek Balam was not a reconstruction  that it was an authentic archaeological work, visible today as the Maya had left it and as the ages had preserved it.

Satisfied beyond our wildest dreams, we left Ek Balam, walking out past the Oval Palace.

After Ek Balam, we still had some time.  So we checked out a well-known cenote in Dzitnup, near Vallalodid:  Cenote X-Kekén.  This particular cenote is considered one of the best by cenote connoisseurs because it is illuminated by a natural skylight at the top of the cave.

I stupidly took the free road back to the east.  I wanted to have a more interesting transit, and to avoid the tolls, so I thought I would be clever.  But the sun set on us (so the road was no more interesting than the toll road) and there were many villages with many, many speed bumps (some of which were unmarked and took us by surprise).  And to make matters worse, the free road dumped us north of Cancún, so we had to figure our way back to the south, throwing off our schedule and rattling my nerves.

But we made it back, finally, to Puerto Morelos.  We considered eating dinner at a different restaurant; one looked promising, with a notice (in English, ugh) that it served the "best ceviche in Puerto Morelos."  But Billy wanted to eat again at Pelicanos, and frankly so did I.  So we returned there and I had pulpo en su tinta (octopus in its ink).  I can't remember the name of the restaurant we skipped  but it came up in conversation at a gathering of some sort after our return:  A fellow said, "Oh yes, _______  you should have eaten there.  That's where all the Americans can be found."  We made the right choice.
