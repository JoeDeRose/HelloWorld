Frequently Asked Questions

Why did it take you twice as long to finish this report as it took to go on the vacation?

(I'm putting this one first, as it is the most frequently asked of the frequently asked questions.)

It takes time!  Look at this thing – it's a freakin' masterpiece!  All right, in truth there are several discrete answers to this question:

  • I've got a lot of other stuff to do.  At work I came back to some major projects that occupied time beyond the standard eight-hour day:  I had a Friday-evening-and-Saturday meeting on the heels of my arrival and I was in Washington for three days on a site visit the next week.  At home I had all the routine chores, a lot of new laundry to clean, TV to watch, friends to socialize with, and pets to entertain.
  • There's a lot of ex post facto research that goes into these reports.  I don't just slap up the pictures and some quick captions; I look in depth to understand some of the things we've seen and the histories of the places we've gone.
  • I'm delighted to share this report with you – but the bottom line is:  It's for me, not for others.  You're going to look through this thing once.  Maybe you'll come again if you're planning a trip to the same area.  About half of you will read the text; the rest will just glance at the pictures.  But I'm going to read this thing over and over, reliving this amazing adventure each time I open it.  I do this with all of my trip reports.  And so I want them to be a thorough record of the experience.

How was the weather?

Perfect!  Billy and I both hate the cold, so a visit to a 27°C/80°F paradise was fabulous.  The people who lived there felt chilly in the night, but we were comfortable in short sleeves for the entire ten days.

It rained a lot while we were there, but never on us and never in a way that inconvenienced us.  It rained almost every night, and it rained a lot while we were driving, but never when we were ready to play.  Really it was like that verse about Camelot:  "The rain may never fall till after sundown. By eight, the morning fog must disappear."

What's more bizarre is that the rain seemed to be a big problem for people around us.  The hotel owner in Belize worried that the straw roofs were saturated and would start to leak – but they didn't.  And other guests at the hotel were unable to do the Actun Tunichil Muknal tour one day because of high water, but we did it the next day with no problem.

How many times did your passport get stamped?

A lot!  I've used this passport for a grand total of 42 international border crossings, into and out of 17 nations (if you count Seborga – 44 crossings and 18 nations).

Of those, 22 crossings actually involved some kind of scrutiny (the rest were areas of the European Community in which there is no border control).

Those crossings have resulted in 17 stamps in my passport, including double stamps in Belize and Guatemala for import and export of the rental car.  (Unlike some tourists, I've never asked for a stamp when they didn't give one as a matter of course.)

Of those stamps, 12 (71%) were made during this trip.

Why do you switch between metric and English measurements in the trip report?

Mexico and Guatemala use the metric system; Belize uses the English system.  Throughout the report, I deferred to local custom as to which system of measurements to use.

What are those angle-y things?

In Spanish, angle quotes (« and ») are used instead of quotation marks.  When I am showing a Spanish word or phrase in quotation marks, therefore, I use the angle quotes; when I am showing an English word, I use the standard English quotation marks.  I do this because I am mildly pretentious.

I'm scared to include this section, as I will feel horrible if I forget anyone.  But this trip was as wonderful as it was because of the tremendous generosity that various friends and family showed.  To that end, thanks to:
