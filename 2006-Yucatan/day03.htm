Xcaret

I must confess that I had misgivings about Xcaret.  I had described it, somewhat derisively, as "Maya Disney."  And I approached it with the assumption that we would run out of things to do long before we ran out of time.  Thus, Billy and I slept late, dawdled at the start of our day, and kept in our minds other activities nearby that we could turn to if we got tired of Xcaret.  As it turns out, this preconception was utterly, entirely misguided:  Xcaret was spectacular, and we barely had time for half the things we wanted to see in it.  This eco-park makes a genuine effort at historical accuracy, but its efforts shine through a filter of artistic beauty.  My inner archaeologist may have left wanting a bit of clarification, but my inner aesthete was unequivocally thrilled.

Map of Xcaret
 

The wristband that allowed us to explore Xcaret
 
Xcaret made a nice first impression with a group of colorful birds at the entrance.  One of the park's primary functions is as a conservator of wildlife, and it was, indeed, amazing to see some of the animals at Xcaret  but here my misgivings about the place have not entirely subsided:  Xcaret enthusiastically advertises a "swim with dolphins" activity, yet I have read numerous reports that such programs universally harm the dolphins.  I suspect that Xcaret is one of the best  but it may be that such a program should not exist at all.  In any event, we made a conscious decision not to participate.  Other animals we saw were vividly presented, but often at the expense of what appeared to be a satisfactory amount of space.  The jaguar and pumas, for example, paced restlessly on their respective "islands" (surrounded by crocodiles, not water) on plots of land that were about the size of a couple of large suburban lots.  Xcaret is working with an environmental organization called Flora, Fauna y Cultura de México, which devotes itself to conservation issues, so one hopes that animal care is an area in which improvement is a base philosophy and an ongoing commitment.

It bears note that Xcaret will not allow normal sunscreen to be used on the premises, requiring visitors to apply a biodegradable version that will not harm the ecosystem.  Knowing and respecting this requirement, Billy and I had refrained from purchasing sunscreen until we could obtain the safe variety at Xcaret  which we did promptly after entering the park, as our arms were already reddened from the Cobá activities yesterday.

Parrots and flamingoes at the entrance to Xcaret

Billy and I arrived just before the 11:30 AM demonstration of the Voladores de Papantla, so we made it our first course of business to view this incredible demonstration.  To the accompaniment of a flute (symbolizing the songs of birds) and drum (symbolizing the voice of god), five men climb a thirty-meter pole  without a net or carabiners or any sort of protective device.  At the top, four of the men sit at the cardinal points while the fifth sits at the top, continuing the melody of flute and drum.  The four slowly wind up their ropes, tie the ends around their waists and legs, participate in a blessing for their endeavor, and release themselves to hang upside down as the ropes unwind  thus lowering them in a spiral down to the earth.  The ritual originated with the Totonacan people of the Veracruz area, and is highly symbolic:  The blessings begin facing the west, as this is the direction of the origin of life.  The fliers circle the pole thirteen times on their way down, a sacred number to the Maya.  And multiplying their number (4) by the number of revolutions (13) results in fifty-two total revolutions  the number of years in the Maya Calendar Round, after which a new sun is born and all life renews.  The only distraction from this beautiful event was a woman behind me who kept commenting to her children and family, "Why would they do this?  They must have been really bored one day!  They must have been crazy!  What kind of person would do this?" and on and on with that sort of "analysis."  Fortunately, most of the spectators seems to understand what a great ritual they were witnessing.  And I think she did eventually shut up.

After the Papantla ritual, the voladores demonstrated the Dance of the Huahuas  another ritual, this one performed vertically on a free-wheeling cross-shaped device which they mount and set to spinning.

    

Dance of the Huahuas
    

After the Voladores, Billy and I went to an on-site restaurant called La Cocina.  As amusement park food goes, this was pretty good.  We had a sampler plate that included some ordinary dishes (chicken and empanadas) and some that we hadn't tasted before (cactus and pok chuc).

One of the exciting activities of Xcaret is the Underground Rivers, and this turned out to be pretty cool indeed.  We rented fins and snorkeling gear (optional, but highly recommended by them and much appreciated by us), turned over our dry clothes to the custodians to be locked in a bag with a key that I kept around my neck, picked up our life preserver (mandatory), and plunged ourselves into the river.  There seem to be a few routes that all go to basically the same place; they wind around, occasionally surfacing near some major building in the park.  Sometimes they are pitch black; sometimes they are bathed in natural or artificial light.  Sometimes they are deep; sometimes shallow.  Sometimes the bottom has interesting features to see through the snorkel; sometimes it's just boring rock.  Anyway, we were in there for a while (more than a kilometer) but eventually arrived at the end, to which our dry bag had been trucked to meet us.  We thoroughly enjoyed the experience, but were left with a nagging question:  How did these rivers get here?  They didn't look artificial, as the walls were occasionally jagged and the bottom was uncomfortable and rough.  But they seemed a bit too uniform in size  and too convenient in layout  to be natural.

Making our way back, we veered away from the ocean because we were shivering a bit coming out of the water.  I had wanted to see the manatees and the turtles, but wasn't ready yet because of the cold, and assumed I would have plenty of time later.  As you may have guessed from this lead in, I never saw the manatees and the turtles.

We did see many animals, though.  We walked past the aforementioned panther and puma islands.  We took some time in the mammoth Butterfly Pavilion (highly educational regarding the life cycle of the butterfly, with butterfly-infested plants in a greenhouse showing every step from egg to cocoon).  We went in the Bat Cave, but didn't see any bats (perhaps because they were annoyed by some minor construction work in the cave).  We saw a tapir (which was pretty cool!), some deer (which were pretty ordinary), some spider monkeys, and a wild boar.  In fairness to the deer, they were included not for zoological diversity, but rather because of their importance in Maya society.  The deer (along with the pheasant and the snake) was one of the three representatives of the Maya people when they were given their land  Mayab  by the god Itzamna.  And the deer factored into their society on both a physical level (as food and as skin they used for writing their codices) and on a spiritual level (as the animal that created the moon's sexual organs).  (I know that last part sounds like an interesting story, but I don't have any more details at the moment.)

Then we made our way to the Maya village while we waited for a tour in which we were interested:  The Maya Underworld.  We embarked on the tour with a Maya guide, Moan, who told us some information about Xibalba and took us past several ceremonies that had (and still have) a part in the Maya civilization and its relationship with this underworld:  the Owl Ceremony (as the owl symbolically whisks the spirit of the dead to Xibalba), a Maya priest blessing a burial site, the women's Corn Dance, and the Fire Dance that would be performed at the culmination of the 52-year Calendar Round cycle.  These were spectacular, especially the Fire Dance, in which fire was handled at length  burning coals were carried around in bare hands for several minutes at a time, fingertips were ignited and left burning, fire was waved underneath sensitive body parts such as the soles of the feet and the backs of the legs.  Moan confirmed that this was real fire, as hot as we imagined, and not mere theatrics using some substance that burned at a much lower temperature.  If this is true, I don't understand how they did it.  I am, however, left with the assumption that the representations have been adjusted in time and in content for the tourist audience.  As related to us, the Fire Dance was performed at a time of critical import to the Maya, when they believed the sun was being reborn, the earth was in danger of extinction, and their obligations to their gods were paramount (including the sacrifice of their kings).  This is not a ceremony that would have been conducted in twenty minutes.

The Owl avatar

As Moan led us from point to point in this tour of the underworld, we were using an underground cave system to get around.  Moan explained these structures to us, answering the question that had come to us in the Underground Rivers:  The structures are neither natural nor new; rather, they are the remains of an ancient mine, where rocks were quarried to pave the sak beob  the Maya "white roads" that shined in the moonlight.

We made our way up to the Maya Cemetery.  Moan insisted that this is the typical structure of a Maya burial place, with 365 graves (representing the number of days in a year) arranged around a central focal point, each grave being distinct in design from the others.  I have not been able to corroborate this structure elsewhere  but I can report that the cemetery (which did not hold real bodies) was artistically spectacular.  The crypts and gravestones were creative and beautiful.  And the layout was extraordinary.  At length, we realized that the whole thing was constructed on the roof of a giant central igloo-like core, and we made our way to see what was below.  Here we found another artistically spectacular setting of water filtering in along walls and below our feet, past endless rows of (electric) candles, to the base of a giant cross.  I can assure you that not every Maya cemetery has one of these under it  but it was amazing to see.

We climbed the hill to see the San Francisco de Asis chapel, which was  again  artistically spectacular, with pews hewn from mighty trees, a sturdy roof to offer protection from the rain, but no walls to block the view of the sea.

It was at this point that we realized we had run out of time, and would miss so many of the things we wanted to see at Xcaret  not only the manatees and turtles, but also the aquarium, the edible mushroom farm, the wildlife enclosure, the (real) archaeological sites, Paradise River, the Scenic Tower, the Stained Glass Plaza, the bee farm, and the orchid and bromeliad greenhouse.  And that doesn't even include the things we chose to skip (like snuba) and that weren't functioning (like the Sky Tour in funky tiny airplanes).

With evening approaching, we had dinner at a snack shop.  Continuing my plan to eat things I'd never heard of, I tried sincronizadas, grilled tortillas stuffed with ham and cheese.

Finally, we took our seats for the ball game, and it was fascinating to see this game in action:  Players try to put a ball through a vertically mounted hoop, touching it only with their elbows, knees, and waists (no hands and no feet).  Our guide at Cobá had told us games would go on for days and would end with the score of the first goal  but in this game there were several scores in a half-hour.  The discrepancy may have arisen because in this demonstration the opposing teams did not try very hard to block goals, as they undoubtedly would in a real game.

    

At the beginning of the Ball Game
    

We had let the sun set on us again, but I felt like it would be okay to drive the 30 kilometers back to the hotel on the main road (and it was; nothing went wrong).  We didn't stay for the final Extravaganza through, because we were both tired and I didn't want to add too much fatigue to the driving-at-night-in-Mexico fear.  I simply resolved that I would try to keep such driving to a minimum, and only on major roads, as we had been warned so strongly against driving at night here.
