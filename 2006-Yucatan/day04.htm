Leaving Rancho

So it was our last day at Rancho Sak-Ol, and we realized that it had turned into the "Hotel Adler" of this trip.  (The Hotel Adler was the first hotel we stayed at in Rome in 2003; we had approached it with trepidation, but became so fond of it that we judged all subsequent hotels against it.)  But we had to move on, so we packed our bags and enjoyed our last breakfast at Rancho (as the owners sometimes call it).  I haven't mentioned the breakfasts yet, have I?  Every morning the Rancho staff would lay out a buffet of fruits  banana, pineapple, cantaloupe, and (scrumptious) papaya  as well as cereals, pastries, and eggs; coffee and tea were on the stove; a basket of oranges was on the counter so I could (and did) squeeze fresh orange juice every morning.  This was always our best time to spend some quality time with the staff  especially Valente, a former archaeologist who now runs Rancho.  Valente's career work was not with the Maya, but he has had a lifelong fascination with their culture and would give us detailed information about places we had seen and planned to go.  And every morning he would make a pitch for us to see Ek Balam, a fairly recent excavation that has some of the best preserved Maya carvings, and a mouth-shaped portal that is unique in the as-yet-uncovered fragments of the Maya world.  We told him, as we did every morning, that we didn't know if our schedule was flexible enough to include Ek Balam  but that we would try to stop by there, and that we planned to return to Mexico and would certainly see it on a future visit if we failed to see it on this one.

So we packed our luggage, loaded the car, said goodbye one last time to Dulce (the guard dog), and hit the road southward toward our next hotel in Belize.

We stopped on the way, though, to see the striking Maya city of Tulum.  This is one of the smaller Maya sites, but one of the most impressive because of its placement looking out over the sea.  We struggled a bit to find our way in, past an ingeniously designed market that the lured the visitor in but caused such disorientation inside that we had trouble finding our way back to the car or to the archaeological site, then to the road where we had to walk about a kilometer to the beachfront ruins.  Tulum is pretty straightforward in its arrangement:  You pay your money, walk through a gate in the perimeter wall, and follow a more-or-less one-way route past all of the major buildings, including the Temple of the Wind and El Castillo.  (Note:  It is not always known what the Maya called their buildings, or even what they used them for.  Typically, the Spanish would call the tallest building of a site «El Castillo» ["The Castle"], and this nomenclature has stuck.  I am avoiding the Spanish terms where I can because they seem inauthentic, but sometimes it is necessary to refer to a building and there is no other option.)

Eager to continue on our journey, we paid 20 pesos for the privilege of riding the tram back rather than walking.  This worked out extremely well for us, as we arrived just as a group of Voladores de Papantla  just like at Xcaret yesterday  were preparing to perform the ritual.  We watched them and then walked through the disorienting market, purchasing a T-shirt and lunch before getting back on the road to the south.

Driving through the southern part of Quintana Roo was fascinating, as the road narrowed to where it felt like the trees were almost touching the car as we passed, widening only to pass through the towns of Felipe Carrillo Puerto and Limones on its way to the state capital of Chetumal.  There were topes (speed bumps) at every village, but we knew to expect this.

One odd thing we noticed in driving through rural Mexico was that virtually every settlement except the most miniscule would have a twenty-four hour restaurant and a twenty-four hour llantería (tire shop).  Now the restaurant I could understand, even though it seemed to provide a startling level of convenience in a place where "make-do" is the dominant philosophy  but I was at a loss to understand how there could be sufficient demand to justify a twenty-four hour tire shop in villages that seemed to have populations in the double-digits.  Nonetheless, there they were  in town after town after town.

We passed through the «Zona Maya» ("Maya Zone"), but even outside this area Maya huts were everywhere; it was more obvious than ever that the Maya culture was thriving.  I'd kept a map in my PDA of where gas stations could be found, as running low in the jungle was one of the hazards I'd been warned against  but the situation never got dire.  At least I didn't have to worry about shopping around:  The only gas stations in Mexico are PEMEX stations, all having the same price.

Notes on the Flag: If I may editorialize, the flag of Belize is a beautiful design filled with all sorts of unfortunate symbolism.  The white disk in the center is ringed with fifty olive leaves, representing the year (1950) that British Honduras, as it was then called, began the process of seeking independence from Great Britain.  A tree is in the top center, flanked by two figures:  a mixed-race man holding an ax (left) and a man of African descent holding an oar (right).  The coat of arms between the two men is divided into three sections:  an oar and a sledgehammer (left), a saw and an ax (right), and a ship (bottom).  Below this mélange is Belize's motto, "Sub Umbra Floreo" ("I Flourish in the Shadow").  The field is blue, reflecting in part a design that was proposed by one of the two main political parties.  The top and bottom red stripes were added to that proposed design, to make it appear less partisan, when the nation achieved independence in 1981.
 
 

At length (after about three and a half hours, to be precise) we made it to the Belize border, and I was ready to start the exciting process of getting across it.  This was my first "real" land border crossing.  I'd gone between the U.S. and Canada, of course  but that was pre-9/11, when things were a lot easier.  And I'd had my passport checked when going from Switzerland to Austria, Austria to Liechtenstein, and Switzerland to Italy.  But they just waved us through going from France to Switzerland.  And a lot of the countries in the European Community don't even have a sign at the border, let alone an immigration stop.  This was my first opportunity to cross a land border where the process was taken seriously.

We drove under a sign reading "Welcome to Belize, Central America."  This was a nice introduction, but we weren't quite there yet:  Exiting Mexico was a snap, but the Belizean immigration system was a bit difficult to navigate.  We passed a collection of buildings and wondered if we needed to stop at any of them  but they did not block the roads and there were no agents to guide us.  Eventually, a man whistled for our attention, and offered to shepherd us through the process.  Apparently, these people work for tips guiding confused travelers through the system.  Putting my trust in a complete stranger at a border crossing (especially a stranger who is clearly a free-lancer), and letting that stranger ride in my car for brief periods (as was necessary), is not something that I find great comfort in  but this is how the system is structured, and it all worked out.  (In retrospect, I realize now that I was in a transition area between Mexico and Belize, and that there would be no place for him to run without quick interception if he robbed or threatened us, but this was not so clear at the time.)

Anyway, as it turns out we did need to stop at the collection of buildings.  First we needed to go to a building on the right to get the mandatory car insurance for Belize.

There was a large building on the left that looked important, but we never needed to go in it, and I don't know what it was for.  Then we came to the Belizean immigration facility, parked in the lot, and went inside to be processed.  We got our passports stamped in short order at immigration, and then went to the customs line.  I've traveled somewhat extensively internationally, and I've never had anything to declare; indeed, I've always assumed that the people who have things to declare must be absurdly rich, or must have come to the country specifically to make some large purchase.  So I went through the line saying I had nothing to declare.  This was a mistake  not a mistake that got me in any trouble, mind you, but a mistake nonetheless:  I was required to import (!) and thus to declare the rental car.  They also wanted to look in our luggage, perhaps because of the confusion engendered by my failure to understand my obligations with the rental car.  Anyway, we weren't smuggling anything, and my errors were clearly made from ignorance, so the Belizean border patrol genially guided us through the process and then sent me with my luggage back to the car, and sent Billy with his luggage on foot outside the border control area into Belize.  This is because only the driver may take the car past the checkpoint; the passenger has to go through on foot.

So I showed the border patrol that my passport had been appropriately stamped for my immigration and for the import of the rental car.  They allowed me through, and I picked up Billy on the other side.  We were in Belize!

I must confess that I approached this country with an assumption that it was on a very slight upswing after a thousand years of decline.  The tallest building in Belize was built at least 1100 years ago.  And the population of the country now (just over 250,000) is a quarter of what the same area held then.  I was thoroughly and completely mistaken about Belize:  It is a wonderful country, and I cannot count how many times since my return I have said, "Someday I'm moving to Belize."

Not far past the border was the first military checkpoint.  Because still-active Maya trails, left over from ancient days, make the nation's borders so porous, these military checkpoints along major roads help ensure that the people in the country are actually authorized to be there.  This checkpoint waved us through, so we continued to trek inward, first to the city of Corozal, where I would be able to load up on Belizean currency at an ATM.  In the trip planning, I had read that Belize's banking systems are generally not integrated with the international networks, and that I would have difficulty finding an ATM that would accept my card, but this information seems to be out of date:  I had no trouble at all finding ATMs to use.  Also of note is that each of Belize's banknotes is designed to show off a different aspect of the nation.

Thus set, we continued for the four hour trip through Belize, passing endless sugar cane trucks, several bridges over rivers (most free, one with a toll), a couple of military checkpoints, a prison, and a few towns (with their "pedestrian walkways," as speed bumps are known here).  This route took us through the Districts of Corozal, Orange Walk, and Belize to our destination, the District of Cayo.

Driving in Belize was a bit awkward in the Mexican car, as the Polo only had information in kilometers, but Belize is not metric, so our entire time in the country was spent multiplying our speed by 2/3, and distance information by 1.5, to gauge our progress.

Mile markers in Belize are handwritten; this picture is along the Northern Highway, the best road in Belize

There was a military checkpoint just before we left the Northern Highway in Ladyville, but they merely checked our passports, insurance, and driver's license and  finding it all in order  allowed us onward.

In the district of Belize, just after we turned onto the Western Highway, we came to a ridge, famous in these parts, called the Sleeping Giant.  This mountain range looks very much like a traditional Maya royal (with the sloped forehead) in repose.

The Sleeping Giant
    

The face of the Sleeping Giant

Farther west we passed through the national capital of Belmopan, where the seat of government was moved after Hurricane Hattie devastated Belize City in 1965.  Belize argues that Belmopan is the smallest national capital in the world  but I know for a fact that Vaduz (Liechtenstein) and Monte Carlo (Monaco) are smaller.  And a Web search on "smallest capital in the world" brings up a lot of references to Victoria (Seychelles).

The drive through Belize was highly pleasant in the daylight, with well-marked roads and interesting scenery.  I had been told that driving at night in Belize was not particularly bad, so I hadn't approached it with dread as I had driving at night in Mexico  but I found that in dim light the abundance of vibrant street life  bicycles, children, and livestock  that made Belize so wonderful in the day was a vibrancy that made driving at night a tad nerve wracking.

San Ignacio

San Ignacio

We made it though, in good order, to the twin cities of Santa Elena and San Ignacio.  The two towns are separated by the Macal River, and crossing it was my first major driving mistake:  The Hawkesworth Bridge is Belize's biggest bridge, and only suspension bridge  so notable that it is featured on the nation's fifty-dollar bill.  But the Hawkesworth Bridge is only one-lane wide.  In the past, this was accommodated by taking turns, but more recently a wooden bridge has been erected for westbound traffic.  I got lost in the approach, found the bridge, recognized it as a landmark on my path, and started across with the expectation that oncoming traffic would yield the right-of-way.  This did not happen  but with the typically Belizean make-do attitude, the oncoming cars squeezed to one side and signaled for me to squeeze past on the other side, and we all made it to our respective destinations.

In town, we asked directions to Eva's Restaurant, where we had arranged for a cave tour the coming Sunday, and where we knew they would be able to direct us to our hotel.  It was close by, and near suppertime, so we found a parking space and walked up the street to Eva's for dinner.  Here we found ourselves in another world:  The restaurant opened onto a narrow sidewalk and an open gutter; the bathroom was out back, but the sink was in the dining room; a cat pawed at our legs until we relinquished a few scraps; people wandered in and out, and a group of friends started playing some music with a Caribbean beat on the sidewalk out front.  I selected yet another dish I'd never heard of before  salbutes (a mix of chicken, black beans, lettuce, cabbage, carrots, and salsa over a corn tortilla).  Billy had a cheeseburger with, he reported, a delicious Central American flair (buttery bun, flavorful meat, and no shortage of artery-clogging goodies).  San Igancio is the exact mix of dirt roads, bicycles, bars, potholes, untended children, and free-running goats that you would expect in the caricature of a Central American border town.  There were no carts on the street selling fried weasel on a stick  but it wouldn't have felt out of place if there had been.  And Eva's was the perfect introduction to San Ignacio.  But I cannot in honesty leave you with the impression that I had anything but an immediate love for the place.  When the band started playing as I was crunching away on my salbutes, that was the moment I knew I someday wanted to live in Belize, to become part of this culture in San Ignacio.

Parrot Nest

After dinner, the folks at Eva's gave us directions to the Parrot Nest, where we would be spending the next three nights.  Parrot Nest is in the nearby town of Bullet Tree Falls, a few miles out of town along an interesting collection of rutted dirt roads and one-lane paths.  At the hotel, we were greeted and informed that we had already passed the test of the guard dogs (Mango and Lollipop), who instinctively knew guests from evildoers.  Our room was in a treehouse, fourteen feet off the ground on stilts.  The windows had screens, but no glass.  The roof was made from the leaves of the bay plant, like all of the Maya huts we had seen (and they kept out the overnight rain marvelously).  The cabins were connected by sawdust walkways gentle enough to walk on barefoot.  The bathroom was down a short staircase, across a platform, down more stairs, and along a jungle path.  The property was surrounded on three sides by the Mopan River, and gentle music wafted until the early hours from riverside bars nearby.  It would be possible to construct a smaller room with two twin beds that allowed the door to open inward and provided walking space between the beds, so I'm confident the design of the room was not the answer to some mathematical challenge  but its design did, in fact, approximate the answer to such a puzzle.  I add all this detail merely to provide context and character; Parrot Nest was a wonderful hotel and I would  will  enthusiastically return.

Illness I

And here's an interesting story:  I got mildly sick the first night.  (Reluctantly, of course) I awoke Theo, proprietor of the Parrot Nest, for advice about "La Turista," in case that was what I was suffering.  And in response to my call, Theo said, "Oh no! Is it the marching army ants in your room?"  She elaborated that "they bite, but they don't leave marks."  We were both comforted that army ants were not the problem.  I suspected the salbutes (as the vegetables may have been washed in tap water)  but Theo assured me that tap water in Belize is safe to drink.  On her advice, I nursed my stomach in the communal area with Coke and lime, and luckily was back to normal by morning.
