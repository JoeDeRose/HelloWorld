Club Med

So the time came to leave Rancho again.  I'm sure we will return – but it was sad to go.

We traced essentially the same route as the previous day, going just a little farther past Vallalodid and Dzitnup to Chichén Itzá.  Everything was as it was the day before except the price:  The road that cost 180 pesos yesterday was, inexplicably, 190 pesos today.

Toll for Mexico 180D
(MXN)$190.00=(USD)$18.20

  

Our first stop was at our hotel, the Club Med Villas Arqueológicas.  It sounds highfalutin, I know – but this is not your ordinary Club Med; this is the low-rent version of Club Med.  Seriously, the spa people own hotels at five archaeological sites in Mexico, including this one.  They hotels are nice, but they are not the environment that one generally associates with the Club Med name.  It wasn't a unique hotel like Rancho or the Parrot Nest, but it did have character:  bougainvillea bloomed at the entry; the decor was distinctive; the artwork was assembled by a serious collector; and the rooms were large and full of character in their design.  On any other trip it probably would have been our favorite hotel.

How to say this?  We should have seen Chichén Itzá first.  It's an amazing site, but the obvious reconstruction of two sides of El Castillo put me off.  And, frankly, by the time we got there, we had become "ruin snobs":  We wanted to see something unique; we wanted to have a lot of access to the ruins; and we wanted our interaction with other visitors to be minimal.  But in Chichén Itzá the unique things were locked away, access was highly limited (more so, in fact, than is typical for this site), and we had no hope of getting away from the madding crowd and the incessant hawkers holding up plastic idols and shouting "Two for a dollar!  You speak English?  Two for a dollar!"  It would have been a great way to begin the trip – but it was off-putting at the end.

Valente, whose views on Chichén Itzá had largely informed ours, described it as "Disneyland."  That may be true of the reconstructed parts, but there is some archaeological authenticity to be found.  At least UNESCO thinks so (though they misspell it, without the accents) as they've declared it a World Heritage Site:

 

Pre-Hispanic City of Chichen-Itza

This sacred site was one of the greatest Mayan centres of the Yucatán peninsula. Throughout its nearly 1,000-year history, different peoples have left their mark on the city. The Maya, Toltec and Iztec vision of the world and the universe is revealed in their stone monuments and artistic works. The fusion of Mayan construction techniques with new elements from central Mexico make Chichen-Itza one of the most important examples of the Mayan-Toltec civilization in Yucatán. Several buildings have survived, such as the Warriors' Temple, El Castillo and the circular observatory known as El Caracol.

Conscription criteria:  Cultural – (i) to represent a masterpiece of human creative genius; (ii) to exhibit an important interchange of human values, over a span of time or within a cultural area of the world, on developments in architecture or technology, monumental arts, town-planning or landscape design; (iii) to bear a unique or at least exceptional testimony to a cultural tradition or to a civilization which is living or which has disappeared.

 

Even so, were were not quite as impressed as UNESCO, and the situation was not helped by the fact that Chichén Itzá brought us immediate disappointment:  Not only were we not permitted to climb the exterior of El Castillo, but we would not be permitted in the interior pyramid either.  The former was only a mild letdown for me, assuaged by the fact that we had climbed pyramids at Cobá, Tikal, and Xunantunich.  But not being able to see the interior pyramid was a grievous disappointment.  This type of excavation was unique among the places I had researched, and had driven the inclusion of Chichén Itzá in our itinerary.  And it wasn't just the pyramid structure that we were to miss:  There was supposed to be a chacmool in the interior pyramid, and another atop the cordoned-off Temple of the Warriors – but we wouldn't be seeing either one.  It seemed like the lockdown wasn't permanent, but it definitely lasted through the day we were there.  Oh well, this is how things get on the "Regrettably Missed" list.

While Billy rested, I ran over to the ball court, which was stunning in its size and layout.  First, this is the largest ball court in Mesoamerica, and it certainly dwarfed any that we had seen before.  Second, under the Aztec influence, this ball court had vertical walls, turning a challenging game into a near-impossible one (remember that the ball could be propelled only by the players' elbows, knees, and waists).

We were both exhausted in the unrelenting heat.  The jungle canopy at other sites had provided more relief than we had realized, but in the open fields of Chichén Itzá the intense February sun wore us down quickly.  So we went back to the hotel – but not before I purchased an Aztec calendar.  I had passed up such a calendar earlier on the trip because the Aztec design was not from the area and didn't seem appropriately authentic.  But Chichén Itzá reflects a strong Aztec influence, so here it seemed okay.

This is also where Billy and I gave our haggling skills a workout:  We hadn't wanted to be too predatory in Puerto Morelos, as they were recovering from one of the most devastating hurricanes in their history.  We didn't have time in Tulum.  It didn't seem to be how things were done in Guatemala (at least where we were).  But here they were ready to bargain and we were so annoyed by their relentless hawking that we wanted a pound of flesh.  The Aztec calendar started at 100 pesos; most vendors wouldn't go below 50; one was willing to sell for 30; I eventually got it for 20.  Billy wanted an onyx panther of a type that some vendors placed at 300 pesos; most of the vendors expected 150 to 200; he got it for 100.  Chichén Itzá rules for the penny pinching tourist:  All of the vendors are set up on blankets side-by-side.  And they all have similar stuff.  You just find what you want, argue a while about the cost, walk away, see if they go lower, and then go to another blanket and use the lowest amount as the starting point on a similar item.  If they weren't so pesky, though, I probably wouldn't have been so relentless.
