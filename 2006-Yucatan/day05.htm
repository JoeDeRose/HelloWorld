Guatemala

We started the day early with breakfast at Parrot Nest (including papayas even better than at Rancho Sak-Ol).  And then we hit the road without delay so that nothing would impede us in our effort to see Tikal.

I've been describing San Ignacio as a border town, because it feels like a border town – but technically one has to pass through eleven miles and another city – Benque Viejo del Carmen – before reaching the border.

So we drove the distance as quickly as we reasonably and legally could:  Considering the confusion with getting into Belize, we were prepared for any kind of confusion getting into Guatemala, and we wanted to allow a sufficient buffer for delay.  Belize border patrol stamped us out of the country, and charged us the appropriate fee for the privilege of leaving.  They dithered for a bit as to whether to export the rental car, but eventually decided to leave it imported in Belize as we would be back later in the day.  Then they sent us on our way to Guatemala.  This side of the equation added a few layers of complexity, and there were definite nods to the third world in the process, but Guatemala's system didn't seem quite so chaotic as Belize's.  Even so, I do remember it in a kind of stream-of-consciousness way:  I parked the car.  People swarmed around to convert my money to Quetzales.  A border agent came to help me get things done.  I converted 200 pesos and 40 U.S. dollars at what I considered a terrible rate.  They sprayed the car's tires for bugs.  I paid them for doing so.  I went to a window.  My passport got stamped.  I made arrangements to import the car.  The agents walked out to look at the car.  They sent me to the bank at the border to pay the import fee.  They held my passport while I went to the bank.  An old man with a rifle told me where to stand in line.  I waited behind some woman as they counted out 24,000 units of some currency for her.  A pig walked by.  The man with the rifle came to check on me.  I went back with a receipt.  They gave me a sticker to make the car legitimate in Guatemala.  They explained to me that when I came back, I would need to take off the sticker and bring it inside.  They said this in Spanish – very slowly and loudly, so I would understand.  They gave me back my passport and lots of receipts.  They told me that there was no car insurance for sale at the border, but I could buy it in Flores if I was going through there (I wasn't).  I asked the border agent who had assisted me if it was appropriate to tip him, and he said no.  I drove to the gate.  They looked at my passport and sticker, and approved.  They raised the heavy counterweighted gate by hand.  And we drove into Guatemala.  I'm not sure it all happened in that order.  In fact, the only parts I'm absolutely certain of are the man with the rifle and the pig – the rest of it may have been a figment of my imagination.

But I liked entering Guatemala more than I liked entering Belize.  I liked having the border agent show me around.  I liked the fact that he didn't expect a tip.  I liked that I could leave my car parked and walk from station to station.  I liked the pig.  Things were looking good:  So far, I liked Guatemala.

And now with a fistful of quetzales, I was juggling four currencies in my wallet (U.S. dollars, Mexican pesos, Belizean dollars, and Guatemalan quetzales), which made me feel very sophisticated indeed.

Immediately after the border is a toll bridge over the Mopan River.  I'm not certain that this bridge was built to handle two-way traffic, but it is being made to work in that regard now.  So we squeezed and we passed and we were in ...

... Ciudad Melchor de Mencos, the border town on the Guatemalan side.  Now mind you, these towns are not interchangeable – each one has a personality as distinct as a fingerprint.  And Melchor de Mencos was among the most distinctive.  First, it was suddenly hilly – like driving across the Ohio River into Kentucky, that kind of sudden change in landscape.  Telephone poles were mounted along the center of the roadway, sort of like a median.  And those telephone poles were painted at about body height with the blue-white-blue of the Guatemalan flag.  I had thought people and dogs ran around endlessly in the streets of Belize, but it was nothing like the streets of Guatemala.  Immediately after crossing the Mopan, we made a wrong turn – but my OCD habit of asking directions every time I see a human being who is not actively engaged in the process of avoiding me got us back on track pretty quickly, and we were finally on the road to the center of El Petén, the city of El Remate and then the archaeological site of Tikal.

El Petén is Guatemala's largest and least densely populated department.  The "Alaska of Guatemala," if you will, it occupies a third of the country's land area, but holds only about 3% of the people.

 
 

Departamento de Petén
Departament of Petén
 
 

Okay, I'll admit that normally I enjoy hanging out a country's dirty laundry.  I got almost gleeful about it with Liechtenstein.  But normally I'm traveling in those well-heeled places that the World Bank refers to as "Category A Countries," and I think they can stand the scrutiny.  I'm not so enthusiastic about Guatemala.  Not only did I like Guatemala, but I want others to like it as well – and a history lesson of the recent decades is not going to paint the picture of Guatemala that I want to paint.  Nonetheless, there is a story to tell.  And it's not just a story about their country; it's also a story about our country.

Through the time of World War II, Guatemala was ruled by a dictatorship that sought economic development by placing land in the hands of wealthy (mostly American) investors.  But in 1944, this dictatorship was overthrown and Guatemala began a period, known as the "Ten Years of Spring," which prioritized social programs and education for the indigenous population (which, by the way is about 60% of Guatemala's total population).  This progressive government then began an initiative for land nationalization  The U.S., which was terrified of anything that smacked of communism, but which had no special concerns as to whether people are allowed to run their own affairs and make their own choices, was alarmed into action:  An operation codenamed PBSUCCESS ("PB" = "Presidential Board") was launched to train and organize guerrillas to take down the government.  Liberals – including Che Guevarra – were driven out of the country.  And Guatemala was plunged into a 35-year civil war.  The American military continued to train and support the insurgents, including via the notorious School of the Americas in Columbus, Georgia.  So invested were they in micromanaging this one small country that the CIA even used Guatemala as its training ground for the disastrous Bay of Pigs Invasion.  And under our nation's leadership, Guatemala's government launched a pogrom against the indigenous Maya population.  People were gathered, village by village, and murdered brutally at the hands of the (U.S.-trained) army.  Those who were not killed immediately were often forced to watch, or even participate in, the murders.  The earth was ruined as crops, livestock, and water were destroyed or polluted.  Many had limbs amputated.  Children were beaten, tortured, raped, and thrown alive into mass graves.  Women were raped and tortured.  And the wombs of pregnant women were cut open.  This was in the early 1980s (so most of you reading this report were alive when it happened).  Eventually 626 villages were attacked and 200,000 Guatemalans were murdered.  The purge lasted two years, and became known as the "Silent Holocaust" (a term that has also been applied to other mass killings that failed to attract international attention, and that has also been co-opted by the anti-choice movement).  The civil war came to an end ten years ago, in 1996.

It wasn't the first time Guatemala had been treated as an international pawn by a  superpower.  Spain started the trend by plundering the area's resources in its exploration of the western hemisphere.  And in the mid-1800s Britain promised the construction of a road from Guatemala City to the Caribbean coast if Guatemala would relinquish any claim on British Honduras, the former name of Belize.  When over a century had passed and Britain had not built the road, as British Honduras had thrived while Guatemala had not, Guatemala declared the agreement void and pressed a claim for the territory.  It was only in 1991 that the Belize and Guatemala were able to normalize relations.

I knew these histories before I entered Guatemala, and they shaped what I expected to see once inside the country.  I expected animosity at the border between the two still-distrustful nations.  I expected war-torn landscapes and defeated peoples.  As an American I expected resentment.  But as I drove the road westward toward El Remate, I found none of this.  As a matter of fact, it was among the most attractive, engaging roads I have driven – stunningly beautiful, really, in places, as it passed alongside lakes, mountains, and valleys.  And though we were certainly treated as objects of curiosity, we were never treated with contempt.  And we never felt at all unsafe.

The route started as a rough dirt road, but eventually it became smooth – smooth enough, in fact, that they felt it necessary to put in speed bumps (called «tumulos» here in Guatemala) when the dirt highway passed through villages.  And after about a half hour of driving, we reached the area where it was paved.  (The process is moving eastward, and will eventually reach Melchor de Mencos.)

The road itself was interesting.  We saw a cattle drive:  We just rounded a gentle curve and saw about 30 head of cattle trotting toward us, all chased by a cowboy on horseback waving a lariat.  Hollywood couldn't have done it better!  (We just came to a stop and let them all trot on by.)  There were a lot of pigs and goats and cows and horses and dogs and chickens and turkeys wandering around, none of them taking too much notice of us or each other.  Military troops were moving on foot from village to village.  I knew this was to keep us safe, as the road from Belize to Tikal is one of Guatemala's cash cows, but had been plagued by armed robberies in the not-too-distant past – and INGUAT (Instituto Guatemalteco de Turismo, the Guatemalan tourism agency) has heavily invested itself in improving safety for tourists along this corridor.  Nonetheless, I couldn't help a nagging thought that military troops moving village-to-village might be serving a different purpose than they had twenty-five years ago – but might not be much different in appearance.

Cattle on the road to Tikal

I've been asked frequently if I saw a lot of poverty on the trip, and especially in Guatemala.  I think its fair to say that we had more interaction with the locals than most tourists, mostly because of my obsessive confirmation of our direction and distance, as well as our ceaseless search for Coke Light (which is a lot harder to find in El Petén than the regular stuff).  Almost every shanty on this two-hour road has a sign out front announcing that soft drinks can be purchased there, and we stopped at a good proportion of them in our search, and spoke to a fair number of people in the process.  (A few of them really did sell Coke Light, so it wasn't entirely a cultural exercise.)  I think it's a safe assumption that the overwhelming majority of the people we met could not afford a car, might not be able to afford a television, and would never be able to build a house like the one I live in.  It is also true that their climate is such that the don't need so grand a house, and that their culture is such that cars and televisions, while nice, aren't necessary.  The people themselves seemed to be doing fine:  Their clothes were not ragged.  They appeared well-fed – indeed, most of our interactions were in places where they were dining, comfortably and jovially, with friends.  They didn't have a sullen or despairing or hungry look – rather, they appeared relaxed, engaged, and curious, both with us and with each other.  When measured in per capita income, they were undoubtedly poor – alarmingly so, I would guess, when compared with an American salary.  (The World Bank rates countries in economic categories from A to D; the U.S., Canada, and most of Western Europe are Category A; Guatemala is Category C.)  But I guess what it comes down to is this:  If I suddenly were told that I had to trade places with one of them, and live my life as they live theirs, I would not approach that situation with panic or dread.  They seemed to have a comfortable life, and to be happy with it.  And they seemed to be interested in my life and Billy's, yet not to be jealous of us.

As an avid fan of Survivor, I found it especially exciting to pass the turn-off to the Yaxhá ruins where the twelfth season was filmed.  (But that's as close as we got:  Yaxhá wasn't on our itinerary.)

And as a traveler, I found it exciting to pass the point outside El Remate where the road gently turns from west-by-southwest to west-by-northwest, as this marked the southernmost point I had yet reached in my life.  The previous record was Ka Lae in Hawai'i – but I bested that at about the time we crossed from Mexico into Belize.  (Okay, for sticklers:  Technically, because one drives on the right in Guatemala, I hit the southernmost point on the trip from Tikal rather than the trip to it – but it was only a difference of a few meters, and this is the point of the narrative where the detail fits best.)

Tikal

Tikal

We passed quickly through El Remate, and a few kilometers north of town entered the enormous Tikal National Park.

As some people – let's call them "scholars of my life" – already know, I'm fascinated with World Heritage Sites.  Only two of the stops on this vacation were such sites, but Tikal was one of them.  Here's what UNESCO has to say about the place:

Tikal National Park

In the heart of the jungle, surrounded by lush vegetation, lies one of the major sites of Mayan civilization, inhabited from the 6th century B.C. to the 10th century A.D. The ceremonial centre contains superb temples and palaces, and public squares accessed by means of ramps. Remains of dwellings are scattered throughout the surrounding countryside.

Conscription criteria:  Cultural – (i) to represent a masterpiece of human creative genius; (iii) to bear a unique or at least exceptional testimony to a cultural tradition or to a civilization which is living or which has disappeared; (iv) to be an outstanding example of a type of building, architectural or technological ensemble or landscape which illustrates (a) significant stage(s) in human history.  Natural – (ii) to be outstanding examples representing significant on-going ecological and biological processes in the evolution and development of terrestrial, fresh water, coastal and marine ecosystems and communities of plants and animals; (iv) to contain the most important and significant natural habitats for in-situ conservation of biological diversity, including those containing threatened species of outstanding universal value from the point of view of science or conservation.

At the gate I paid our admission, and Billy and I continued our journey.  Finally we reached the parking area, ate a quick lunch at the restaurant on site, and went to see what all the fuss was about.

Tikal has been described as the "New York" of the Maya world.  Its Temple IV is the tallest pyramid of any yet excavated, and the once-inhabited city stretched for 60 km2.  Because we had limited time, our plan was to start at Temple IV – the farthest from the car among the main excavations – and then work our way back seeing what we could.  The first thing we came to was a magnificent specimen of a ceiba tree.  To the Maya, the ceiba was sacred, so much so that they believed the columns in caves were the spiritual roots of the ceiba.  (To put this in context, remember that the caves were revered by the Maya as Xibalba, the underworld, a nether region from which our world was spawned.)  In their traditional understanding of the construction of the universe, the heavens were suspended in the branches of five giant ceibas, one at each corner of the earth and one in the center.

So we walked, passing the Gran Plaza, finally reaching Temple IV.  This is what the fuss is about.  There's nothing I can say here, no picture I can show, that will give you a sense of what it is like to stand atop this pyramid and look out at creation.  We rested at the top for a long time basking in the silence; later chatting with other visitors; watching spider monkeys leap among the trees below.  I wish you all could have been there, and I hope you all will someday go.  This is a place that will deepen your soul.

As some of you already know, I maintain a "Regrettably Missed" list of places we intended to see on trips, but weren't able to experience for one reason or another.  I had been convinced almost to the last minute that I would have to add Tikal to that list.  Even just five hours ago, it has all seemed so unrealistically complicated:  entering another country; driving halfway across it along a dirt road that was known for armed robbery ambushes; making our way without getting lost and without running out of time.  As we walked those jungle paths back from Temple IV, Billy and I both shared a giddy elation that we had actually managed to come here and to experience this place.  My only regret is that we didn't see it last, because we visited more pyramids in our remaining days in Mesoamerica, and all of them were wonderful in their own ways – but nothing ever seemed stupendous after Temple IV.

Finally, knowing that we had to get on the road quickly to reach Belize before nightfall, we did some quick souvenir shopping and then got on our way.

This is the bowl I purchased at Tikal  (N.B.:  One thinks of Guatemala as a bit of a backwater – but the gift shop took American Express.)

Back to Belize

On our way back, we couldn't bring ourselves to drive past El Remate without stopping just to glance at the souvenir shops.  We didn't buy anything, but it was nice to spend a few minutes at least in this vibrant town on the banks of Lake Petén Itzá.  This area is known for woodworking, and we saw some amazing carvings – including an incredible long boat with vividly costumed Maya characters riding in it (which would never have fit in the car, let alone in our luggage).  Billy had purchased some nice carvings at Tikal, and I had decided to take a pass, but we were both glad to see what was on offer in El Remate.  This diversion did, however, mean that sunset was likely to come before I reached Melchor de Mencos.  Mind you, the one thing that had been hammered into my head for this trip was:  Don't drive at night.  And if for some reason you are stupid enough to drive at night in Mexico or Belize, don't – for the love of God and all that is holy – drive at night in Guatemala.

So the sun set at about an hour-and-a-half into our two hour trip.  And you know what:  Driving at night in Guatemala isn't a whole lot different from driving at night anywhere else.  You turn on your headlights.  You turn the wheel clockwise for right; counterclockwise for left.  You go a little slower.  And eventually you get where you're going.  One has to be more careful with regard to people and wildlife than in, for example, rural Virginia – but it wasn't a nail-biting experience.  I finally decided to wash my hands of this whole driving-at-night paranoia.

And we arrived, without incident, at the border.  Guatemala stamped us out, documented the car as exported, and collected the windshield sticker.  I had the car fumigated again (I really don't know what they think is going to cross the border on my tires that can't just crawl across on its own, but I'm sure they have a reason for this exercise).  Belize stamped us in.  And we drove eleven miles back to Parrot Nest (at night, without disaster).
