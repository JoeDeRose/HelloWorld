Cobá-Chimuch Adventure Tour

Vocabulary and Pronunciation

    Mayan road; "sak be" means "white road."  They were so named because they were covered with a pavement that the Maya created from the seashell-rich rocks along the sea.  This pavement allowed the Maya to travel at night, as the pavement would glow white in the moonlight.  Most of the roads were at ground level, but in areas they were elevated as much as four meters off the ground.

Okay, the people who say I cram too much stuff into a vacation day should really have a go at this tour.  Compared to this, traveling with me is like sitting on a bench in a retirement home.

Voucher for the Cobá-Chimuch tour

We parked our car on the main road – Mexico 307 – where it passes the turn-off to Puerto Morelos.  We had been instructed to find the AllTourantive van at the bus station, but the staff there said they could see it in front of the gas station down the block.  So we walked over and met Guillermo, who would be our "driver, guide, and friend" on the day's events.  We also met our tour-mates, including two Venezuelan women who spoke little English, and with whom I sat in the rear of the van practicing my Spanish.

Cobá

An hour down the highway, and another hour on a dirt road, took us to Cobá.  There we were turned over to the official guide of the archeological site, who gave us our introduction to the Maya in general and to this site in particular.  The guide first explained the nature of Maya society:  The cities were quasi-independent, connected by sac beob (roads) to other major cities, and ruled by kings and royal families who, unlike the lower levels of society, engaged in body modification such as skull shaping (by pressing the head between planks in infancy to make the forehead slope backward), jewel encrusting in the teeth, and tooth filing.  Further, he told us more about the Maya blood rituals:  The Maya believed that the world had been created by the sacrifice of the gods' blood, and that this gift had to be repaid constantly for the earth to survive.  The kings would pierce themselves – generally their tongues and penises – allowing the blood to run in a bowl, soak into a sacred parchment, and be burned in sacrifice to the gods.  We further learned about the levels of the Maya world, including Xibalba, the underworld to which so much of their world view was oriented.

The guide then let us explore the Cobá group, including «La Iglesia» ("The Church"; so called because locals view it as a shrine) the smaller of the two main pyramids at the site.

Then we were led to the ball court where the Maya played the «Juego del Pelote» to settle disputes and to show reverence to the gods.  Almost every Maya city has a ball court, but the sizes vary widely, usually in proportion to the size of the city.  Most have sloped walls, but those in cities influenced by the Aztecs – such as Chichén Itzá – had vertical walls.  The balls were about head-sized and made of rubber.  In Aztec games, they might be set on fire.  Finally, he told us that in certain circumstances – but not in every game – the captain of the winning team would be sacrificed to the gods.  This was a reward in the Maya world, and the teams would play enthusiastically to allow their captains this honor.  Games might last days, and would end when the first point was scored.

Ball Court («Juego del Pelote»)

After the ball court, we were given about forty-five minutes to make our way to and back from the larger pyramid, Nohoch Mul.  I chose to ride a bicycle; Billy took the tricycle limo.  Having a little more flexibility on the bike, I stopped on the way to look at another ball court and the Xaibe (crossroads) pyramid.

Billy got to Nohoch Mul first and raced to the top.  When I finally figured out that he wasn't behind me, I joined him there to see the spectacular views of the jungle from the summit.

Looking for a way to bump up my different-forms-of-transportation count, I asked the driver if I could join Billy on the tricycle limo for the ride back (as the fare covers two passengers, even though he was riding alone).  He welcomed me on and we quickly made our way back to the van – thoroughly satisfied, I might add, with our trip to Cobá.  While waiting for the others to return, Guillermo encouraged me to try a crêpe dish particular to Cobá:  marcacitas.  These are slightly sweet crêpes filled with savory cheese.  I like interesting food.  And I like good food.  Here I won on both counts.

Back in the van we drove for about a half hour north to a small lake.  Each of us selected a "brake" (without further definition or explanation) from a stack of notched sticks, and then we hiked as a group through the jungle – finally arriving at a platform on a high point overlooking the lake.  Here we strapped ourselves to a zip line, held the brake behind us, lifted our feet, and rode the line for about the length of a football field over the lake to a dock on the far side.  This is something I've wanted to do ever since I heard it existed.  Indeed, the gorge adventure in Switzerland last year, which was canceled on us unexpectedly, had zip-line traverses – and missing the opportunity was one of the bigger disappointments of that trip.  So finally getting to do it was totally cool!  I got twisted around on the way across, as did many of our tour-mates – but it wasn't hard to get myself righted, and the experience was fantastic!

At the far side, we got into kayaks in groups of two and paddled our way back across the lake, through some interesting clusters of reeds.  The tranquility of the kayak was a nice juxtaposition to the high-adrenalin crossing on the zip line.

Back on the far side of the lake, we reached a clearing and were greeted by a Maya elder.  It was explained by Guillermo that we would be swimming here in the Cenote Chimuch, but that it was necessary for the elder to ask permission of the gods that we enter this sacred place.  A corn incense was burned and the elder circled our group twice, first blessing us in Maya (with prayers to the Maya gods), and then in Spanish (with prayers to the Christian god).

We climbed down the hole into the cenote, swam a bit, and then retuned to the surface where the same elder extended another blessing wishing us safety as we continued our travels and then returned to our homes.

We returned to the van and drove another twenty minutes or so to the Maya village of Tres Reyes, where we learned a little more about the Maya:  Guillermo pointed out a cross that looked like a grave marker, and noted for us that "nobody died there."  Rather, it is a Maya symbol:  the Tree of Life.  Now is as good a time as any to mention the Maya response to the missionary invaders:  The Spaniards were savage; that is beyond dispute.  And they came with the intention of plundering the New World.  But the religion they brought with them was not rejected by the Maya.  Already there was similarity in the physical imagery of the two cultures.  And in the spiritual imagery, the Maya found Christianity highly enticing:  The Maya creation story held that the world was created by the blood-gift of the gods, through familial sacrifice.  The story of the Son of God, sacrificed to save the world, his skin pierced with nails, bleeding to death on a cross, was therefore a story they embraced and meshed with their own.  The missionaries were unsuccessful in driving out their indigenous faith – but into it was melded Christianity, an intertwining of beliefs that continues to this day.

After hiking, biking, zip lining, kayaking, hiking, swimming, hiking some more, and learning, we were famished.  Fortunately, in Tres Reyes, a feast was laid our for us:  vegetable soup, chicken with achote, chicken mole, pork, empanadas, three kinds of salsa, tamarind juice, jamaica juice, and peaches.

As soon as we finished the feast, we were invited to step to the adjacent cenote for our final adventure:  rappelling about 20 meters down to a kayak waiting for us below.  Heights, while not a phobia, are definitely my greatest source of unease – so I thought this would freak me out.  But it didn't freak me out at all:  I was able to put my weight on the rope while still above solid rock, and then to step out over the abyss knowing that the rope was supporting me.  So I stepped right out, let my rope slack a bit to begin the descent, held my feet against the wall, and continued downward.  After a couple of meters, I realized that we had been standing on a rock overhang, and that the wall was receding from me – leaving me hanging like a spider from its web.  So I continued releasing the rope and noticed, just before it was too late, that the kayak hadn't yet come to catch me.  I hung there in mid-air for a little while as they paddled over, then made my landing and rode back to the shore.

Back up top, we did some souvenir shopping in the village, and I purchased a representation of the Maya calendar for 120 pesos (about [USD]$11.50).

The hut in which we did our souvenir shopping
    

The calendar I bought; the Haab months are along the rim; the character in the center is the «Cargador del Tiempo», the "Carrier of Time."

After another hour in the van, Billy and I were back in Puerto Morelos.  My great fear of the trip had been driving at night – but we really had no choice on this day:  We had to drive to where the tour left; the tour had returned after dark; and we had to drive back.  Fortunately, it was only a few kilometers back to the Rancho Sak-Ol.  (And we made it without peril.)  I resolved to avoid driving at night as much as possible, but not to fear it when necessary.

At the hotel, we showered off a few layers of dirt (but somehow managed to leave a few layers on).  Then, once presentable, we went into Puerto Morelos for dinner – again at Pelicanos;  I had fish with tikinchik sauce, a local specialty.

After dinner we walked down to the beach again.  Then we slept soundly again in the electricity-free calm of the Rancho Sak-Ol.
