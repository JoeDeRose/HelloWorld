Xunantunich

Vocabulary and Pronunciation
 
Xunantunich   Place of the Stone Maiden
pronounced "shoo-NAHN-too-neech"

Almost as interesting as Xunantunich itself is the unusual way it is accessed:  across a hand-cranked ferry over the Mopan River.  Oddly, similar to the border crossings into and out of Belize, only the driver may be in the car during the crossings; Billy was required to step outside the car before I drove it onto the boat, and to stand next to it during the crossing.

The ferry runs between guide wires on either side of the vessel, and a third wire  wound around what appears to be a tire rim connected to a crank  is the mechanism by which it is propelled.  On the return I was eager to try my hand at the cranking, and the ferry operator was eager to let me do so.  I can report that this is not an effortless mechanism, and you'd definitely get at least one of your arms in shape doing it every day.

Once across the river, we made our way up the short hill to Xunantunich.  Because of the heat and some lingering fatigue after my illness the night before, we spent longer than we ordinarily would at the interpretive center.  This was a nice place to do it though, as Xunantunich had one of the best interpretive sites we had seen.

Xunantunich means "Place of the Stone Maiden"  but this is not the name the Maya gave to this place.  The name comes from a century-old legend of a maiden appearing beside the pyramid, and then vanishing suddenly.

After resting at the interpretive center, we made our way up the hill, past a few guards with rifles  which seemed a bit out of place in Belize  to the Grand Plaza.  Xunantunich is a small site, but an impressive one.  And after Caracol, El Castillo at Xunantunich is the second-tallest building in Belize.  We walked around the building, up the unusual winding stairs on the back side, and enjoyed the restored friezes on the east and west sides.  The friezes in these pictures are fiberglass molds that were made and placed over the originals in 1996; the excavated originals are buried behind the overlays, about a yard behind them.  It bears note that the friezes are not part of the final pyramid that you see in these pictures, but part of an older pyramid that was covered, and thus preserved, by this final construction.

Back to Mexico

Now, alas, it was time to leave beautiful Belize.  So we took the ferry back across the Mopan, made our way back to San Iganacio, took a couple of goodbye pictures of the place, crossed the Hawkesworth Bridge (in the proper direction this time), and began our four-hour journey to the Mexican border.  Every site along the way  both interesting and mundane  seemed like a sad goodbye to Belize:   the endless clusters of horses and dogs and bicycles and pedestrians, the swarms of school children in their uniforms, the sugar cane trucks in Corozal, the hand-painted mile markers along the Northern Highway, even the military checkpoints.  We knew we had to go  but we both knew we'd be back.

Knowing what was expected makes the border crossing easier.  I had, however, let the cash on hand run a bit low, and had to scramble with the money changers to turn enough pesos into Belizean dollars to get ourselves out of the country.  But this was done and we paid our exit fees.  We got stamped out of Belize in good order; and with minimal confusion the car was exported from Belize with a simple cancellation stamp in my passport.  Getting that export stamp, however, involved walking (as instructed by the guards, of course) through a corridor labeled "Authorized Personnel Only; Others Will Be Prosecuted."  I was slightly uneasy in passing this sign without escort, especially as I discovered that this corridor took me past the detention cells.  But, fortunately, I wasn't placed in one.  At the other side the Polo was repatriated to Mexico with a mighty rubber stamp and I was sent forth to continue the journey.  Fearful of the corridor, I tried to exit and walk around the exterior of the building, but in doing so discovered why I was sent through the foreboding hallway in the first place:  One side of the building is in unrestricted Belize; the other side is in the border control zone.  Technically, as I had already been stamped out, I had just snuck into Belize as an undocumented alien.  But no one seemed to take any notice, and I simply walked back through the customs an immigration area in the wrong direction, and back through the sternly labeled corridor.  I guess a lot of people have to do this.

We knew nightfall would come while we were still on the road, but by this point we didn't even care.  I was only disappointed that we would miss seeing sights along the way.  We drove up narrow Mexico 307 again past Limones and Felipe Carrillo Puerto.  At the latter, we stopped for grub on the side of the road at a simple roadside stand with four boiling pots and a stack of tortillas.  This is another thing we had been told not to do  to eat in a place such as this  but Billy and I have had our best traveling experiences in ignoring the counsel of others, and decided to hope for the best.  Billy selected a chicken dish, enjoyed it thoroughly, and never got ill.  I wasn't hungry at the time, but I picked at his scraps and can attest that it was wonderful.

Back to Rancho

Our original plan had been to find a hotel on the road toward Mérida, so that we could see Uxmal the next day.  But the advice of Vallente had preyed on our minds for the last week, and so we decided to return to the Rancho Sak-Ol for a couple more nights, and to explore Ek Balam instead.  We waited to call Rancho until after 7:00 PM, as we knew this was when they ran the generator, and would be most likely available by phone.  Indeed, we reached Ilda and they would be happy to have us back for two more nights!

In Playa del Carmen, my hunger finally caught up with me.  In Europe, I had enjoyed trying out McDonald's, as the outlets there had unusual menu items and a different feel from the American franchises.  I had hoped for a similar experience in Mexico, but didn't find it:  The McDonald's in Mexico was indistinguishable from its American counterparts, and I ended up with an ordinary burger after being so envious of Billy with his roadside chicken tacos.


Rancho Sak-Ol
 

A few kilometers later, we reached Puerto Morelos, turned up the road toward the sea, and then followed the two-kilometer dirt road to Rancho Sak-Ol.  Dulce the guard dog welcomed us back.  We kept the same two great rooms and settled in for the peaceful rest that we remembered from our previous days at Rancho.  We were not disappointed.
