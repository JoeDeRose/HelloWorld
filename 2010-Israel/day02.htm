The Western Wall was historically called the "Wailing Wall"➴ because it was a symbol for Jews of all that had been lost to them. In some respects this remains true, but today it is also a profound symbol of all that they have won.

 
➴ In such a contentious place, the rejection of the name "Wailing Wall" is a rare source of concord: Jews find the term demeaning. And some Muslim scholars recognize a {{fatwa}} that Muslims are forbidden to use this name because it can be thought to establish Jewish rights to a support structure for the Muslim holy space above. While the preferred term is Islam is the {{al_Buraq_Wall}}, named for Mohammed's winged horse that carried him to on the Night Journey to Jerusalem, all groups can accept the term "Western Wall".

It is easy in 2010 to see Israel as the powerful aggressor, and nothing more -- and to think of the abuses that led to the creation of the State of Israel (horrific though they were) as having been a continent away, deeply affecting the people perhaps, but not necessarily the land. In 2010 it is very easy to forget how hard-won Jerusalem was for the Jewish people, and how many enemies had to be fought to get it. Alas, the Western Wall is the nexus for many of the bitterest of these memories.

The tension between Muslims and Jews around this land is well-known and deeply rooted -- but some of the cruelest atrocities were perpetrated by the British troups who ostensibly kept the peace, but who in practice kept their own station by playing the two sides against each other regardless of the consequence. The moral nadir of the British "protectorate" came in the late 1920s, in the context of enforcement of an Ottoman decree banning new construction at the Western Wall. The British interpreted "construction" in a ludicrously broad sense, and pursued their enforcement with a ludicrously heavy hand: In the British mind, "construction" forbade the use of chairs, even by the elderly in the desert heat -- and whenever they would see grandmothers and grandfathers praying in chairs at the wall, they would without warning yank those chairs out from under the person heedless of the insult or injury they might cause. Indeed, there was a British functionary stationed at the wall for whom this was the sole job responsibility. Worse, when a temporary {{mechitza}} was set up to divide men from women during prayer, as is customary in orthodox Judaism, the British seized the wooden slats of the partition and used them to beat the women who had set it up. And when the British were not physically abusing the Jewish population, they ignored their plight altogether: British indifference led to rising tensions in 1929, culminating in what is known as the {{Western_Wall_Uprising}} -- a disturbance that began at the Western Wall, and spread throughout Palestine. In one particularly noxious incident, British soldiers looked on without action as they watched Jews massacred by an Arab crowd by the Jaffa Gate. So grievous and famous was the British government's mishandling of Palestine that the League of Nations approved a British requst for a commision on the status of the Western Wall only under the condition that no British citizen could serve on it.

Affronts -- both trivial and grievous -- to Jews worshiping at the Western Wall continued in the following decades: Muslims would try to disrupt Jewish religious services with noise or animal drives past the wall, and by allowing animal excrement to accumulate in the alleys leading to it. And the League of Nations ruled that -- while Jews must be given access to the prayer space -- the use of a {{shofar}}, the ram's horn used in religious observance in Judaism, was forbidden there; this edict was ignored every year at {{Yom_Kippur}}, resulting in imprisonment for up to six months for the person who fulfilled the obligation of blowing the shofar.

So dire was the situation of Jews under the {{British_Mandate_for_Palestine}} that the one of the Zionist organizations there, the Lehi➴¹ group, made an overture to Hitler's Nazi government in 1940➴² to assist in transferring Jews out of Germany in exchange for the Third Reich's assistance in expelling the British from Palestine.

 
➴¹ {{Lehi}} is an acronym in Hebrew for {{Fighters_for_the_Freedom_of_Israel}}.
➴² A belief persists among those who have heard of it that this request was issued before the full scope of Nazi anti-Semitism was understood. But, in fact, the Lehi group reached out to Hitler two years after Kristallnacht.

It's hard to envision the world of 2010 had this proposal succeeded: Jewish lives spared, but at the cost of validating and institutionalizing European anti-Semitism. In any event, the communication was never answered, and six million Jews➴ died in the following years at the hands of Germany's Nazi regime.

 
➴ And let's not skip too quickly over that well-known figure, for it is key to understanding why the Jews felt, and still feel, the need to protect their safe space regardless of the consequence in world opinion: The ancient Roman army used a practice called "decimation" to punish its enemies -- coming from the root word {{decem}} and meaning "to reduce by one tenth". In practice the reduction of a population by one tenth was so brutal, so crushing to the spirit of the survivors, that the term "decimate" came to be a synonym for "utter destruction". At the hands of the Nazis, one third of the world's Jews were wiped from the face of the earth.

Even at the end of World War II, when the British were patting themselves on the back for being one of the Allied powers that liberated the Jews in Germany, they were still enforcing anti-Semitic policies in Palestine that resulted in the humiliation, mistreatment, and death of Jews: Beyond a small quota that were permitted entry by the British, the vast majority of Jews had to enter Palestine surrepticiously -- even if they had no home nor safe harbor in their countries of origin; even if they were struggling to regroup and rebuild after seeing their businesses destroyed, their homes and property stolen, and their families murdered in German concentration camps -- and were treated as illegal immigrants if discovered. Further, British laws made it illegal to sell property to Jews, regardless of their legal status, in 95% of the Mandate area.

Very few could be said to owe the scurrilous Nazis a debt of gratitude -- but it may be that such a debt is owed by the British: Had not the swastika been so tainted with blood, had the Lehi group's offer -- even with its vile undertones -- been accepted, it might well be the Union Jack that we look on with revulsion when we think of the suffering of innocent Jews.

Lest you think I exaggerate the sins of the British, I draw your attention to the circumstances under which journalist Ruth Gruber took her most famous photograph. Gruber was in Haifa when the British Royal Navy attacked the Exodus 1947, a ship carrying 4,500 Holocaust survivors to what was then still the British Mandate for Palestine. Rather than be allowed to stay in a place of relative safety after having been rescued from the killing factories of the Nazi regime, these refugees were placed aboard the British prison ship Runnymede Park, housed in wire cages, and carried back to Germany -- the nation that had fought a world war in order to criminalize their very existence. The refugees left no doubt as to their feelings on the collusion between Allies and Axis at this juncture: In the confines of their prison ship they raised a Union Jack on which they had painted a swastika. And Ruth Gruber made journalistic history when she captured the image of their protest.

When the British were finally expelled, and an Israeli state was established, Israel was immediately attacked by its neighbors in the {{1948_Arab_Israeli_War}}, the resolution of which left the rest of the world with the impression of Israel as a powerhouse➴¹ -- but left Israel with a feeling of profound loss: The Western Wall was on the other side of the Green Line -- and the government of Jordan would deny Jews the right to pray there for the next nineteen years➴².

 
➴¹ Indeed, the more common term for this conflict in Arabic is {{al_Nakba}}.
➴² Of course, as spoils of an ongoing war, this transfer of control was not recognized with international unanimity. In fact, only one country formally and unequivocally endorsed Jordan's new claims over the Old City; that country was the United Kingdom.

The length and depth of this struggle may explain, but not excuse, the swiftness and relentlessness of the Israeli response when in June 1967 it came into possession of the Old City at the end of the {{Six_Day_War}}: When Israeli paratroopers landed at the base of the Western Wall, the gathering space at the foot of it was a narrow street, only [[4_m]] wide between the wall and the houses of the 800-year-old {{Moroccan_Quarter}}. Two days later, the Moroccan Quarter residents were told to leave their homes and never return -- and then within hours bulldozers started flattening those homes without regard to whether the people inside had heeded, or even heard, the warning; several died under the tumbling stone walls of their former neighborhood. And thus the congregation area at the Western Wall grew tenfold, to [[40_m]], and the vast [[2_ha]] Western Wall Plaza took the place of this once-thriving neighborhood, forming a direct, open connection between the wall and the {{Jewish_Quarter}}.

Everything I've written about above was awful. And everything now is ... different. Far from being viewed as a forbidden construction, a very solid mechitza now divides the Western Wall into a prayer area for men on the left, and for women on the right. Some women, of course, cherish this divider just as they did when they faced British wrath in setting one up in 1928 -- but for others it represents a expression of Judaism that does not match their own experience, aspirations, or faith. Now it is not the orthodox➴ Jews trying to blow the shofar who face prosecution for their expression of Judaism at this holy place, but rather the members of {{Women_of_the_Wall}}, who believe in gender equality in religious expression, but who have faced waves of prosecution and persecution for attempting to practice the Jewish faith, as they embrace it, at the Western Wall.

 
➴ The lower case is correct. In the U.S., a synagogue may describe itself as (upper-case) Orthodox, meaning that it belongs to an association at a particular level on the liberal-to-conservative spetrum, such as Agudath Israel, the Orthodox Union, or the National Council of Young Israel. But here in the shadow of the Temple Mount, the groups are more numerous; the relationships are more contentious; and the needle, on average, points much farther to the right.

And even devout orthodox women, who have no wish to challenge the separation, can and do make claims of discrimination: The women's prayer area is but a third of the total, which is an unfair inequality in the contexts of Jewish law and simple math -- but worse in the context of orthodox practice: Men, by orthodox custom, are allowed to pray in a {{minyan}} while women are required to pray individually. The clustering effect reminds one of the restroom situation in most office buildings: Women need more space, but are allotted less.

But no one -- regardless of her or his philosophy, faith, or practice -- can reasonably come to Jerusalem without a visit to the Western Wall. And this is what Mom and I did as our first act on our first morning in the Holy Land: We made our way down David Street from our hotel to the Western Wall Plaza, walking through its metal detectors and then through its ghosts, and finally arrived at that point which is at the bottom of the hill, but at the apex of Jewish heritage and the fulcrum of the State of Israel: The Western Wall.

Mom went to the right to explore the women's section, and I went to the left to explore the men's. Both sections include areas beyond the view of the Western Wall Plaza: The women's section includes a small enclave by Robinson's Arch under the walkway that leads up to the Temple Mount. And the men's section includes a large warren of rooms➴ under the elevated Muslim quarter, leading under Wilson's Arch, stocked with books and scriptures.

 
➴ The "hidden" portion of the men's prayer area is indisputably more desirable -- clean, attractive, and interesting (especially from an archeological perspective); offering protection from the elements; and richly stocked with educational resources. While I want to respect diverse religious practice, I cannot help but hold the opinion that it is both shameful and a shame that women are barred access to this area.

The {{Western_Wall_Tunnel}} is the public portion of an excavation under the {{Muslim_Quarter}}, which was elevated centuries ago to provide easier access to the Haram ash-Shareef (Temple Mount) after Muslims took control. The high retaining walls (such as the Western Wall) that created a hard-to-reach and elite space were fully in keeping with the sacredness of that plaza under Jewish control during the eras of the First and Second Temples -- but suited neither the theology nor the logistical needs of the Muslim populace. The effect was a forgotten, underground city, essentially preserved in time -- a Disneyland for archaeologists.

These excavations were begun by the British in 1864 and continue to this day -- continue, indeed, with such progress that, like Disneyland, the Western Wall Tunnel tour welcomes visitors with new attractions every year: Mom and I concluded our tour by walking through multiple chambers of the Struthion Pool, which had a much smaller footprint on the tour when Billy and I visited in 2007.

Alas, for reasons that are a mystery to me now, I didn't take any pictures during the tour despite the fact that photography was not prohibited at any point. So you'll have to go back to my 2007 trip report if you want to see the tunnel features through my eyes (or Wikipedia if you want better shots).

Conveniently (and, actually, in accordance with my plan), we exited the Western Wall Tunnel➴ right at the beginning of the next item on our agenda: the Via Dolorosa -- but not before taking a moment to sit and rest in an adjacent shop while each enjoying a cup of freshly squeezed pomegranate juice. It was an indisputably cherished experience to walk among the ancient and famous places on our itinerary this day -- but equally cherished was the opportunity to sit with my mother in this tiny and spartan shop, enjoying something as exotic and refreshing as a cup of freshly squeezed pomegranate juice.

 
➴ It should be acknowledged here that this exit, while very convenient for us, was not easily come by, nor (in my opinion) worth the price. The {{Via_Dolorosa}} is, abstractly, the path followed by Jesus on the way to his crucifixion; broadly, one well-known route in Jerusalem that represents the portion of this journey on which Jesus carried the cross, and that incorporates stops at each of the fourteen traditional "Stations of the Cross"; and narrowly, the name of the street in the Jerusalem where this route begins and where the first eight stations of the cross are sited.

The coordinates of these stations of the cross are dubious in the extreme -- and this is not just me talking; even the faithful come here with the wish to follow in Jesus's example, but knowing that they are unlikely to follow in his footsteps. First, the streets may not even be where they were in that era: This Jerusalem shares a name and location with the Jerusalem of Jesus's time -- but the First Jewish-Roman War followed Jesus's execution by about forty years, and in the words of {{Josephus}} the city was "so thoroughly razed to the ground by those that demolished it to its foundations, that nothing was left that could ever persuade visitors that it had once been a place of habitation". The Romans built their new city, {{Aelia_Capitolina}} in that same place -- but using a street pattern traditional to Roman cities, one that likely varied substantially from the one in the city they had just destroyed.

Second, even within the constraints of the current layout, uncertainty about the locations runs very deep: In the Middle Ages, the Roman Catholic Church in Jerusalem split into two factions, with those that controlled the western hill favoring a route of the Via Dolorosa that passed churches under their sway, and those that controlled the eastern hill predictably favoring a route that took pilgrims past their own institutions -- a situation that persisted until Pope Clement VI issued a bull, {{Nuper_Carissimae}}, establishing Fransiscan control over the Holy Land, and thus, by extension, allowing them to entrench their eastern-hill understanding of the Via Dolorosa, which we still follow to this day➴. Ironically, though, modern archaeological evidence strongly suggests that the western hill crowd may have been much closer to the target.

 
➴ Although backward from what the Fransiscans observed when they first established control: Originally they walked from the Church of the Holy Sepulchre to the House of Pilate. It was nearly 200 years later when they decided to reverse the course and visit the stations in the same sequence that Jesus did.

Finally, the stations themselves are suspect -- not just where they happened, but whether they happened. And again, this is not just me talking: There is absolutely no scriptural foundation for stations 3, 4, 6, 7, and 9 (the three falls; Jesus meeting his mother; and Veronica wiping Jesus's face), and a "Scriptural Way of the Cross" to supersede the traditional Stations of the Cross was advocated by no less a luminary than {{Pope_John_Paul_II}} in 1991, and reaffirmed by his successor, {{Pope_Benedict_XVI}} in 2007. Those of y'all who have stayed with me through multiple trip reports know this about me already: I get cranky when the thing I'm trying to explain defies explanation. Alas, the {{Church_of_the_Holy_Sepulchre}} is an explanation-defying entity if ever there was one. Quite possibly the second-most contentious piece of real-estate in the world➴, it's name isn't even unambiguous: By Orthodox Christians it is known instead as the {{Church_of_the_Resurrection}}.

 
➴ The record holder being (1) down the street and (2) in tomorrow's report.

Jerusalem is characterized as a nexus of discord between the dominant Abrahamic religions -- Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. But the Church of the Holy Sepulchre is marred by such disagreement between the factions of its own Christian faith that intercession by the others is indispensible to keeping the peace. The keys to the church are not even held by Christians, but rather by members of the Muslim {{Joudeh}} and {{Nusseibeh}} families, who have been entrusted for eight centuries with a job that they are expected to retain for all time -- that most basic of church functions: letting people in. Every morning, a member of the Joudeh family brings the keys to the church and hands them to a member of the Nusseibeh family; the latter unlocks the door and returns the keys, which are then taken back to the Joudeh home. The process is repeated in reverse each evening.

And in another regular event, the miracle➴ of the Holy Fire that has been celebrated at the church on Holy Saturday of every year since 1106, it is Jewish authorities who are brought into the church at the behest of the bickering Christian factions within. In this event, the Greek Orthodox Patriarch of Jerusalem is inspected by the aforementioned Jewish authorities to confirm that he carries no device capable of starting a flame before being allowed to enter the holy sepulchre with only a bundle of thirty-three white candles. As the congregation outside chants {{Kyrie_eleison}}, the Holy Fire is said to descend on the interior of the tomb and light the candles. The patriarch emerges within a half hour, the candles blazing.

 
➴ Okay, not to harsh anyone's buzz here, but the reason they even need the Jewish authorities is that this occurrence is extremely suspicious -- having been referred to as "a profiteers’ miracle" even by faithful persons within the church. Amongst those who are not convinced that the hand of the divine is at work, a couple of plausible theories hold sway: The dominent speculation is that the candles have been dipped in white phosphorus along with a binding organic solvent that evaporates during the course of the miracle, thus allowing the phosphorus to ignite when the binder no longer blocks its access to the air; this would be an elegant approach to feign the miracle using chemical properties that were understood even in ancient times, and was demonstrated to work in 2005. A second theory offers the more base explanation that the candles are simply lit by an icon lamp hidden in the sepulchre -- an event that the Russian Orthodox archimandrite, Bishop Porphyrius, claimed to have witnessed when he burst into the chamber during an execution of the miracle in the 1840s.

In any event, these Jewish authories have dutifully confirmed the absence of flint, matches, lighters, etc., but generally they are not so moved by what they witness so as to immediately convert to Christianity. So, there's that.

But it is the physical space, rather than the temporal events, that highlight the challenges of sharing in this particular sandbox: The church houses six denominations:

  • The {{Greek_Orthodox_Church}},
  • The {{Roman_Catholic_Church}},
  • The {{One_Holy_Universal_Apostolic_Orthodox_Armenian_Church}},
  • The {{Syriac_Orthodox_Patriarchate_of_Antioch_and_All_the_East}},
  • The {{Coptic_Orthodox_Patriarchate_of_Alexandria_and_of_All_Africa}}, and
  • The {{Ethiopian_Orthodox_Tewahedo_Church}}.

Most of these sects are represented sui generis, but the Roman Catholic holdings fall under a single individual, the {{Custodian_of_the_Holy_Land}}, an official of the {{Order_of_Friars_Minor}} -- AKA the Franciscans -- who represents the Pope in Jerusalem. (At the time of our visit, this person was a man by the name of Pierbattista Pizzaballa.)

But the space is not divided evenly; rather, the Greek Orthodox Church gets the lion's share, with the best of the remaining sections going to the Roman Catholic and Armenian Orthodox churches. Late entrants in the Nineteenth Century, the remaining groups are given rights only to lesser chapels on the periphery of the building -- with the holdings of the Ethiopian Orthodox Tawahedo➴ Church being almost ridiculous in their meagerness: A group of hovels on the roof of the building in which its monks reside (and the ownership of which is even disputed by the Copts), and a couple of small chapels alongside a staircase from that rooftop to the courtyard in front of the church's main entrance. The premium spots are shared -- but not evenly: Jesus's tomb is the province of the Greek Orthodox, Roman Catholic, and Armenian Orthodox sects exclusively. And there are two side-by-side chapels -- Greek Orthodox and Roman Catholic -- atop Calvary. Common areas are shared by all faiths with the exception of the front door, which is under the control of the aforementioned Muslim families.

 
➴ {{Tewahedo}} means "unified" -- but not in reference to the church; rather, in reference to Jesus. The Ethiopian Orthodox Tawahedo Church split from the then-still-united Catholic and Orthodox churches over the issue of the "Hypostatic Union", which sounds like a chemical term, but which is actually a reference to the unmixed, but unseparated, dual nature of Christ as both god and man. The Ethiopian church believes instead that there is but a single and complete Nature of Christ, which was imbued within him, unchanged, in the time before, during, and after his corporeal life.

"Cooperation" within the common areas is governed by an agreement called the "Status Quo", which defines the areas of control for each of the six groups and mandates that changes in the common areas must be agreed upon by all parties. Far from easing tensions and facilitating interaction, however, the Status Quo➴ instead has put the building into a state of eternal inertia that in some cases is amusing, and in others is negligent to the point of risking catastrophe: In the former camp, there rests above the entrance to the church a ladder that happened to be in place when the Status Quo was signed in 1853. It's not needed; indeed, it was just propped up at that time for some long-forgotten maintenance. But because it falls in a common area all six factions would have to agree simultaneously to its removal, and that has not happened in the 157 years since the agreement was penned. So the ladder remains. And it does not merely remain: It is even replaced, per the obligations of the Status Quo, when it periodically rots.

 
➴ It's not Latin, by the way (or at least not accurate Latin). The Latin term for "status quo" is {{statu_quo}}.

On the "negligent to the point of risking catastrophe" side, the edicule (the tomb building inside the church) is in desperate need of repair, but renovations have been delayed because of how the various groups fear the construction process and completed project would affect their areas of control under the Status Quo. A repair of the dome was completed in 1997, and was heralded as a miracle of unity between the groups -- but could only be achieved to avoid the impending nightmare of having the church's dome crash to the ground, destroying the tomb of Jesus and slaying his worshipers. The door that I've mentioned above is the only door to the building, and has proved so woefully inadequate as an escape route when fires have broken out in the building that the groups agreed in 1999 to build another door; eleven years have passed, and making the decision is as far as they've got.

Finally there's this: The city of Jerusalem recently revoked the exemption it had granted to synagogues, mosques, and churches for their water consumption. All of the other religious institutions in the city have complied with the new requirement -- but at the Church of the Holy Sepulchre they have so far dodged the obligation because they are unable to form an agreement as to who will receive the bill.

But it's not always fun and games (unless the term "monk fight" amuses you, in which case it is): In 2002 a Coptic monk moved his chair into the shade, which a nearby Ethopian monk interpreted as a forbidden encroachment in violation of the Status Quo; eleven people were hospitalized as a result of the ensuing fight. In 2008, a Greek monk was ejected from the building by a rival; when police were called to the church, they, too, were seized upon by the rioting clergy.

And all this for a plot of land that is of dubious historical significance at best: None of the Protestant faiths recognize this as the burial site of Jesus, nor of the crucifixion (which they don't always put in such proximity that they would fit in a single building anyway). And a belief in these loci is not a tenet of faith for any sect, including the ones that so jealously guard it.

Still, the biggest criticism is one that they do have an answer for: People are forever exclaiming, "How could this be the site of the crucifixion? It's right in the middle of downtown!" Because Jerusalem is so old, and because this part of Jerusalem is called the "Old City", it is easy to assume that these neighborhoods reflect the original layout. These lines show where some archaeologists believe the city walls lay in 70 CE -- encompassing areas outside the current walls (notably the {{City_of_David}} and Mount Zion) and excluding much of today's Christian Quarter, thus safely putting the Church of the Holy Sepulchre outside the gates.

That word "safely", however, is the bugbear: "Calvary" is short for “Mount Calvary", and other archaeologists point out that it would be nonsensical -- a threat to the safety of the citizens -- to put a defensive wall adjacent to Calvary, leaving the Tyropœon Valley (which was indisputably inside the walls) vulnerable to attack from that very hilltop. Many historians are of the view that a city with the power and wealth of Jerusalem would not be led to such a decision, neither by compromise nor by mistake. In any event, we will not soon know the definitive answer: The meager archaeological evidence that has been collected is inconclusive. And ongoing digging in this part of the world moves very, very, very slowly, for scientific, political, and theological reasons. Likely, though our lifetimes, the location of Calvary will remain a matter of faith.

Give me a second on the name, though, before we get to the site -- for you may wonder why we refer to this place interchangeably as "Calvary" and "Golgotha": "Calvary" is the English name for what comes to us from the original scriptures as “Golgotha (which means 'The Place of the Skull’)”. (This is Matthew 27:33 in the New International Version -- but the wording is similar in all four gospels.) Anyway, the parentheses aren't part of every translation, but the "which means 'The Place of the Skull'➴" is clearly an afterthought in all versions; the name of the mountain is {{Golgotha}} (or some cognate thereof), and nothing else. The translation -- "Place of the Skull" -- was offered as a helpful explanation by all four evangelists in their gospels to an audience reading in something other than Aramaic (usually Greek), and itself translates to Latin as {{Calvariae_Locus}}. What I can tell you of the experience, though, is this: I had utterly no idea whatsoever what was going on while I was in the building -- a confusion that was shared by all but those who had either (1) a lifetime of devotion or (2) a fantastic tour guide. As we were shuffling our way forward in the Fransiscan and Greek Orthodox chapels upon Golgotha, all of the English-speaking people around us commented amongst themselves, and occasionally to us, "What is this again?" At the 9th Station of the cross, we were invited by an English- (and only English-) language sign to view the cistern inside the Queen Helen Coptic Orthodox Church -- named for {{Saint_Helena}}, mother of {{Constantine_the_Great}}. Saint Helena, credited with finding the relics of the True Cross, is canonized in Roman Catholic as well as Orthodox traditions; indeed, she is the patron saint of new discoveries -- so who could argue with such a diversion? (Well, because of the dark, steep, and slippery staircase, Mom could. But I made a quick trip down to the bowels of the church while she waited by the street.)

The cistern below this church was discovered by Saint Helena in the 4th Century CE, and provided water for drinking and for building the Church of the Holy Sepulchre.

The sign promised a musical surpise if we sang a hymn in the echo-chamber of the cistern, and that promise was delivered in spades: As I approached on the steps, I could hear a faint sound of "Amazing Grace", which I first assumed to be a recording. But as I entered the dim chamber, I found the sound to be coming from one of the many pilgrims to Jerusalem, playing a favorite hymn on a harmonica he had brought with him on his journey. He finished the song a short time after I arrived (giving me enough time to enjoy the echoing strains of the familiar tune), and then was thanked with a thunderous applause as the ten-or-so of us in the cistern clapped in approval in the cistern's echoing expanse.

The walls of Jerusalem are bound so intrisically to the identity of this city that even in its inscription on the UNESCO list of World Heritage Sites, the name "Jerusalem", with its deep and contentious history, is not enough. The name inscribed on that list is (emphasis added), "Old City of Jerusalem and its Walls”.

It seems fitting to mention here that Jerusalem is incribed on not one, but two, compendia by the World Heritage organization: (1) World Heritage List and (2) World Heritage in Danger. And something as fundamental as its nomination illustrates why it's on list #2 at all: Jerusalem's nomination to the World Heritage List in 1981 came not from Israel (which had governed the land since it came into possession of the Old City at the end of the Six-Day War), nor from the {{Palestinian_Liberation_Organization}} (which had its headquarters in Jerusalem -- always by convention, and in reality during those years that it and Israel were not at war). Rather the nomination came from {{Jordan}} -- a nation that has ceased to demand restoration of its control over this land seven years prior and that would officially cede all claims, in deference to the PLO, six years later as a precursor to the {{Israel_Jordan_Peace_Treaty}}. Yet it was Jordan, amidst a decade-long process of reliquishing its claim, that initiated the process of recognizing and protecting the Old City.

Clambering up and around these structures like these -- in castles throughout Europe; in the pyramids of Central America; in temples along the Nile -- has always been a childlike joy for me. Jerusalem, absolutely, was no exception -- but the weight of history in Jerusalem not only waxes far more distant than in most places I've seen; it also wanes much more recent. So the ramparts for me were something much more than a fun diversion into a labyrinth with some interesting views: They were a dive into a history as weighty as the stones themselves.

Walled cities, of course, weren't built with the amusement of tourists in mind; they existed to protect their citizens from a hostile outside world -- and it may be true that the walls of Jerusalem hewed to this function more and longer perhaps than any other such fortifications on the planet. It was under these walls➴ that the knights of the {{Crusades}} fought the soldiers of the {{Great_Seljuq_Empire}} for the right of Christians to enter Jerusalem. But it was also here -- and within my lifetime -- that the {{Jordanian_Armed_Forces}} perched behind these same crenelations and fired into the streets of {{West_Jerusalem}} in the hope of convincing Jewish settlers that this was not going to be the safe space for which they yearned. And it was into the space framed by these ramparts that the {{Israel_Defense_Forces}} sent paratroopers at the end of the Six-Day War to strike fear into their opponents throughout the region and throughout the world, and to establish the claim that yes, in fact, it was.

I typically don't go for the "look like a local" approach to travel anyway -- but here it wasn't even remotely an option, unless I could somehow, magically and instantly, come by a beard, sidelocks, a wide-brimmed hat, and a full-length suit jacket. So my standard approach of being myself in the context of showing respect for the locals had to carry the day (perhaps with a greater-than-usual emphasis on the showing respect side of the equation). I had done my research and I think I pulled it off: I arrived alone rather than in a group; I wore dark colors and a conservative style; I avoided interactions and never took pictures of people. And at the end of the day, I was eyed suspiciously on multiple occasions, but never greeted with hostility. I believe that is the best that can be expected at Mea Shearim.

The number 100 comes up repeatedly in the early history of Mea Shearim -- and I have been unable to find sources that even remark the coincidence, let alone mention whether it was intentional or providential. Even so, here's what I've found: The community was founded by 100 shareholders in 1874. By 1880 there were 100 apartments ready for occupancy, which were assigned by lottery (so obviously not to the original 100). The name can also be translated as "hundred gates", and commentaries exist that the community was once surrounded by exactly that: 100 gates (a claim that I find inconceivable in light of the tiny number of streets, entrances, and acres/ares that describe this small neighborhood).

Mea Shearim was one of the earliest efforts by this group of shareholders in establishing living quarters outside of the Old City, which at that point had become overcrowded and unsanitary, and has preserved a Jewish way of life within its walls that is unique even within the context of the modern state of Israel. First, most residents reject the use of Hebrew as a street language, believing it should be reserved for religious use; for casual conversation they prefer Yiddish (which is why my non-scriptural references in this section also include Yiddish, even though most of these examples differ little from Hebrew). But more interestingly, Mea Shearim is the nexus of the Jewish anti-Zionist movement in Israel.

The Zionist movement, and the establishment of the State of Israel, are founded in a historical notion of {{Aliyah}}, a concept generally embraced in both the religious and secular Jewish worlds and enshrined is Israel's {{Law_of_Return}}. Aliyah encourages a return to Israel and has generally been taken as an endorsement for the establishment of the State of Israel -- but certain groups, notable among them Mea Shearim's {{Neturei_Karta}}, believe that the establishment of Israel as a nation, prior to the coming of the messiah, is sinful. Naturei Karta espouses a peaceful dismantling of the State of Israel, and has worked aggressively with the country's enemies -- various Palestinian factions and the government of Iran -- to bring about that circumstance, and simultaneously to separate the concepts of anti-Semitism and anti-Zionism; they have also been a fervent voice for better treatment of Palestinians inside and outside of Israel. Farther to the extreme is the {{Sikrikim}} group, named for the {{Sicarii}}, who attempted to expel Rome in the decades prior to the fall of Jerusalem in 70 CE by using concealed {{sicae}} to stab both the Roman occupiers and wealthy Jews who were comfortable under Roman rule. The Sikrikim, named as "terrorists" by many in the Israeli government, take this fight to the modern day -- most notably by inflicting over [[250000_ILS]] in damages to a bookstore near their headquarters that refused to quit stocking Zionist literature and throwing stones at Israeli police who enter the neighborhood. (They've also been involved in terroristic activity not specifically associated with their anti-Zionist stance, such as hurling eggs and rocks at children attending a girls schools that they thought allowed immodest dress and breaking the nose and finger of an electronics store owner whose portable video players enraged the Sikrikim.) It is common in Mea Shearim to see anti-Zionist placards, one of which is in my photograph group below.

Dialog regarding community standards among the residents of Mea Shearim is accomplished largely by those same placards, {{pashkvilim}}, placed throughout the neighborhood on public walls. A pashkvil that survives may be considered a community standard; one that is covered over may be considered controversial at best.

Also worth mentioning at this point is the use of "G-D" on the English side of the pashkvil that begins by addressing itself "TO WOMEN & GIRLS". This is a carry-over into English of a requirement in Hebrew regarding the circumstances under which the name of God can be written, and how it must be handled once such writing is done. These requirements are generally not considered an issue of faith in languages other than Hebrew, but omitting the vowel in English echoes ways that the issue is handled in Hebrew and is often done by observant Jews as a sign of respect.

Finally, I made my way back to the Old City, reconnoitered with Mom at the hotel, and took her to a restaurant that Billy and I had fondly enjoyed during our previous visit to Jerusalem: the Armenian Tavern. I tend to the nostalgic in these situations, and had a dish of minced meat in grape leaves that I had enjoyed in 2007, called khaghoghi derev. But I did branch out for the dessert course and ordered a rolled fruit sausage-like thing that was inoffensive to the taste, but, to be frank, looked like a caramel-covered turd.
