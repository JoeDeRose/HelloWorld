What do you do with [[31.777972x35.235806]]? There is no picture you can take of it, no story you can tell about it, no name you can even call it, that does not scrape a scab off 2,000 years of raw wounds over who should be allowed to love it.

The Jews name it the {{Temple_Mount}}. They owned it first, and built the foundation -- so I'm giving them primacy, but I'm not giving them a pass. I desperately want to like this place as much as I (very much) like the two groups who dispute it -- but at the end of the day, in the absence of true justice here, at least we can find poetic justice: The nexus of the most acrid dispute in the written history of our species is, itself, a nexus of ugliness. If it is a jewel in any sense, it is only so in the historic; in the here-and-now the Temple Mount is a visual monstrosity -- featureless without being sleek; enormous without being grand; elevated without being lofty. If you ask me, it couldn't be less attractive if they took a BeDazzler to it.

{{Herod}} was considered a master builder, and I'll grant that this drab rectangle might have looked more enticing with his architectural masterpieces demarcating it as the most sacred place in the world -- but now it is a blight. Where once there was the peak of Moriah➴¹ or Zion➴², now there is [[37_acre]] of a big concrete and stone patio -- slightly malformed in all dimensions, but not enough to suggest anything but sloppiness on the part of the laborers. If a child built a similar form out of blocks, her parents would be concerned.

 
➴¹ This is disputed -- but what the hell, what isn't?

Anyway ... if we interpret (although not everyone does) that {{Moriah}} is a mountain rather than a mountain range ...

And then if we try to parse 2 Chronicles 3:1 which states "Then Solomon began to build the house of the Lord at Jerusalem in mount Moriah, where He appeared unto David his father; for which provision had been made in the Place of David, in the threshing floor of Ornan the Jebusite." ...

And then if we further stipulate (as most do) that the threshing floor of {{Araunah}} and the threshing floor of {{Ornan}} refer to the same threshing floor of the same individual ...

... Well, then -- Bob's your uncle -- we're on top of Mount Moriah.
➴² {{Mount_Zion}} is now viewed as the mountain a quarter-turn clockwise around the Old City, but it seems to have wiggled around Jerusalem not unlike how the the magnetic north pole wiggles around Resolute Bay. In the day of {{Solomon’s_Temple}} (AKA, the "First Temple"), the edifice was described as being "where Mount Zion was" -- but absolutely not where Mount Zion is.

And like the proverbial "turd in a punchbowl", the Temple Mount cannot be ignored: No picture of Jerusalem seems complete without this grotesque hunk of disappointment looming somewhere in the frame.

But there is at least one thing for which we can be thankful as we look at the Temple Mount: Of all the places in our world that one culture or another reveres as a "holy mountain", this is the only one that has been entombed in a sarcophagus.

Devout orthodox Jews will not set foot on it -- not because the Temple Mount is so ugly, but because it is so holy: With the exception of {{priests}} performing specific duties in an active temple, no one may set foot in the Holy of Holies (or the space therefor consecrated) -- but with three competing theories about the location of the temple (on Mount Moriah; north of Mount Moriah; and south of Mount Moriah), and with measurements from the Second Temple period that conflict with each other, every step on the Temple Mount represents a potential trespass. The Muslim organization charged with oversight of the space -- {{The_Al-Aqsa_Association_for_Waqf_and_Heritage}} -- welcomes all visitors, but Jews are turned back by their own religious authorities:
